CIB

Been There, Done That

The Combat Infantryman Badge — awarded to infantrymen who have personally fought in active ground combat. The most respected badge in the United States Army, earned only through direct engagement with the enemy.

The CIB is exclusively reserved for infantrymen and Special Forces soldiers. Your Military Occupational Specialty must be 11-series (Infantry) or 18-series (Special Forces) at the time of the qualifying combat action.

This is the most critical gate — no other MOS qualifies, regardless of combat exposure. Soldiers with non-qualifying MOS who engage in combat are eligible for the Combat Action Badge (CAB) instead, but never the CIB.

You must be assigned to an infantry or SF unit of brigade size or smaller, actively performing infantry duties. Staff officers or those temporarily attached may not qualify without proper documentation.

Qualifying MOS Codes
  • 11B — Infantryman (primary qualifying MOS)
  • 11C — Indirect Fire Infantryman (mortarman)
  • 11Z — Infantry Senior Sergeant
  • 18-series — Special Forces (all specialties)
  • Historical MOS codes also qualify for retroactive awards
  • Officer branch must be Infantry (11A) or SF (18A)
  • CMF 11 / 18 must be primary MOS at time of action

The core requirement: you must be personally present and under hostile fire while engaged in active ground combat. This is not a proximity award — you must have directly participated in combat action against the enemy.

Indirect fire alone does not always qualify. The action must involve direct engagement with hostile forces in a ground combat situation. Rocket or mortar attacks on a FOB, while dangerous, may not meet the standard unless accompanied by direct engagement.

The combat action must occur in an area designated as a combat zone or during a qualifying conflict. Each war and conflict has specific qualifying dates and geographic boundaries defined by Department of the Army.

Qualifying Combat Actions
  • Firefight with enemy combatants — direct fire exchange
  • Enemy ambush requiring return fire and maneuver
  • Assault on enemy positions under fire
  • Defense against enemy ground attack
  • IED / mine strike with enemy contact
  • Close combat — hand-to-hand or close quarters
  • Patrol actions resulting in direct enemy engagement

The award process begins with a commander's recommendation through the chain of command. The combat action must be verified against operational reports, SIGACTs, and unit records. This is where many deserving soldiers lose their award — paperwork gets lost in the fog of war.

Documentation is everything. Combat action reports, patrol debriefs, and witness statements all support the award. Units rotating out of theater often lose track of pending awards, leaving soldiers without the recognition they earned.

Retroactive awards are possible with sufficient documentation. Veterans who can provide evidence of qualifying combat action — even decades later — may submit requests through HRC for review and approval.

Required Documentation
  • DA Form 638 — Recommendation for Award
  • Combat action reports / SIGACTs
  • Unit orders confirming assignment
  • MOS verification from personnel records
  • Witness statements (if available)
  • Deployment orders to qualifying combat zone
Deployment Cycles

Winter rotations often overlap with unit transitions. Incoming and outgoing units trade battle space, creating gaps in operational continuity. CIB-qualifying actions during this period are at higher risk of incomplete documentation as leadership and admin personnel rotate.

Winter Deployment Tips
  • Document every contact — don't rely on outgoing unit's records
  • Ensure SIGACTs are filed before rotation handoff
  • Coordinate with incoming unit S-1 for pending awards
  • Cold-weather combat zones add environmental hazards
  • Keep personal copies of all combat action documentation

Spring marks the traditional start of fighting season. In Afghanistan, spring thaw opened mountain passes and enemy forces resumed offensive operations. Units deploying in spring often saw their first major contact within weeks of arrival, making spring rotations historically significant for CIB awards.

Spring Deployment Tips
  • New rotations should establish documentation SOPs immediately
  • Rising contact rates mean more CIB-qualifying events
  • Coordinate with S-1 for rapid award processing
  • Don't wait for end of deployment to submit paperwork
  • Train replacements on award documentation procedures

Summer is historically the most intense combat period. From the beaches of Normandy to the valleys of Kunar Province, summer has produced more CIB-qualifying engagements than any other season. High operational tempo means more patrols, more contact, and more qualifying actions.

Summer Deployment Tips
  • High OPTEMPO means paperwork falls behind — stay on top of it
  • Document contacts in real-time when possible
  • Multiple qualifying actions still yield one CIB per conflict
  • Heat casualties compound combat stress — maintain hydration
  • Ensure platoon leaders track all qualifying engagements
Fall — Late Season Ops & Redeployment

Fall is the critical window for award finalization. As fighting seasons wind down and units prepare for redeployment, pending CIB recommendations must be processed before rotation. Awards not submitted before redeployment are exponentially harder to process retroactively.

Fall Deployment Tips
  • Audit all pending CIB recommendations before redeployment
  • Ensure every qualifying action has supporting documentation
  • S-1 should prioritize award processing during drawdown
  • Soldiers should verify awards appear in iPerms before PCS
  • Late-season engagements still qualify — document thoroughly
