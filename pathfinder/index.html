Course Overview

What is Pathfinder School?

Pathfinder School trains soldiers to establish and operate helicopter landing zones, parachute drop zones, and sling-load operations. Located at Fort Moore, Georgia, the course produces experts in navigating Army aviation assets to precise ground locations under any conditions — day or night, in any terrain.

Graduates earn the Pathfinder Badge (the "torch") and serve as the ground link between aviation and ground forces. The course is one of the most academically challenging in the Army, with a heavy emphasis on precision measurements, air traffic control procedures, and rapid computation under pressure.

Course Phases

3 Weeks to the Torch

  • Week 1 Navigation & DZ/LZ Operations — Precision land navigation, drop zone and landing zone selection, survey, and marking. Students learn to compute winds, establish impact points, and set up visual and electronic navigational aids.
  • Week 2 Sling-Load Operations — Rigging and inspection of all standard Army sling-load configurations. Students learn cargo preparation, helicopter hookup procedures, and load-calculation methods. Culminates in live sling-load exercises with rotary-wing aircraft.
  • Week 3 Air Traffic Control & FTX — Ground-based air traffic control of rotary and fixed-wing aircraft. Students establish and operate austere landing strips and conduct the final field training exercise integrating all skills under tactical conditions.
Key Standards

What It Takes

Candidates must hold the rank of E-4 or above and meet APFT standards. Each phase culminates in written and practical examinations with a minimum 70% passing score. Precision is paramount — a single dropped decimal in a wind calculation or an incorrectly set navigational aid can result in failure.

The academic rigor is the primary challenge. Students must memorize extensive technical data including aircraft specifications, sling-load weight limits, and air traffic control procedures. The course moves fast, and there is little room for error.
