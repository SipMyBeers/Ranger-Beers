Course Overview

What is Pathfinder School?

The U.S. Army Pathfinder Course is conducted at Fort Moore (formerly Fort Benning), Georgia, by the 1st Battalion (Airborne), 507th Infantry Regiment. The three-week course trains soldiers in precision navigation of aircraft to drop zones and landing zones, sling-load operations, and ground-based air traffic control. Graduates are awarded the Pathfinder Badge — the torch and wings insignia symbolizing mastery of these critical skills.

Pathfinder School is one of the most academically demanding courses in the U.S. military. It transforms soldiers into specialists capable of establishing and operating drop zones, landing zones, and helicopter landing sites under any conditions. The course emphasizes mathematical precision, technical proficiency, and the ability to perform flawlessly under pressure.

Course Phases

3 Weeks to the Pathfinder Badge

  • Week 1 Navigation & DZ/LZ Operations — Precision land navigation, drop zone and landing zone selection, survey, and marking. Students learn to compute winds, establish impact points, and set up visual and electronic navigational aids.
  • Week 2 Sling-Load Operations — Rigging and inspection of all standard Army sling-load configurations. Students learn cargo preparation, helicopter hookup procedures, and load-calculation methods. Culminates in live sling-load exercises.
  • Week 3 Air Traffic Control & FTX — Ground-based air traffic control of rotary and fixed-wing aircraft. Students establish and operate austere landing strips and conduct the final field training exercise integrating all skills.
Key Standards

What It Takes

Candidates must hold rank E-4 or above and meet APFT standards. Each phase culminates in written and practical examinations with minimum 70% passing score. Precision is paramount — a single dropped decimal in a wind calculation or incorrectly set navigational aid can result in failure.

The academic rigor of Pathfinder School is legendary among Army courses. With an approximate 50% graduation rate, the course demands exceptional attention to detail and the ability to perform complex calculations under stress. There is no room for error when guiding aircraft to a landing zone.
