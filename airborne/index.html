Ground Week teaches the fundamentals of military parachuting. Students learn proper body position for exiting aircraft, the five points of contact for parachute landing falls (PLFs), and emergency procedures for malfunctions.

Physical training is intense — daily PT sessions include runs, push-ups, pull-ups, and flutter kicks. The lateral drift apparatus and suspended harness build confidence and muscle memory for actual jumps.

Students practice mock-door exits from C-130 and C-17 mockups, learning proper body position, count, and check procedures. Any failure to demonstrate safe technique results in additional training or dismissal.

Ground Week Training
  • Parachute landing falls (PLFs) — front, left, right, rear
  • Mock door exits — proper shuffle, hand placement, body position
  • Lateral drift apparatus — simulates wind drift
  • Suspended harness training — canopy control, steering
  • Emergency procedures — reserve chute deployment
  • Aircraft orientation — C-130 Hercules, C-17 Globemaster
  • Physical fitness — runs, pull-ups, push-ups, flutter kicks
Tower Week — Heights & Confidence
Week 2

Tower Week advances training to the 34-foot tower for mock-door exits and the iconic 250-foot free-drop towers. Students practice mass exits, wind drift corrections, and equipment rigging while continuing intense PT.

The 34-foot tower simulates aircraft exits with a zip-line descent, building confidence for actual jumps. The 250-foot towers — visible landmarks at Fort Moore — lift students under an open canopy and release them for a controlled descent, closely replicating the final portion of a parachute jump.

Tower Week is where many students face their fear of heights. Refusal to exit the 34-foot tower results in immediate dismissal from the course.

Tower Week Training
  • 34-foot tower exits — controlled descent via cable
  • 250-foot tower drops — free-fall under open canopy
  • Swing landing trainer — landing under oscillation
  • Mass exit procedures — stick formation and timing
  • Equipment rigging — combat loads and weapon cases
  • Wind drift theory — steering and landing zones
  • Continued intense PT and PLF practice
Jump Week — Earn Your Wings
Week 3
Duration5 Days
LocationFryar Drop Zone, Fort Moore
AircraftC-130 / C-17
Altitude1,250 ft AGL
Requirements
Total Jumps5 (minimum)
Combat Equipment≥ 1 jump
Night JumpOptional (Hollywood)
Refusal to JumpImmediate dismissal

Jump Week is where it all comes together. Students execute five live static-line parachute jumps from C-130 or C-17 aircraft at 1,250 feet above ground level. At least one jump is conducted with full combat equipment.

Each jump builds on the last — starting with a basic "Hollywood" (no equipment) jump and progressing to full combat loads. Weather delays are common, and students may wait hours in full kit before jumping. Refusal to jump at any point means immediate dismissal.

Successful completion of all five jumps earns the Basic Parachutist Badge — "jump wings" — one of the most recognized badges in the U.S. military. Graduation day is typically Friday of Week 3.

Jump Week Progression
  • Jump 1 — Hollywood (no equipment), individual exit
  • Jump 2 — Hollywood, mass exit
  • Jump 3 — Hollywood or combat equipment
  • Jump 4 — Combat equipment jump (weapon, ruck)
  • Jump 5 — Final qualifying jump
  • Graduation — Pinning of Basic Parachutist Badge

Winter at Fort Moore is mild but wet. Morning PT in 30-degree temps is a shock for students from warm climates. Jump Week wind holds are more common, potentially extending the course by days.

Winter Gear Tips
  • Polypro cold-weather base layers for PT
  • Fleece cap & glove inserts (authorized under kit)
  • Waterproof boots — DZ is muddy
  • Extra socks — wet boots are the enemy
  • Neck gaiter for early morning formations

Spring is the best season for Airborne School. Temperatures are comfortable, rain is manageable, and jump conditions are generally favorable. Bring allergy medication — Georgia pollen is brutal.

Spring Gear Tips
  • Allergy medication — Georgia pollen is extreme
  • Light layers for morning-to-afternoon swings
  • Rain jacket — afternoon showers are common
  • Sunscreen for outdoor PT and DZ time
  • Break in boots early — rain softens leather

Georgia summer is brutal. Heat index regularly exceeds 100°F. Heat casualties are the #1 non-training-related drop reason. Hydration discipline is critical — cadre enforce water consumption but you must acclimatize before arrival.

Summer Gear Tips
  • Hydration system — drink before you're thirsty
  • Electrolyte packets — essential during PT
  • Extra undershirts — you will soak through multiple daily
  • Gold Bond / anti-chafe — humidity causes issues
  • Arrive 1-2 weeks early to acclimatize

Fall is arguably the best time for Airborne School. Heat has broken, winds are generally calm, and jump conditions are excellent. The fiscal year starts in October, so the first classes are fresh with new students and full cadre energy.

Fall Gear Tips
  • Light layers — mornings are cool, afternoons warm
  • Standard packing list is sufficient
  • Broken-in boots — don't bring new footwear
  • Sunglasses for DZ — low autumn sun angle
  • Lip balm — wind on DZ dries skin fast

Can't Do Pull-Ups?

Georgia heat is brutal. Hydration plans and heat acclimatization protocols keep you in the fight.
