The Ground Phase is purely academic — students must master the theory behind jumpmaster responsibilities including aircraft inspection procedures, personnel inspection (JMPI), wind drift calculations, drop zone selection, and safety-of-flight procedures.

The written exam on Day 3 requires a minimum 70% score with NO retest. This is the single biggest failure point — approximately 40% of all drops occur here.

Students who fail the written exam are immediately removed from the course with no opportunity to retest. The only path back is to reclassify for a future class and start from Day 1.

Ground Phase Training
  • Classroom instruction — jumpmaster duties and responsibilities
  • T-11/MC-6 parachute systems — components and operation
  • JMPI procedures (theory) — inspection sequence and standards
  • Wind drift calculations — formulas and application
  • Drop zone selection — criteria, markings, and approval
  • Safety-of-flight procedures — abort criteria and protocols
  • Written exam (Day 3) — 70% minimum, no retest

The Practical Phase tests hands-on Jumpmaster Personnel Inspection (JMPI) skills. Students must inspect jumpers for deficiencies in their parachute rigging. A missed deficiency during evaluation is an instant failure — if you miss something that could injure or kill a jumper, you're done.

Students inspect dozens of jumpers with deliberately inserted deficiencies — twisted risers, misrouted static lines, unsecured equipment. The evaluators know exactly what's wrong; students must find every single one.

JMPI is a tactile skill. You must be able to identify deficiencies by touch as well as by sight, since many inspections occur in low-light conditions or under time pressure.

Practical Phase Training
  • JMPI practical (hands-on) — full inspection sequence
  • Deficiency identification — deliberately rigged errors
  • T-11 inspection — static line, risers, canopy, harness
  • MC-6 inspection — steerable canopy rigging points
  • Combat equipment inspection — weapon cases, rucksacks
  • Timed JMPI evaluations — speed and accuracy under pressure

The Jump Phase puts students in the jumpmaster seat during actual airborne operations. Students serve as the primary jumpmaster on C-130 or C-17 aircraft, responsible for the safety of every jumper on board.

They must execute all jumpmaster actions: pre-jump briefing, aircraft inspection, 20-minute and 10-minute time warnings, commands, and door duties. Any safety violation or procedural error results in failure.

This is where everything comes together — the academic knowledge from Ground Phase, the inspection skills from Practical Phase, and the leadership required to be the last line of defense between a jumper and a malfunction.

Jump Phase Operations
  • Pre-jump briefings — weather, DZ, aircraft, abort criteria
  • Aircraft inspection — anchor line cables, static lines, exits
  • Jumpmaster actions (20-min) — first time warning sequence
  • Jumpmaster actions (10-min) — final checks, hookup commands
  • Door duties — wind check, spot, green light, exit control
  • Sustained airborne training — full operational evaluation
  • Live jump evaluation — primary JM on actual airborne op

ATPS T-11 Pre-Jump Training

T-11 Advanced Tactical Parachute System — Pre-Jump Briefing

1. Actions Inside the Aircraft

The Jumpmaster commands the stick through all pre-jump actions. Upon the command “GET READY,” jumpers assume the preparatory position. On “STAND UP,” jumpers rise and face the skin of the aircraft. “HOOK UP” — attach the static line snap hook to the anchor line cable. “CHECK STATIC LINES” — trace your static line from the snap hook to the pack tray. “CHECK EQUIPMENT” — conduct a head-to-toe self-check and check the jumper in front of you. “SOUND OFF FOR EQUIPMENT CHECK” — slap the jumper ahead and shout “OK!”

2. Proper Exit Technique

On “GO,” hand your static line to the safety and step into the door. Maintain a tight body position — feet and knees together, hands on the ends of the reserve, chin on chest, elbows tight. Jump up and out at a 45-degree angle with a vigorous exit. Count: “One thousand, two thousand, three thousand, four thousand.” Check canopy.

3. Canopy Check & Control

At the end of the four-second count, look up and check your canopy. The T-11 is a round parachute with a modified shape that reduces oscillation and descent rate compared to the T-10D. Verify: canopy is fully deployed, no malfunctions (blown panels, line-overs, cigarette rolls, Mae Wests, or inversions). If the canopy is good, gain canopy control by pulling on the appropriate risers to steer toward the DZ.

4. Malfunctions & Reserve Procedures

If you have a total malfunction (no canopy deployment by the end of your count): immediately activate the reserve parachute. Pull the ripcord handle with your right hand, pull the reserve straight out, and throw it into the airstream away from your body. For partial malfunctions (canopy deployed but not functioning properly): if you cannot correct it with riser manipulation within 2 seconds, activate the reserve.

  • Total malfunction — activate reserve immediately
  • Partial malfunction — attempt to correct, then reserve
  • Towed jumper — await Jumpmaster action, prepare reserve
  • Entanglement — communicate with other jumper, separate if possible
5. Parachute Landing Fall (PLF)

The T-11 descends at approximately 19 feet per second (compared to 24 fps for the T-10D). Prepare for landing at 200 feet AGL. Feet and knees together, knees slightly bent, chin on chest, elbows tight. Make contact on the balls of the feet and execute a PLF through the five points of contact: balls of feet, calf, thigh, buttocks, and pull-up muscle. Do NOT try to stand up — fall into the PLF.

6. Jumpmaster Personnel Inspection (JMPI)

The JMPI is the heart of Jumpmaster School. The Jumpmaster inspects each parachutist from head to toe in a specific sequence covering every point of attachment, routing, and rigging. A single missed deficiency on the JMPI practical exam results in failure. The sequence covers: helmet, reserve parachute, harness, main parachute, static line, combat equipment, and lowering line.

  • Front inspection — helmet, reserve, harness, equipment
  • Right-side inspection — static line routing, connector snap
  • Rear inspection — main pack tray, static line stowage
  • Left-side inspection — universal static line, ALICE clips
7. Sustained Airborne Training (SAT)

After graduation, Jumpmasters are responsible for conducting sustained airborne training for their units. This includes pre-jump briefings for every airborne operation, JMPI of all jumpers, safety of flight coordination, and DZ operations. The Jumpmaster is the final authority on whether conditions are safe for a jump. This responsibility is absolute.
