Course Overview

What is Jumpmaster Course?

The U.S. Army Jumpmaster Course is the premier airborne qualification conducted at Fort Moore, Georgia. Over nine intense days, airborne-qualified soldiers learn to assume total responsibility for the safe conduct of parachute operations — from pre-jump planning through execution. Graduates earn the Jumpmaster designation, the highest standard of airborne proficiency.

Jumpmaster is one of the most demanding courses in the airborne community. With an attrition rate near 60%, the course tests academic knowledge, attention to detail, and the ability to perform flawlessly under pressure. The Jumpmaster Personnel Inspection (JMPI) is the defining skill — a timed, high-stakes evaluation where missing a single major deficiency means failure.

Course Phases

9 Days to Jumpmaster

  • Days 1–3 Ground Phase — Intensive academic instruction on jumpmaster duties, aircraft procedures, drop zone operations, weather evaluation, and the JMPI sequence. Students memorize the precise 18-point inspection. Written exams require minimum 70%.
  • Days 4–6 Practical Phase (JMPI) — Students conduct timed Jumpmaster Personnel Inspections on rigged jumpers with deliberate deficiencies. Missing a major deficiency or false-calling a good rig results in failure. This phase eliminates the most candidates.
  • Days 7–9 Jump Phase — Students serve as jumpmaster on live airborne operations, controlling all aspects from pre-jump briefing through execution. Includes day and night operations. Successful completion earns the Jumpmaster designation.
Key Standards

What It Takes

Must be airborne-qualified with minimum 12 static-line jumps. Written exams require 70% minimum. Practical JMPI evaluations graded on detecting all major deficiencies within time standard. Zero tolerance for missed deficiencies.

The Jumpmaster Course is not a test of physical endurance — it is a test of precision, knowledge, and composure. Every inspection must be conducted exactly to standard. One missed safety deficiency on a jumper can mean the difference between a successful operation and a catastrophic failure.
