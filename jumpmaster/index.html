Course Overview

What is Jumpmaster School?

The Jumpmaster Course at Fort Moore, Georgia, trains airborne-qualified soldiers to serve as the jumpmaster — the senior parachutist aboard an aircraft who is responsible for the safety, preparation, and execution of all static-line parachute operations. The jumpmaster's word is law on the aircraft.

Jumpmasters inspect every parachutist's equipment before flight, issue jump commands, select exit points over the drop zone, and make the final GO/NO-GO decision. A single missed deficiency during a JMPI (Jumpmaster Personnel Inspection) could result in a parachutist's death. The ~40% graduation rate reflects the unforgiving standard of perfection required.

Course Phases

9 Days to Jumpmaster

  • Days 1-3 Ground Phase — Intensive academic instruction on jumpmaster duties, aircraft procedures, drop zone operations, weather evaluation, and the JMPI sequence. Students memorize the precise 18-point inspection and begin practicing on rigged jumpers. Written exams require a minimum 70% score.
  • Days 4-6 Practical Phase (JMPI) — Students conduct timed Jumpmaster Personnel Inspections on rigged jumpers with deliberate deficiencies. Each jumper has specific faults that must be found within strict time limits. Missing a major deficiency or false-calling a good rig results in failure. This phase eliminates the most candidates.
  • Days 7-9 Jump Phase — Students serve as jumpmaster on live airborne operations, controlling all aspects of the jump from pre-jump briefing through aircraft procedures to execution. Includes day and night operations. Successful completion earns the Jumpmaster designation.
Key Standards

What It Takes

Candidates must be airborne-qualified with a minimum of 12 static-line jumps. The course demands flawless memorization of the JMPI sequence and the ability to detect parachute rigging deficiencies under time pressure. Written exams require 70% minimum; practical JMPI evaluations are graded on detecting all major deficiencies within the time standard.

The JMPI practical is where most candidates fail. With lives literally on the line, there is zero tolerance for missed deficiencies. Successful candidates develop an almost obsessive attention to detail — the kind that catches a twisted connector link or improperly routed riser in seconds.
