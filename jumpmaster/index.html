The Ground Phase is purely academic — students must master the theory behind jumpmaster responsibilities including aircraft inspection procedures, personnel inspection (JMPI), wind drift calculations, drop zone selection, and safety-of-flight procedures.

The written exam on Day 3 requires a minimum 70% score with NO retest. This is the single biggest failure point — approximately 40% of all drops occur here.

Students who fail the written exam are immediately removed from the course with no opportunity to retest. The only path back is to reclassify for a future class and start from Day 1.

Ground Phase Training
  • Classroom instruction — jumpmaster duties and responsibilities
  • T-11/MC-6 parachute systems — components and operation
  • JMPI procedures (theory) — inspection sequence and standards
  • Wind drift calculations — formulas and application
  • Drop zone selection — criteria, markings, and approval
  • Safety-of-flight procedures — abort criteria and protocols
  • Written exam (Day 3) — 70% minimum, no retest

The Practical Phase tests hands-on Jumpmaster Personnel Inspection (JMPI) skills. Students must inspect jumpers for deficiencies in their parachute rigging. A missed deficiency during evaluation is an instant failure — if you miss something that could injure or kill a jumper, you're done.

Students inspect dozens of jumpers with deliberately inserted deficiencies — twisted risers, misrouted static lines, unsecured equipment. The evaluators know exactly what's wrong; students must find every single one.

JMPI is a tactile skill. You must be able to identify deficiencies by touch as well as by sight, since many inspections occur in low-light conditions or under time pressure.

Practical Phase Training
  • JMPI practical (hands-on) — full inspection sequence
  • Deficiency identification — deliberately rigged errors
  • T-11 inspection — static line, risers, canopy, harness
  • MC-6 inspection — steerable canopy rigging points
  • Combat equipment inspection — weapon cases, rucksacks
  • Timed JMPI evaluations — speed and accuracy under pressure

The Jump Phase puts students in the jumpmaster seat during actual airborne operations. Students serve as the primary jumpmaster on C-130 or C-17 aircraft, responsible for the safety of every jumper on board.

They must execute all jumpmaster actions: pre-jump briefing, aircraft inspection, 20-minute and 10-minute time warnings, commands, and door duties. Any safety violation or procedural error results in failure.

This is where everything comes together — the academic knowledge from Ground Phase, the inspection skills from Practical Phase, and the leadership required to be the last line of defense between a jumper and a malfunction.

Jump Phase Operations
  • Pre-jump briefings — weather, DZ, aircraft, abort criteria
  • Aircraft inspection — anchor line cables, static lines, exits
  • Jumpmaster actions (20-min) — first time warning sequence
  • Jumpmaster actions (10-min) — final checks, hookup commands
  • Door duties — wind check, spot, green light, exit control
  • Sustained airborne training — full operational evaluation
  • Live jump evaluation — primary JM on actual airborne op
