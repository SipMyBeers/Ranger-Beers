Module 1
Pistol Fundamentals

Precision pistol shooting — also known as bullseye shooting — demands absolute control of the pistol through proper grip, stance, sight alignment, and trigger control. These fundamentals apply across all pistol disciplines.

Grip Fundamentals:

  • High grip on the backstrap maximizes control
  • Firm pressure with support hand, consistent every time
  • Trigger finger contacts trigger at 90 degrees
  • No gap between hands — seamless grip interface
  • Equal pressure from both hands throughout the shot

Stance Options:

  • One-Handed (Bullseye): Body angled 45 degrees to target, shooting arm extended
  • Isosceles: Square to target, both arms extended equally
  • Weaver: Push-pull dynamic, shooting arm pushes, support arm pulls
  • Consistent stance for every shot is critical
  • Weight slightly forward, knees slightly flexed

Sight Alignment and Picture:

  • Equal height, equal light on both sides of front sight
  • Front sight crystal clear, target and rear sight slightly blurry
  • Focus never leaves front sight during trigger press
  • Consistent sight picture for every shot
  • Accept some wobble — perfect stillness is impossible

Trigger Control:

  • Smooth, continuous rearward pressure
  • No disruption to sight alignment during press
  • Surprise break — don't anticipate or flinch
  • Follow-through: maintain grip and sight picture after shot
  • Trigger reset for rapid fire stages

Bullseye shooting is 90% mental and 90% fundamental. Master these basics through dry-fire before attempting competitive scores.

Module 2
Slow Fire Technique

Slow fire is the precision foundation of bullseye shooting. Ten shots in ten minutes from the standing position — there is no excuse for poor fundamentals.

Slow Fire Procedure:

  • One hand shooting only (support hand in pocket or at side)
  • 50-yard distance (or 25-yard with reduced targets)
  • 10 minutes for 10 shots
  • Single-loaded or magazine-loaded depending on rules
  • Scoring: 10-ring is 2-inch diameter at 50 yards

One-Handed Shooting Technique:

  • Body angled 45 degrees, shooting arm extended naturally
  • Wrist locked, not broken
  • Elbow rotated outward to lock arm structure
  • Minimal muscle tension — bone support where possible
  • Accept arc of movement — don't fight it

The Shot Process:

  1. Assume position and establish grip
  2. Raise pistol to target and acquire sight picture
  3. Find minimum arc of movement (natural wobble)
  4. Focus exclusively on front sight
  5. Begin trigger press as sights enter 10-ring
  6. Accept the shot — don't try to "save" a bad hold
  7. Follow through, then lower and prepare for next shot

Mental Approach:

  • One shot at a time — previous shots don't matter
  • Take your time — 10 minutes is ample
  • If hold is unstable, lower and restart
  • Don't chase the dot — let the sights settle
  • Positive visualization before raising pistol

Slow fire separates precision shooters from the rest. Every aspect of fundamentals is exposed over 10 deliberate shots. Master this stage first.

Module 3
Timed Fire

Timed fire introduces the pressure of limited time while maintaining precision. Five shots in 20 seconds from either one-handed or two-handed position depending on match format.

Timed Fire Format:

  • Two strings of 5 shots each (10 shots total)
  • 20 seconds per string
  • 25-yard distance
  • Target: NRA B-8 (black bullseye)
  • Timed fire target is larger than slow fire

Position Options:

  • NRA Precision Pistol: One-handed, 25 yards
  • Conventional Pistol: May allow two-handed
  • Check match rules before competition
  • Practice both one and two-handed for versatility

Timed Fire Strategy:

  • Load before the command to save time
  • First shot should break within 5 seconds
  • Maintain sight focus throughout the string
  • Consistent rhythm: raise, acquire, press, follow-through
  • Never rush the trigger — 20 seconds is sufficient

Common Errors:

  • Rushing the first shot due to time pressure
  • Losing sight focus to watch the target
  • Jerking the trigger under time stress
  • Poor follow-through between shots
  • Failure to reset trigger properly

Transition to Rapid Fire:

Timed fire is the bridge between slow fire precision and rapid fire speed. The fundamentals remain identical — only the time available changes. Use timed fire to develop your speed while maintaining accuracy.

Module 4
Rapid Fire

Rapid fire is the ultimate test of pistol fundamentals under pressure. Five shots in 10 seconds — smooth, accurate, and controlled speed.

Rapid Fire Format:

  • Two strings of 5 shots each (10 shots total)
  • 10 seconds per string
  • 25-yard distance
  • Target: NRA B-8 (same as timed fire)
  • Pistol starts at 45-degree ready position

Rapid Fire Technique:

  • First shot on target within 2 seconds of start signal
  • Shot cadence: approximately 2 seconds per shot
  • Maintain sight picture through recoil
  • Trigger reset immediately after each break
  • Smooth, efficient magazine changes if required

Recoil Management:

  • Firm grip absorbs recoil energy
  • Wrist and arm locked to control m flip
  • Pistol returns to natural point of aim
  • Don't fight recoil — manage it
  • Sight realignment should be automatic

Common Rapid Fire Errors:

  • Starting too fast and throwing first shot
  • Losing grip consistency through the string
  • Trigger slapping instead of pressing
  • Running out of time due to poor cadence
  • Flinching or anticipating recoil

Practice Progression:

  1. Start at 15 seconds per string (slow rapid)
  2. Progress to 12 seconds when consistent
  3. Finally achieve 10-second standard
  4. Focus on clean hits, not just speed
  5. Use timer in practice to simulate pressure

Rapid fire rewards the shooter who maintains composure and fundamentals under time pressure. Smooth is fast — never sacrifice fundamentals for speed.

Module 5
Equipment & Setup

Precision pistol equipment must be reliable, consistent, and suited to your shooting style. While equipment doesn't replace fundamentals, poor equipment can prevent success.

Pistol Selection:

  • .22 LR: Ruger Mark IV, Smith & Wesson Model 41, High Standard
  • Centerfire: 1911 platform, Beretta 92, CZ 75
  • .45 ACP: 1911 Government model (traditional choice)
  • Match-grade barrels and triggers preferred
  • Reliability is paramount — match guns should run flawlessly

Sight Options:

  • Iron sights: adjustable target sights preferred
  • Front sight width: 0.125" or narrower for precision
  • Rear notch: provides adequate light on sides of front sight
  • Red dot optics: permitted in many match formats
  • Dot size: smaller dots (2-4 MOA) for precision work

Ammunition Selection:

  • .22 LR: Standard velocity for reduced recoil
  • Centerfire: Match-grade factory or handloads
  • Consistent bullet weight and velocity
  • Test multiple lots for accuracy
  • Zero with the ammunition you will compete with

Support Equipment:

  • Shooting box or cart for organized gear
  • Spotting scope for observing hits
  • Shooting mat for comfort and stability
  • Timer for practice sessions
  • Data book for recording scores and observations
  • Gun rag and cleaning supplies
  • Spare magazines and parts

Grip Modifications:

Many competitive shooters customize grips for optimal hand fit. Grip panels, stippling, or custom grips can improve control and consistency. Ensure any modifications comply with match rules.

Module 6
Match Preparation

Success in precision pistol competition requires systematic preparation before, during, and after the match. Develop routines that build confidence and consistency.

Pre-Match Preparation:

  • Confirm pistol zero with match ammunition
  • Function test all magazines
  • Clean and lubricate pistol to match standards
  • Pack backup equipment (spare pistol if available)
  • Review match format and rules
  • Mental rehearsal of shot process

Match Day Routine:

  • Arrive early to set up and acclimate
  • Inspect equipment before each stage
  • Consistent pre-shot routine for every string
  • Stay hydrated and maintain energy
  • Focus on your performance, not other shooters' scores

Stage Management:

  • Slow fire: Take your time, don't rush
  • Timed fire: Smooth rhythm, consistent cadence
  • Rapid fire: Controlled speed, maintain fundamentals
  • Record shots and conditions in data book
  • Make sight adjustments based on group centers

Mental Game:

  • Focus on process, not score
  • One shot at a time — forget the last, don't anticipate the next
  • Positive self-talk during difficult stages
  • Accept that bad shots happen — recovery is key
  • Visualize success before each string

Post-Match Analysis:

  • Record final scores and classification
  • Review target plots for patterns
  • Identify strengths and weaknesses by stage
  • Note equipment issues or malfunctions
  • Plan practice focus areas for next match

Precision pistol is a lifetime pursuit of perfection. Every match teaches lessons. The best shooters are students of the game, constantly refining their approach.
