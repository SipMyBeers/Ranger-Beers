Module 1
Rifle Fundamentals

High-power rifle shooting demands mastery of the fundamental marksmanship principles. These foundations are non-negotiable for competitive success at the President's Hundred.

Sight Alignment:

  • Center the front sight post in the rear aperture
  • Equal height, equal light on both sides of the post
  • Focus on the front sight — target should be slightly blurry
  • Consistent sight picture for every shot
  • Check for cant: rifle must be level for consistent elevation

Trigger Control:

  • Surprise break — never anticipate the shot
  • Smooth, straight-back press
  • No disturbing the sight alignment during trigger press
  • Follow-through: keep sights on target after recoil
  • Call your shots based on sight picture at break

Breathing Control:

  • Natural respiratory pause at end of exhale
  • Never hold breath for more than 8 seconds
  • If not ready, breathe and start over
  • Consistent breathing pattern for every shot
  • Heart rate control critical for standing position

Natural Point of Aim:

  • Rifle settles on target without muscle tension
  • Adjust body position, not rifle position
  • Check by closing eyes, settling, then opening
  • Foundation of all competitive positions
  • Re-establish after each shot in slow fire

These fundamentals are practiced through dry-fire until they become automatic. Live fire confirms what dry-fire has built.

Module 2
Position Shooting

The National Match Course tests four positions: standing, seated rapid, prone rapid, and prone slow fire. Each requires specific technique and dedicated practice.

Standing (Offhand) Position:

  • Body bladed 90 degrees to target (approximately)
  • Support elbow under rifle, not to the side
  • Rifle butt high in shoulder pocket
  • Head erect, chin forward for natural sight alignment
  • Weight balanced on feet, knees slightly flexed
  • Sling adjusted for upward pressure on rifle

Seated Position:

  • Cross-legged or open-legged depending on flexibility
  • Both elbows grounded for stability
  • Anklebone provides support for support-side elbow
  • Rifle supported by sling and bone structure
  • Minimal muscle tension required

Prone Position:

  • Body angled 20–30 degrees to target
  • Left elbow (for right-handed shooter) directly under rifle
  • Right leg flexed, left leg straight (or both flexed)
  • Head down, minimal neck strain
  • Sling provides primary support
  • Bone-on-bone support, minimal muscle

Rapid Fire Transitions:

  • Standing start for all rapid fire stages
  • Drop to position on target appearance command
  • Acquire natural point of aim immediately
  • Smooth magazine changes within time limits
  • Practice dry-fire position changes repeatedly

Each position must be practiced until it is automatic. In competition, there is no time to think about position — it must be muscle memory.

Module 3
Wind Reading

Wind reading is the single most important skill for high-power rifle competition. Matches are won and lost on the 600-yard line where wind deflection is measured in feet, not inches.

Wind Effects by Distance:

  • 200 yards: Minimal effect, 1–2 MOA correction maximum
  • 300 yards: Moderate effect, 2–4 MOA correction
  • 600 yards: Extreme effect, 4–10+ MOA correction
  • Full value wind (90 degrees) has maximum effect
  • No value wind (0/180 degrees) has minimal effect
  • Oblique winds require cosine component

Wind Indicators:

  • Range flags: most reliable direct indicator
  • Mirage: heat waves indicate wind direction and speed
  • Grass and vegetation: movement speed correlates to wind
  • Dust and debris: visual wind indicators
  • Other shooters' targets: observe splash and trace

Mirage Reading:

  • No mirage: either no wind or wind >12 mph
  • Slow boil (rises vertically): zero wind or very light
  • Angled mirage: wind direction indicated by angle
  • Fast boil: moderate to strong wind
  • Use high magnification on spotting scope
  • Read mirage at target distance, not just firing line

Wind Strategies:

  • Pick a condition and wait for it
  • Don't chase every wind change
  • Center your group in the average wind
  • Record conditions and corrections in data book
  • Learn your rifle's specific wind sensitivity

At Camp Perry, Lake Erie creates switchy, unpredictable winds. Top shooters develop instinct through years of reading these conditions.

Module 4
600-Yard Mastery

The 600-yard slow fire stage is where President's Hundred competitors are made. Twenty rounds at maximum distance with iron sights — this stage determines the top 100.

Technical Setup:

  • Elevation zero must be precise — verify before match
  • Windage zero should center group with no wind
  • Sling tension consistent for every shot
  • Position provides clear sight picture with minimal strain
  • Natural point of aim perfect — no muscling

Reading Conditions at 600:

  • Wind at firing line may differ from target
  • Use multiple indicators: flags, mirage, vegetation
  • Observe other shooters' impacts and corrections
  • Note switchy conditions and time your shots
  • Record everything in your data book

Shot Process:

  • Set initial sights based on observed conditions
  • Confirm natural point of aim
  • Exhale, pause, acquire sight alignment
  • Smooth trigger press with follow-through
  • Call the shot before looking at target
  • Spotter confirms — adjust sights if needed

Mental Approach:

  • One shot at a time — 20 separate events
  • Don't get rushed by time, but don't dawdle
  • Accept that some wind calls will be wrong
  • Adjust and continue — never dwell on bad shots
  • Focus on process, not score

A strong 600-yard performance requires technical perfection, wind reading skill, and mental discipline. This is where championships are earned.

Module 5
Rapid Fire Technique

Rapid fire stages at 200 and 300 yards test speed and consistency under pressure. The transition from standing to shooting position must be efficient and automatic.

Rapid Fire Seated (200 yards):

  • Start standing with rifle at port arms
  • Load two 5-round magazines before command
  • On "Targets" command, drop to seated position
  • Acquire natural point of aim immediately
  • Fire 5 rounds, reload, fire remaining 5
  • 60 seconds total — practice with timer

Rapid Fire Prone (300 yards):

  • Start standing, loaded with two 5-round magazines
  • On "Targets" command, drop to prone
  • First shot taken at 200 yards
  • Target moves to 300 yards after first string
  • Reload and fire second 5-round string
  • 70 seconds total — time for deliberate shots

Transition Technique:

  • Practice dry-fire drops until automatic
  • Drop straight down, rifle stays on shoulder
  • Immediate natural point of aim acquisition
  • Sling stays adjusted — no fumbling
  • First shot within 10 seconds of drop

Reloading Under Pressure:

  • Magazine placement consistent and accessible
  • Smooth, positive insertion
  • Bolt release or charging handle manipulation
  • No looking at rifle — maintain target focus
  • Practice until reload is unconscious

Rapid fire rewards efficiency. Every second spent fumbling position or reload is a second lost for aiming and wind reading.

Module 6
Data Book Management

A comprehensive data book is essential for competitive success. It tracks your zeros, wind corrections, ammunition performance, and match history.

Zero Data:

  • 200-yard zero elevation and windage settings
  • 300-yard come-ups for rapid fire stage
  • 600-yard elevation zero and adjustments
  • Date, temperature, and conditions for each zero
  • Record which ammunition lot for each zero

Wind Data:

  • Windage clicks for various wind speeds at each distance
  • Full value, half value, no value corrections
  • Specific to your rifle and ammunition
  • Camp Perry specific data if available
  • Update as you learn your rifle's behavior

Match Records:

  • Date, location, and match name
  • Weather conditions (temperature, wind, light)
  • Ammunition lot number used
  • Score for each stage and aggregate
  • Notes on what worked and what didn't

Shot Plotting:

  • Plot each shot in slow fire stages
  • Identify group patterns and called shots
  • Track sight adjustments and results
  • Look for trends indicating equipment issues
  • Compare called shots to actual impacts

Pre-Match Review:

  • Review previous Camp Perry data before National Matches
  • Check rifle zeros from last session
  • Confirm ammunition lot performance
  • Set initial sight settings based on conditions
  • Mental preparation from previous successes

Your data book is your competitive memory. Detailed records separate prepared shooters from those who repeat the same mistakes.
