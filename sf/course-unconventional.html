Module 1
Unconventional Warfare Fundamentals

Unconventional Warfare (UW) is the cornerstone of Special Forces operations. Defined as operations conducted by, with, or through irregular forces against an opposing power, UW enables the United States to influence conflict without large-scale conventional military commitment.

Definition and Scope:

UW encompasses military, political, psychological, and economic activities designed to undermine an occupying power or hostile government. SF ODAs serve as the primary force for conducting UW, providing training, leadership, and support to resistance movements.

Seven Phases of UW:

  • Preparation: Planning, intelligence collection, regional expertise development
  • Initial Contact: Infiltration and link-up with resistance organization
  • Infiltration: Covert entry into denied or hostile territory
  • Organization: Structure the resistance into effective military and political elements
  • Buildup: Train, equip, and expand resistance capabilities
  • Combat Employment: Conduct guerrilla operations to achieve strategic objectives
  • Demobilization: Transition to post-conflict stability or conventional operations

Strategic Context:

UW supports national strategic objectives by:

  • Denying enemy control of territory and population
  • Diverting enemy resources from main efforts
  • Creating instability in hostile regimes
  • Preparing the battlefield for conventional forces if needed
  • Achieving objectives without large-scale US footprint

Historical Examples:

  • World War II: OSS operations with European resistance movements
  • Cold War: Support to anti-communist insurgencies
  • Afghanistan 2001: SF ODAs with Northern Alliance overthrew Taliban
  • Northern Iraq: SF operations with Kurdish Peshmerga (2003)

The Resistance Trinity:

Successful resistance requires three components: a viable underground (clandestine network), auxiliary (support elements), and guerrilla forces (armed combatants). SF ODAs work to develop and coordinate all three elements for maximum effect.

Module 2
Guerrilla Warfare

Guerrilla warfare is the military component of unconventional warfare — irregular combat conducted by small, mobile forces against a larger, conventional enemy. Guerrillas rely on surprise, mobility, and popular support rather than firepower and position.

Principles of Guerrilla Warfare:

  • Political Base: Guerrillas must represent a political cause with popular support
  • Mobile Warfare: Avoid fixed positions; strike and withdraw rapidly
  • Intelligence: Superior local knowledge provides operational advantage
  • Popular Support: Population provides recruits, intelligence, supplies, and concealment
  • Gradual Expansion: Start small, build capabilities, expand operations

Guerrilla Tactical Operations:

  • Raid: Quick attack on enemy installations, then withdrawal
  • Ambush: Strike enemy forces in transit or vulnerable positions
  • Sabotage: Destroy infrastructure, supplies, or communications
  • Reconnaissance: Gather intelligence on enemy movements and dispositions
  • Psychological Operations: Undermine enemy morale; build resistance legitimacy

Avoiding Decisive Engagement:

The cardinal rule of guerrilla warfare: never engage the enemy conventionally unless you have overwhelming advantage. Guerrillas lose when they become fixed targets for enemy firepower. Hit weak points, avoid strong points, and live to fight another day.

Base Areas and Safe Havens:

  • Remote areas provide sanctuary for training and rest
  • Cross-border sanctuaries complicate enemy counter-guerrilla operations
  • Base areas require security, logistics, and political organization
  • Population in base areas provides recruits and support

Counter-Guerrilla Threats:

Guerrillas face multiple enemy responses:

  • Clear-and-hold operations to separate guerrillas from population
  • Search and destroy operations against base areas
  • Psychological operations to undermine popular support
  • Amnesty programs to induce defections
  • Counter-intelligence to penetrate resistance organization

SF Role in Guerrilla Operations:

SF ODAs do not replace guerrillas — they enable them. The ODA provides training, leadership, communications, fire support coordination, and liaison with conventional forces. Guerrillas provide manpower, local knowledge, and political legitimacy.

Module 3
Resistance Organization

Effective resistance requires sophisticated organization spanning military, political, and support functions. SF ODAs must understand resistance structure to effectively advise, assist, and integrate with irregular forces.

The Underground:

The clandestine arm of the resistance conducts covert activities:

  • Intelligence collection and counter-intelligence
  • Sabotage and subversion in occupied areas
  • Distribution of propaganda and information
  • Clandestine communications networks
  • Escape and evasion networks
  • Assassination of key enemy personnel (selective)

The Auxiliary:

Support elements that sustain the resistance:

  • Logistics: food, ammunition, medical supplies
  • Intelligence: civilian informants and observers
  • Communications: message passing, courier networks
  • Transportation: movement of personnel and supplies
  • Recruiting: identification and induction of new members
  • Medical: treatment of wounded guerrillas

The Guerrilla Force:

The armed combat element of the resistance:

  • Organized into squads, platoons, companies based on capabilities
  • Armed with weapons from cache, capture, or external supply
  • Trained in small unit tactics, weapons, and fieldcraft
  • Mobile and capable of operating throughout the operational area

Resistance Leadership:

  • Political Leader: Provides ideological direction and legitimacy
  • Military Commander: Directs guerrilla operations
  • Underground Chief: Runs clandestine networks
  • Regional Leaders: Command operations in specific geographic areas

Compartmentalization:

Security requires that members know only what they need to know. Cells operate independently with cutouts for communication. If one element is compromised, the damage is limited. SF ODAs must respect and reinforce this structure.

Building the Resistance:

SF ODAs assess existing resistance capabilities, identify gaps, and build capacity. This includes recruiting vetted personnel, establishing training programs, creating command structures, and developing sustainment systems.

Module 4
Indigenous Force Development

The core SF capability is training and leading indigenous forces. SF ODAs must assess, organize, train, and employ foreign military and paramilitary personnel to achieve US strategic objectives. This requires cross-cultural competence, patience, and adaptability.

Force Assessment:

Initial evaluation of indigenous capabilities:

  • Existing military or paramilitary structure
  • Leadership quality and loyalty
  • Personnel strength and demographics
  • Equipment and ammunition status
  • Training level and tactical proficiency
  • Motivation and political reliability

Training Methodology:

  • Train-the-Trainer: Develop indigenous instructors for sustainability
  • Progressive Training: Individual skills → team skills → collective training
  • Battlefield Relevance: Focus on tactics appropriate to the environment
  • Cultural Adaptation: Respect local customs and leadership styles
  • Hands-On: Demonstrate, practice, correct, repeat

Training Priorities:

  1. Weapons proficiency (individual and crew-served)
  2. Marksmanship and fire discipline
  3. Small unit tactics (patrolling, ambush, raid)
  4. Communications procedures
  5. First aid and casualty evacuation
  6. Leadership and decision-making

Leadership Development:

SF ODAs identify and cultivate indigenous leaders. This requires:

  • Assessing natural leaders within the force
  • Providing leadership training and mentorship
  • Building confidence and decision-making capability
  • Respecting existing hierarchy while improving competence
  • Ensuring loyalty to the resistance cause

Equipment and Sustainment:

  • Assess equipment needs based on mission and environment
  • Request external supply through SF support channels
  • Teach maintenance and repair of weapons and equipment
  • Develop local supply networks for food and non-military items
  • Establish medical evacuation and treatment procedures

Integration with Conventional Forces:

As UW operations mature, guerrilla forces may support or transition to conventional operations:

  • Provide intelligence and reconnaissance for conventional forces
  • Conduct flank security and interdiction operations
  • Serve as guides and linguists
  • Assist with civil-military operations
  • Integrate into regular military structure post-conflict

SF ODA Positioning:

SF teams embed with indigenous units at the tactical level. This proximity enables real-time advising, demonstrates commitment, and builds rapport. ODA members serve as tactical advisors, weapons specialists, communications links, and force multipliers.

Module 5
Foreign Internal Defense

Foreign Internal Defense (FID) is the companion mission to UW. While UW supports resistance movements against hostile governments, FID assists legitimate governments in defeating internal threats. Both missions leverage SF's ability to work with indigenous forces.

FID Definition and Purpose:

FID is participation by civilian and military agencies of one government in action programs taken by another government to free and protect its society from subversion, lawlessness, insurgency, terrorism, and other threats to internal security.

FID vs UW:

  • UW: Support insurgents against hostile power
  • FID: Support government against insurgents
  • Commonality: Both work with indigenous forces; both require cultural expertise
  • Legal Status: FID operates with host nation government consent and support

Internal Threats:

  • Insurgency — organized movement to overthrow government
  • Terrorism — violence against civilians to achieve political goals
  • Subversion — undermining government authority and legitimacy
  • Criminal networks — drug trafficking, human trafficking, smuggling

FID Operational Activities:

  • Training host nation military and police forces
  • Advising host nation commanders and staffs
  • Intelligence sharing and fusion
  • Logistics support and equipment provision
  • Combined operations against insurgent forces
  • Border security and interdiction

Comprehensive Approach:

Effective FID requires more than military action:

  • Security: Protect population from insurgent violence
  • Governance: Strengthen government legitimacy and services
  • Development: Economic progress reduces insurgent appeal
  • Information: Counter insurgent propaganda; build government narrative

SF Role in FID:

  • Assess host nation force capabilities and needs
  • Develop training programs tailored to the threat
  • Advise and accompany host nation units in operations
  • Serve as liaison between US and host nation forces
  • Support civil affairs and information operations

Challenges in FID:

  • Host nation corruption or abuse undermining legitimacy
  • Cultural and language barriers
  • Resistance to change within host nation forces
  • Insider threats and compromise
  • Balancing US objectives with host nation sovereignty

FID Success Factors:

Sustainable FID requires host nation commitment, legitimate government, viable security forces, and support from the population. SF can enable success but cannot create it alone. The host nation must own the solution.

Module 6
Robin Sage Exercise

Robin Sage is the culminating exercise of the Special Forces Qualification Course. Named for the fictional country of "Pineland," Robin Sage tests everything SF candidates have learned in a realistic unconventional warfare scenario spanning rural North Carolina.

Robin Sage Overview:

  • Duration: 4 weeks
  • Location: 15 counties in rural North Carolina
  • Scenario: ODA infiltrates Pineland to support guerrilla movement
  • Participants: SF candidates, role players, active duty OPFOR
  • Evaluation: Cadre assess every aspect of ODA performance

Exercise Phases:

  1. Isolation: Mission planning, intelligence preparation, rehearsals
  2. Infiltration: Covert entry into Pineland via air, land, or maritime means
  3. Link-up: Establish contact with guerrilla leadership
  4. Organization: Structure guerrilla forces, establish base areas
  5. Training: Train guerrillas in weapons, tactics, communications
  6. Combat Operations: Conduct raids, ambushes, reconnaissance
  7. Exfiltration: Withdraw from Pineland with mission complete

Key Events:

  • Initial infiltration and evasion of enemy forces
  • Establishing rapport with guerrilla leadership
  • Training indigenous forces from basic to advanced
  • Planning and executing combat operations
  • Managing logistics and sustainment in field conditions
  • Coordinating with other ODAs and higher headquarters
  • Dealing with civilian populations and local politics

Evaluation Criteria:

Cadre evaluate Robin Sage candidates on:

  • Tactical competence and decision-making
  • Leadership and team coordination
  • Cultural awareness and rapport building
  • Training effectiveness with guerrilla forces
  • Operational security and tradecraft
  • Adaptability when plans fail
  • Maintenance of SF standards and ethics

Role Players:

Civilian volunteers portray Pineland residents, guerrilla fighters, enemy forces, and government officials. Their interactions with SF candidates are unscripted and realistic. Building genuine rapport with role players is essential for success.

OPFOR (Opposing Force):

Active duty soldiers play the Pineland regime's military and security forces. They conduct patrols, search operations, and counter-guerrilla activities. SF candidates must avoid detection while conducting operations — capture by OPFOR means exercise failure.

Success at Robin Sage:

Successful completion of Robin Sage earns the SF candidate the Green Beret and Special Forces Tab. Failure results in recycle to a future Robin Sage or removal from the SF pipeline. The exercise is designed to be passable for prepared candidates but reveals those who lack the judgment, character, or skills for Special Forces.

Lessons for Preparation:

  • Master all MOS skills before arriving — you will use them all
  • Practice tactical decision-making under fatigue
  • Develop interpersonal skills for working with diverse personalities
  • Study UW doctrine and historical examples
  • Build physical endurance for sustained field operations
  • Internalize the quiet professional ethos
