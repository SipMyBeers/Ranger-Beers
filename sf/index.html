SFAS is the gateway to the Special Forces pipeline. Over 24 grueling days at Camp Mackall, candidates are assessed on physical endurance, mental toughness, teamwork, and leadership potential. The cadre are watching everything — how you carry yourself, how you treat others, how you perform when exhausted.

Long-distance ruck marches with heavy loads, timed runs, combat water survival, and solo land navigation courses in the forests of North Carolina push candidates to their absolute limits. Team events reveal who can solve problems under stress and who breaks down when the pressure mounts.

Approximately 50% of candidates are eliminated at SFAS — through voluntary withdrawal, non-selection based on performance, or medical removal. Those who are selected move forward into the SFQC pipeline.

SFAS Events
  • Long-distance ruck marches — progressive distance and load
  • Timed runs, swims, and obstacle courses
  • Individual and star land navigation — day and night
  • Team problem-solving events under extreme fatigue
  • Psychological evaluation and peer assessments
  • Stress inoculation — sleep deprivation, sustained operations
  • Leadership evaluation in ambiguous, high-pressure scenarios

What sets Special Forces apart from every other SOF unit is the language and cultural capability. Students are assigned a language based on their projected SF Group and spend 6 to 12 months achieving working proficiency.

Languages range from Spanish and French to Arabic, Dari, Korean, and more. Most students have zero prior experience in their assigned language. The immersive training demands intense daily study, memorization, and conversational practice.

Cultural awareness training accompanies language study — understanding the people, customs, politics, and history of the regions where SF operates. Failure to achieve the required DLPT score results in recycling or removal from the pipeline.

Language Training Highlights
  • Intensive immersion language instruction — 6+ hours daily
  • Defense Language Proficiency Test (DLPT) requirements
  • Regional and cultural awareness studies
  • Conversational practice with native speakers
  • History, politics, and customs of target regions
  • Continued physical training and readiness
  • Category I – IV language difficulty determines duration

Every Green Beret is a specialist. During MOS training, students learn the technical skills of their assigned specialty to a level that enables them to teach those skills to indigenous forces around the world.

18B Weapons Sergeants master U.S. and foreign weapons systems. 18C Engineers learn demolitions, target analysis, and field construction. 18D Medical Sergeants undergo some of the most intense medical training in the military — including surgical procedures, trauma medicine, and veterinary skills. 18E Communications Sergeants master advanced radio systems and signal planning.

The 18D medical pipeline is the longest MOS phase and one of the most demanding medical courses in the world. Failure rates vary by MOS but all demand total commitment to technical mastery.

MOS Training Highlights
  • 18B — Mastery of U.S. and foreign weapons, marksmanship, fire support
  • 18C — Explosives, demolitions, target analysis, construction
  • 18D — Trauma surgery, pharmacology, extended field care, veterinary
  • 18E — HF/VHF/SATCOM systems, antenna theory, signal planning
  • All MOSs learn to teach their specialty to foreign partner forces
  • Technical evaluations and practical hands-on testing
Robin Sage — Unconventional Warfare Exercise
4 Weeks
Duration4 Weeks

Robin Sage is the culminating exercise of the SFQC and one of the most realistic unconventional warfare exercises in the world. Students operate as an Operational Detachment Alpha (ODA) in the fictional country of "Pineland" — the rural counties of North Carolina.

Students must infiltrate, establish rapport with guerrilla forces, train indigenous fighters, plan and execute combat operations, and navigate the complex political dynamics of unconventional warfare — all while being evaluated on every decision.

Civilian role players, hostile forces, and unpredictable scenarios test whether candidates can truly operate as Special Forces soldiers. Successful completion of Robin Sage earns the coveted Green Beret and Special Forces Tab.

Robin Sage Requirements
  • Infiltrate and link up with guerrilla forces in "Pineland"
  • Establish rapport and build trust with guerrilla leadership
  • Train indigenous forces in combat operations
  • Plan and execute unconventional warfare missions
  • Navigate complex political and cultural dynamics
  • Demonstrate all SF MOS skills in a field environment

Winter SFAS classes are cold, wet, and miserable. Camp Mackall sits in the North Carolina sandhills where 25–35°F nights combined with rain and wind create serious hypothermia risk during 20+ mile ruck marches. Wet gear and sleep deprivation magnify the cold.

Winter Prep Tips
  • Train rucking in cold, wet conditions — simulate the misery
  • Practice layering and moisture management under load
  • Waterproof your ruck contents — wet gear kills morale
  • Cold-weather running to condition lungs and extremities
  • Mental toughness for sustained cold and wet operations

Spring at Camp Mackall means mud. Rain saturates the sandy soil and turns trails into ankle-deep slop. Temperatures are the most comfortable of the year, but constant wetness creates trench foot risk. Many consider spring the best time to attend SFAS overall.

Spring Prep Tips
  • Foot care is critical — practice changing socks on the move
  • Train in the rain — rucking in wet conditions builds resilience
  • Waterproof boots and extra sock changes
  • Comfortable temps allow focus on performance over survival
  • Bug spray — ticks and mosquitoes emerge in force

Summer SFAS is the most physically punishing. North Carolina heat and humidity push candidates to the edge of heat stroke during long rucks. Heat casualties spike during summer classes, and Robin Sage in summer is especially brutal — sustained field operations in 90°F+ heat with high humidity.

Summer Prep Tips
  • Heat acclimatization — train in heat for 2+ weeks prior
  • Hydration discipline — pre-hydrate and electrolyte loading
  • Ruck in high heat to simulate SFAS conditions
  • Know the signs of heat exhaustion and heat stroke
  • Lighter clothing under ruck — moisture-wicking base layers

Fall is the best time for SFAS and Robin Sage. Comfortable temperatures, low humidity, and dry conditions at Camp Mackall allow candidates to focus on performance rather than battling the elements. Late fall brings cooler nights but nothing dangerous.

Fall Prep Tips
  • Best conditions for peak performance — maximize your training
  • Light layers for cool mornings and warm afternoons
  • Reduced insect pressure compared to summer
  • Excellent land navigation conditions — clear visibility
  • Use the weather advantage to focus 100% on the course

Don't Be That SFAS Candidate.

Every failure in the SF pipeline has a type. We built a fix for each one.

🥾
Can't Ruck?

50+ mile weeks with 45lb+ ruck. Leg endurance is everything at SFAS. If you can't move under load for hours, you won't be selected.
🏊
Weak Swimmer?

Water confidence test and combat swim weed out many. If you're not comfortable in deep water with full gear, start now.
🧭
No Land Nav?

Alone in the dark with a compass. Find the points or fail. SFAS land navigation is the single biggest objective eliminator.
🗣
Language Intimidated?

6–12 months learning a new language from zero. Start building study habits and language aptitude before you arrive.
🤝
Team Player?

SF selects for collaboration, not individual heroics. If you can't put the team first and solve problems together, you'll be non-selected.
🧠
Wrong Mindset?

The quiet professional — humility over ego. SF doesn't want the loudest guy in the room. They want the one still working when everyone else quits.
