Course Overview

What is the SFQC?

The Special Forces Qualification Course (SFQC), also known as the "Q Course," is conducted by the John F. Kennedy Special Warfare Center and School at Fort Liberty (formerly Fort Bragg), North Carolina. It is the multi-phase training pipeline that produces Army Special Forces soldiers — the Green Berets.

The Q Course is one of the longest and most demanding military training programs in the world. Candidates learn unconventional warfare, foreign internal defense, special reconnaissance, direct action, and counter-terrorism. Each soldier specializes in one of five functional areas: weapons, engineering, medical, communications, or operations and intelligence.

Course Phases

The Long Road to the Green Beret

  • Phase 1 SFAS (Assessment & Selection) — 24 days of physical and mental screening at Fort Liberty. Land navigation, rucking, obstacle courses, team events, and psychological evaluation. Approximately 50% of candidates are selected to continue.
  • Phase 2 Small Unit Tactics (SUT) — 13 weeks of advanced individual and squad-level training. Covers patrolling, ambushes, raids, reconnaissance, and planning at the tactical level.
  • Phase 3 MOS Qualification — 24-58 weeks depending on specialty. Weapons Sergeants (18B), Engineers (18C), Medical Sergeants (18D), and Communications Sergeants (18E) each receive intensive training in their functional area.
  • Phase 4 Unconventional Warfare (UW) — The culminating field exercise, known as "Robin Sage." Students operate in a simulated country, working with guerrilla forces to plan and execute UW operations over several weeks.
  • Phase 5 Language & Culture — Intensive language training in the soldier's assigned language (Arabic, French, Spanish, Mandarin, etc.). Cultural immersion and regional studies prepare Green Berets to operate with foreign militaries.
  • Phase 6 SERE — Survival, Evasion, Resistance, and Escape training. Students learn to survive in austere environments, evade capture, resist interrogation, and escape captivity.
Key Standards

What It Takes

Candidates must be E-4 or above, airborne-qualified, pass the SFAS fitness test (push-ups, sit-ups, run, swim), and hold a minimum GT score of 110. The selection process evaluates not just physical fitness but leadership potential, problem-solving ability, and the capacity to work with indigenous forces.

The Q Course is designed to identify soldiers who can think independently, operate in small teams far from support, and thrive in ambiguous, high-stress environments. Attrition is roughly 70% from initial entry to graduation. The motto says it all: De Oppresso Liber — To Free the Oppressed.
