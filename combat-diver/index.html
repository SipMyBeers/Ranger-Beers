The Pool phase is where CDQC breaks most students. Drown-proofing with hands and feet bound is the signature event — students must survive in the water using only body movement. Panic is the enemy, and the cadre will find it.

Underwater knot tying, buddy breathing, and extended treading sessions build the water confidence required for combat diving. Pool harassment drills simulate equipment failures at depth — cadre strip masks and regulators while students must remain calm and recover.

Students who show any sign of panic, refuse to enter the water, or fail confidence drills are immediately dropped. ~20% of all CDQC failures happen here.

Pool Phase Events
  • Drown-proofing — hands and feet bound, bobbing and floating
  • Underwater knot tying at depth under time pressure
  • Buddy breathing — sharing a single air source
  • Pool harassment — equipment removal and recovery underwater
  • Extended treading — sustained periods in full gear
  • Open-circuit SCUBA introduction
  • Breath-hold exercises and confidence swims

Open Water is where pool skills meet the ocean. Students transition from controlled pool environments to open-ocean diving with currents, waves, and limited visibility. Long-distance surface swims and underwater navigation drills are conducted daily.

Underwater compass navigation is the core skill — students must swim precise bearings to designated targets without surfacing. The introduction to closed-circuit rebreather (LAR-V) operations begins here, teaching students to dive without producing telltale bubbles.

Night dives in zero-visibility conditions test mental toughness. ~12% of failures occur on underwater navigation events — getting lost underwater is both a safety hazard and a mission failure.

Open Water Events
  • Long-distance ocean swims with fins and combat load
  • Underwater compass navigation — bearing and pace count
  • Closed-circuit rebreather (LAR-V) introduction
  • Night dive operations in zero visibility
  • Dive physics, medicine, and emergency procedures
  • Surf entry and exit techniques
  • Equipment maintenance and pre-dive checks

The Ship Attack phase integrates every combat diving skill into full-mission profile exercises. Students plan and execute subsurface approaches to target vessels, place simulated limpet mines, and conduct beach reconnaissance — all using closed-circuit rebreathers.

Tactical evaluation is unforgiving. Students must demonstrate precise underwater navigation to reach the target ship, execute the attack, and exfiltrate without detection. Beach reconnaissance and hydrographic survey missions test the full spectrum of combat diver capabilities.

The final graded evaluation determines whether a student has demonstrated the skill, composure, and tactical proficiency required to earn the Combat Diver badge.

Ship Attack Requirements
  • Plan and execute closed-circuit ship attack profiles
  • Subsurface approach and limpet mine placement
  • Beach reconnaissance and hydrographic surveys
  • Over-the-beach infiltration and exfiltration
  • Night tactical dive operations
  • Successfully complete all graded evaluations

Winter in Key West is mild by mainland standards, but water temperatures in the upper 60s°F during extended subsurface operations create real cold stress. Students spend hours in the water daily, and even tropical water extracts body heat over time.

Winter Gear Tips
  • Wetsuit thickness matters — 3mm minimum for winter dives
  • Rash guard for pool phase to prevent chafing
  • Thermal cap for extended water exposure
  • Calorie-dense snacks — cold water burns energy fast
  • Hydration discipline despite cooler temperatures

Spring offers some of the best diving conditions in Key West. Water clarity peaks and temperatures rise into the upper 70s°F. Conditions are ideal for open water navigation training, though increasing afternoon swells challenge surf entry skills.

Spring Gear Tips
  • Sunscreen — UV exposure intensifies on the water
  • Quality dive mask with anti-fog treatment
  • Hydration is critical as heat ramps up
  • Fin selection — full-foot fins for pool, open-heel for ocean
  • Break in your wetsuit before arriving

Summer CDQC is brutal above water, not below. Air temperatures in the 90s with extreme humidity make land-based PT and equipment drills punishing. Water temperatures are warm, but reduced visibility from summer algae blooms complicates underwater navigation.

Summer Gear Tips
  • Electrolyte supplements — heat casualties are common
  • Lightweight moisture-wicking clothing for land phases
  • Extra water — minimum 1 gallon per day on land
  • Anti-chafe products for extended wet/dry cycles
  • Watch for jellyfish — peak season in Keys waters

Fall offers a good balance of conditions. Heat stress decreases while water temperatures remain warm. Visibility improves as summer algae blooms fade. Late hurricane season can disrupt training schedules, but October and November provide some of the most consistent conditions.

Fall Prep Tips
  • Build swim endurance — 2-mile ocean swim is required
  • Practice underwater breath holds and confidence drills
  • Compass navigation skills — practice on land first
  • Core and leg strength for finning under load
  • Mental preparation for pool harassment events

Don't Be That Dive Student.

Every failure at CDQC has a type. We built a fix for each one.

💨
Can't Hold Breath?

Breath control is non-negotiable. If you panic when your air is cut, you're done. Train hypoxic tolerance and stay calm when the lungs burn.
🌊
Fear of Dark Water?

Night dives in zero visibility separate combat divers from recreational divers. If dark water triggers panic, start controlled exposure now.
🧭
Bad Underwater Nav?

Miss your target by 10 degrees and you surface a hundred meters off course. Compass navigation underwater is precision work — practice relentlessly.
🏊
Weak Swimmer?

A 2-mile ocean swim is baseline. If you can't sustain a combat sidestroke for distance, you'll never make it past the first week. Build swim endurance now.
🛠
Equipment Fumble?

Rebreather assembly, pre-dive checks, and emergency procedures must be automatic. Fumbling with gear underwater gets you dropped — or killed.
👣
No Dive Experience?

Prior dive certification isn't required but massively helps. Basic open-water SCUBA experience reduces the learning curve and lets you focus on tactical skills.
