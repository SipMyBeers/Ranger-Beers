Medical

Medical Requirements & Preparation

Arrive medically ready or don't arrive at all. 100% participation is required — no temporary profiles, no duty limitations. Failure to meet medical standards at in-processing will result in dismissal before Day 1.

Medical Requirements at Arrival
Required Forms & Documentation
DD 2807-1 & DD 2808: Physical exam completed within 18 months of course start, meeting AR 40-501 standards
MEDPROS Printout: Must include full immunization summary — bring a hard copy
DD Form 2216E: Current audiogram on file
Labs / Radiology / EKG: As applicable based on your medical history and age
Flu Vaccination: Required for winter courses (Oct 16 — Apr 14)
Participation & Contact
100% Participation: No temporary profiles or duty limitations of any kind are accepted. You must be fully medically cleared.
Pre-Existing Conditions: Disclose everything during in-processing. Hiding a condition that surfaces during the course will get you dropped and potentially flagged.
BDE Surgeon Contact: (706) 544-6674 — call before your class date if you have any medical questions or concerns.
Non-Waiverable Medical Conditions

The following conditions will prevent you from starting the course. There are no exceptions — resolve these before you arrive or you will be sent home.

Automatic Disqualifiers
Chronic / Daily Medications: Case-by-case basis — most are disqualifying. Contact BDE Surgeon well in advance.
Non-Deployable Conditions: Any condition rendering you non-deployable per AR 40-501 is a no-go
LASIK / PRK: Must be more than 3 months post-surgery before course start date
Seasonal Restrictions
Previous Heat Injuries: Restricted from attending Apr 15 — Oct 15 courses. Heat cats are tracked in MEDPROS.
Previous Cold Injuries: Restricted from attending Oct 16 — Apr 14 courses. Cold weather injuries are tracked and flagged.
Plan your class date around your medical history. If you've had a heat or cold injury, pick the opposite season.

Cellulitis
Bacterial skin infection caused by cuts, scrapes, and abrasions that go untreated in the field. Symptoms include redness, swelling, warmth, and pain — can escalate rapidly to hospitalization.
Prevention: Treat every cut immediately. Bring extra moleskin, antibacterial ointment, and keep wounds clean and dry. Change socks regularly.
Flora & Fauna Hazards
Poison Ivy / Poison Oak: Extremely common in Benning and Mountain phases. Learn to identify and avoid. Wash exposed skin ASAP.
Fire Ants: Prevalent at Fort Moore and Camp Rudder. They will find you at night. Check your sleep position.
Copperheads: Active during warm months, especially in leaf litter at Camp Darby and Camp Merrill. Watch where you step and sit.
Spiders (Brown Recluse / Black Widow): Found in woodlines and abandoned structures. Shake out gear before wearing.
Heat Acclimatization Guide

Heat acclimatization is the physiological adaptation to working in hot environments. It improves sweating efficiency, lowers core temperature, reduces heart rate, and decreases the risk of heat casualties. If you're attending a summer class — this is mandatory preparation.

What Changes with Acclimatization
Heart Rate: Lower resting and working heart rates in the heat
Sweat Response: Earlier onset, greater volume, more dilute sweat (less sodium lost)
Core Temperature: Lower resting and peak temperatures during exertion
Skin Blood Flow: Improved heat dissipation through peripheral vasodilation
Perception: Reduced perceived exertion and thermal discomfort
How to Acclimatize
Duration: Minimum 2 weeks of daily heat exposure before course start
Protocol: 2+ hours per day of cardiovascular exercise in the heat (outdoor running, rucking, PT)
Week 1: Expect >60% adaptation — your body begins adjusting sweat rate and core temp regulation
Week 2: >80% adapted — cardiovascular and thermoregulatory systems are significantly improved
Retention: Acclimatization retained for ~1 week without exposure, then decays over ~3 weeks
Acclimatization Strategy
Start Early: Begin 1 month before your class date to build a full adaptation buffer
Mimic the Climate: Train in conditions as close to Fort Moore, GA heat/humidity as possible. If you can't, add layers to simulate.
Adequate Stress: Heart rate must be elevated — walking in the heat isn't enough. Run, ruck, and do calisthenics.
Progress Gradually: Start at 60-70% intensity and build up. Don't start with a 12-mile ruck in 95°F heat.
Hydration Protocol
Sweat Rate: You can lose >1 quart per hour during intense exertion in the heat
Weigh Yourself: Before and after training sessions — every pound lost equals ~0.5 quarts of fluid deficit
Replace: Drink 0.5 quarts per pound of body weight lost during training
Electrolytes: Water alone isn't enough for extended heat exposure. Use electrolyte packets to maintain sodium and potassium levels.
Pre-Hydrate: Drink 16-20 oz of water 2 hours before heat exposure. Don't wait until you're thirsty.
