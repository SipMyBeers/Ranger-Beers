Module 1
Arctic Warfare Course Overview

The Cold Weather Leaders Course (CWLC) is the Army's premier Arctic warfare training program conducted at the Northern Warfare Training Center (NWTC) in Black Rapids, Alaska. This preparation course ensures you arrive ready to meet the challenges of extreme cold-weather operations.

CWLC Mission: To train selected personnel in the knowledge and skills required to lead small units in cold-weather and mountainous environments, with emphasis on operations in extreme Arctic conditions.

Course Structure:

  • Duration: 15 days (3 weeks including travel)
  • Location: Northern Warfare Training Center, Black Rapids, AK
  • Training Unit: NWTC, Fort Wainwright, Alaska
  • Class Size: 30-40 students per class
  • Graduation Rate: Historically 55-65%
  • Eligibility: E-4 and above, O-1 and above

Course Phases:

  • Classroom Phase (Days 1-4): Cold-weather theory, injury prevention, equipment, basic ski and snowshoe techniques
  • Field Phase (Days 5-10): Six continuous days in Alaskan wilderness at sub-zero temperatures
  • FTX Phase (Days 11-15): Culminating tactical exercise integrating all skills

The Arctic Tab: Graduates earn the Arctic Tab, authorized for wear on the Army uniform. This skill tab signifies mastery of cold-weather survival, movement, and tactical operations — skills critical to operating in the Arctic, Alaska, and high-altitude mountainous regions worldwide.

Module 2
Cold Acclimatization Protocol

Acclimatization to cold is not optional — it's essential for survival. The human body can adapt to cold environments, but this process requires deliberate exposure and conditioning.

Physiological Adaptations:

  • Vasoconstriction Response: Improved peripheral blood flow control reduces heat loss
  • Shivering Threshold: Body delays shivering response, conserving glycogen stores
  • Metabolic Heat Production: Non-shivering thermogenesis increases basal metabolic rate
  • Cold-Induced Vasodilation (CIVD): "Hunting response" alternates vasoconstriction and vasodilation to protect extremities

Acclimatization Timeline:

  • Days 1-3: Initial cold shock, discomfort, reduced manual dexterity
  • Week 1: Basic physiological adjustments begin
  • Weeks 2-4: Significant improvements in cold tolerance
  • Weeks 4-6: Full acclimatization achieved

Pre-Course Acclimatization Plan:

  • Cold Showers: End showers with 30-60 seconds of cold water
  • Outdoor Training: Exercise in cold weather with minimal insulation
  • Ice Baths: 10-15 minute immersions (supervised) for physiological adaptation
  • Sleeping Cold: Gradually reduce sleeping bag temperature rating
  • Cold Exposure Walks: Short walks in cold with reduced clothing layers

Arrival in Alaska:

Arrive 3-5 days before course start if possible. The NWTC training area routinely experiences -40°F and colder. Sudden exposure without acclimatization dramatically increases cold injury risk and reduces performance. Use arrival time to finalize gear and begin local cold exposure.

Module 3
Physical Preparation & Conditioning

Arctic warfare is physically demanding in ways that differ from temperate environments. The combination of extreme cold, heavy clothing, deep snow, and equipment movement creates unique physiological challenges.

Arctic-Specific Physical Demands:

  • Clothing Load: ECWCS layers add 15-20 lbs of clothing weight
  • Equipment: Ahkio sleds, weapons, and ammunition add additional load
  • Movement Resistance: Deep snow and skis increase energy expenditure by 40-60%
  • Respiratory Stress: Cold dry air causes bronchoconstriction and increases respiratory rate
  • Caloric Requirements: 4,000-6,000+ calories per day in extreme cold

Physical Training Components:

1. Cardiovascular Endurance:

  • Build to 60+ minutes continuous aerobic activity
  • Include hill training to simulate uphill ski movement
  • Train in cold weather when possible
  • Target heart rate zones: 130-150 BPM for extended efforts

2. Leg Strength & Power:

  • Squats and lunges with emphasis on eccentric control
  • Step-ups with weight to simulate climbing in snowshoes
  • Wall sits to build isometric endurance for ski positions
  • Calf raises for ankle stability in ski boots

3. Core Stability:

  • Planks and side planks for load-bearing stability
  • Rotational exercises for ski pole planting
  • Dead bugs and bird dogs for spinal stability under load

4. Grip & Upper Body:

  • Farmer carries to simulate equipment handling
  • Pull-ups and rows for ski pole power
  • Wrist and forearm conditioning for weapon manipulation with gloves

12-Week Pre-Course Program:

  • Weeks 1-4: Base building — moderate intensity, volume focus
  • Weeks 5-8: Strength development — heavier loads, compound movements
  • Weeks 9-11: Sport-specific — ski simulation, loaded movement
  • Week 12: Taper — reduce volume, maintain intensity, arrive fresh
Module 4
Equipment Familiarization

Knowing your equipment before you need it is critical in the Arctic. Equipment failure or misuse in extreme cold can be fatal. This module covers essential CWLC equipment systems.

ECWCS Gen III Extended Cold Weather Clothing System:

  • Level 1 (Lightweight Undershirt/Drawers): Moisture-wicking base layer, polypropylene or silk
  • Level 2 (Midweight Shirt/Drawers): Additional insulation, wicking properties
  • Level 3 (Fleece Jacket): Primary insulation layer, breathable
  • Level 4 (Wind Jacket): Wind protection without insulation
  • Level 5 (Soft Shell Jacket/Trousers): Soft shell for active use
  • Level 6 (Extreme Cold/Wet Weather Jacket/Trousers): Waterproof hard shell
  • Level 7 (Extreme Cold Weather Parka/Trousers): Heavy insulation for static use

Footwear System:

  • Vapor Barrier (VB) Boots: "Bunny boots" — required for extreme cold (-20°F and below)
  • Booties: Insulated camp booties for sleeping/rest
  • Vapor Barrier Socks: Prevent foot moisture buildup inside boots
  • Foot Care: Dry socks daily, foot powder, inspect for frostbite

Handwear System:

  • Inner Gloves: Thin liner gloves for dexterity
  • Intermediate Gloves: Insulated gloves for moderate cold
  • Mittens: Trigger finger mittens for weapon manipulation
  • Shell Mittens: Waterproof outer layer

Head & Face Protection:

  • Balaclava: Full face coverage with breathing port
  • Cold Weather Cap: Insulated watch cap
  • Neck Gaiter: Prevents heat loss from neck area
  • Ski Goggles: Eye protection from wind and snow glare

Sleep System:

  • Sleeping Bag: Rated to -40°F minimum
  • Sleeping Pad: Insulated pad (R-value 4+)
  • Bivy Cover: Waterproof/breathable outer shell
  • Sleep Management: Dry clothes, hydration, calorie loading before sleep

Critical Equipment:

  • Ahkio Sled: Pulka-style sled for equipment transport
  • MSR XGK Stove: Liquid fuel stove for snow melting
  • Snowshoes: Military magnesium snowshoes
  • Cross-Country Skis: 75mm 3-pin binding system
  • Ski Poles: Adjustable with large baskets
Module 5
Mental Conditioning & Mindset

The mental component of Arctic warfare is often the decisive factor. Extreme cold, isolation, sleep deprivation, and physical discomfort test psychological limits. Mental preparation is as important as physical conditioning.

Psychological Stressors in Arctic Operations:

  • Cold Stress: Constant discomfort, threat of injury, energy drain
  • Sleep Deprivation: 2-4 hours per night during field phases
  • Isolation: Limited communication, whiteout conditions
  • Darkness: 4-7 hours of daylight in winter months
  • Physical Exhaustion: Ski movement is brutally demanding
  • Fear: Real danger from cold injuries, avalanches, equipment failure

Mental Preparation Strategies:

1. Stress Inoculation:

  • Deliberately expose yourself to cold and discomfort in training
  • Practice skills while cold, tired, and uncomfortable
  • Build confidence through progressive challenge

2. Visualization:

  • Mentally rehearse successful completion of course events
  • Visualize remaining calm and effective in extreme cold
  • Picture yourself earning the Arctic Tab

3. Goal Setting:

  • Set process goals ("Execute proper layering") not just outcome goals
  • Break the course into manageable segments
  • Celebrate small victories daily

4. Positive Self-Talk:

  • Develop mantras for difficult moments
  • Replace negative thoughts with constructive ones
  • Focus on controllable factors

5. Team Cohesion:

  • Build relationships with fellow students quickly
  • Embrace the "suffering together" mentality
  • Support others — helping teammates helps you

Coping Strategies for the Field:

  • Focus on the Present: Don't think about graduation; focus on the next task
  • Control Breathing: Use tactical breathing to manage stress response
  • Maintain Perspective: Remember why you're there and what you'll gain
  • Humor: Find moments of levity; laughter combats misery
  • Rituals: Develop small routines for comfort (hot drink before sleep)

The "Arctic Mindset":

The Arctic is indifferent to your comfort. It doesn't care about your rank, your previous accomplishments, or your excuses. Respect the environment, trust your training, rely on your team, and embrace the challenge. The Arctic Tab is earned through suffering — make the suffering meaningful.

Module 6
Pre-Deployment Timeline & Checklist

Proper preparation prevents poor performance. This timeline and checklist ensure you arrive at the Northern Warfare Training Center fully prepared for the challenges ahead.

12 Weeks Before CWLC:

  • Begin structured physical training program
  • Start cold acclimatization protocol
  • Research CWLC and read ATP 3-97.11 (Cold Weather Operations)
  • Secure required gear and begin fitting/testing
  • Schedule medical/dental appointments
  • Notify chain of command and unit

8 Weeks Before:

  • Ramp up training intensity
  • Test all gear in cold conditions
  • Practice layering system and clothing management
  • Begin ski training if access available
  • Review cold injury prevention

4 Weeks Before:

  • Peak training volume — heaviest loads, longest sessions
  • Final gear acquisition and inspection
  • Break in VB boots completely
  • Practice stove operation and maintenance
  • Study navigation in snow-covered terrain

2 Weeks Before:

  • Begin taper — reduce volume, maintain intensity
  • Pack and inventory all equipment
  • Confirm travel arrangements
  • Arrange early arrival in Alaska (3-5 days)
  • Final medical/dental clearance

1 Week Before:

  • Light training only — stay active, don't fatigue
  • Final gear check and packing
  • Confirm lodging in Fairbanks area
  • Review course materials and mental preparation
  • Get adequate sleep and nutrition

Required Gear Checklist:

  • ECWCS Gen III complete system (Levels 1-7)
  • Vapor Barrier (Bunny) boots with booties
  • Complete sleep system (-40°F rated)
  • Handwear system (liners, gloves, mittens, shells)
  • Headwear system (balaclava, caps, neck gaiter)
  • Ski goggles with multiple lenses
  • Individual first aid kit with cold injury supplies
  • Headlamp with red lens and extra batteries
  • Sunscreen and lip balm (SPF 30+)
  • Notebook and waterproof writing materials
  • Personal hygiene items (minimal)
  • Earplugs and eye mask for sleep
  • Hand warmers (backup)

Arrival Day:

  • Report to NWTC with all required documents
  • In-process and gear issue
  • Final medical screening
  • Course briefing and safety orientation
  • Meet your instructors and classmates

Final Advice:

The Cold Weather Leaders Course is one of the most challenging schools in the Army. The cold is real, the risks are real, and the standards are high. But graduates consistently report that CWLC was one of the most rewarding experiences of their military careers. Prepare thoroughly, embrace the suck, support your teammates, and earn that Arctic Tab. Cold As Steel!
