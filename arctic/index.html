The Classroom & Skills phase teaches cold-weather survival fundamentals. Students learn injury prevention, the layering system that keeps soldiers alive in sub-zero conditions, and equipment familiarization including the ahkio sled and snowshoes.

Military skiing fundamentals begin here — most students have never skied before. Weapon maintenance in extreme cold is critical — lubricants freeze, metal becomes brittle, and bare skin sticks to frozen steel.

Shelter construction teaches students to build quinzhees (snow shelters) that maintain 32°F inside when it's -40°F outside. This skill is tested repeatedly throughout the course.

Classroom Topics
  • Cold-weather injury prevention — frostbite, hypothermia, trench foot
  • Layering system — base, insulating, and outer layers
  • Snowshoe and ski movement techniques
  • Shelter construction — quinzhee, ahkio, and improvised
  • Weapon maintenance at extreme cold temperatures
  • Equipment familiarization — white camouflage, sleds, stoves
  • Avalanche awareness and terrain assessment

Field Training is where CWLC gets real. Students spend six continuous days in the Alaskan wilderness, maintaining themselves and their equipment in temperatures that drop to -40°F or colder.

Ski movement, cold-weather bivouac operations, river ice crossing, and avalanche awareness are all practiced in the field. Sleeping in the cold is itself a skill — improper sleeping bag management leads to hypothermia.

Students who cannot maintain their cold-weather equipment, fail to prevent cold injuries, or refuse to continue in extreme conditions are dropped from the course.

Field Training Events
  • Cross-country ski movement with full equipment
  • Cold-weather bivouac — multiple nights at -40°F
  • River ice crossing and thickness assessment
  • Avalanche awareness and route planning
  • Survival skills — fire, water procurement, emergency shelter
  • Tactical movement on snowshoes and skis
  • Equipment and self-maintenance in extreme cold

The FTX integrates every cold-weather skill into a sustained tactical operation. Students plan and execute patrols, establish cold-weather defensive positions, and conduct operations — all while managing the constant threat of cold-weather injuries.

Leadership is evaluated throughout the FTX. The CWLC is a leaders course — graduates must demonstrate they can lead soldiers in Arctic conditions, not just survive them.

The final assessment determines whether a student has demonstrated the knowledge, skills, and leadership required to plan and execute operations in extreme cold. Successful graduates earn the Arctic Tab.

FTX Requirements
  • Plan and execute cold-weather tactical patrols
  • Establish and maintain cold-weather positions
  • Demonstrate leadership in Arctic conditions
  • Manage equipment and personnel in extreme cold
  • Maintain zero cold-weather injuries in your element
  • Successfully complete all graded leadership events

Winter is THE season for CWLC. Most classes run January through March when conditions are most extreme. Temperatures routinely drop below -40°F and daylight is limited to a few hours. This is the full Arctic experience.

Winter Gear Tips
  • ECWCS Gen III system — all layers essential
  • Vapor barrier boots (VB / "bunny boots") — required
  • Extreme cold sleeping bag (-40°F rated)
  • Balaclava and ski goggles for face protection
  • Hand warmers — backup for glove system failure

Spring "breakup" in Alaska is dangerous. Warming temperatures destabilize snowpack creating peak avalanche conditions. River ice weakens unpredictably. Late CWLC classes in March still experience extreme cold with added spring hazards.

Spring Gear Tips
  • Avalanche beacon, probe, and shovel
  • Sunglasses — snow blindness risk increases
  • Waterproof layers — melt-freeze cycles soak gear
  • Full ECWCS still needed for nighttime temps
  • Gaiters for wet, deep snow conditions

CWLC does not run in summer. Alaska summers bring near-24-hour daylight and above-freezing temps. The NWTC runs other courses during summer months. Use this time to physically prepare and study cold-weather doctrine.

Summer Prep Tips
  • Build cardiovascular endurance — skiing is exhausting
  • Practice cross-country skiing if possible
  • Study ATP 3-97.11 (Cold Weather Operations)
  • Familiarize with ECWCS layering system
  • Cold-water immersion training for acclimatization

Fall is your last prep window before winter classes. Alaska's interior sees first snow in September and sub-zero temps by November. Some late-fall CWLC classes may begin in November, offering slightly "milder" conditions than January classes.

Fall Prep Tips
  • Finalize physical preparation — cardio and leg strength
  • Practice with your cold-weather gear system
  • Break in VB boots if you have access
  • Study cold-weather injury recognition
  • Mental preparation for sustained cold exposure

Don't Be That Arctic Student.

Every failure at CWLC has a type. We built a fix for each one.

🥶
Can't Handle the Cold?

Cold acclimatization isn't optional. If you've never experienced -40°F, your body and mind will revolt. Start preparing now.
⛺
Never Skied Before?

Most students arrive with zero ski experience. Cross-country skiing with a full combat load is brutally exhausting. Build leg endurance now.
🌡
Bad Layering?

Sweat kills in the Arctic. Understanding the ECWCS layering system and moisture management is the difference between life and death.
🧣
Wrong Boots?

Vapor barrier boots are required. Standard combat boots will freeze your feet. Get the right footwear or don't go.
😴
Can't Sleep in Cold?

Sleeping bag management is a survival skill. One mistake with moisture and your bag loses all insulation value.
🔥
No Stove Skills?

You need fire to survive. Stove operation, fire starting in snow, and water procurement from ice are mandatory skills.
