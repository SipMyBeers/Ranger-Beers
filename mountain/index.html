The Technical Skills phase teaches fundamental mountaineering and climbing techniques. Students learn rock climbing on Vermont's natural rock faces, beginning with basic movement and progressing to multi-pitch routes that demand confidence and skill.

Rappelling is introduced early — students descend cliff faces with increasing height and exposure. Anchor construction is critical — students must build reliable natural and artificial anchor systems that will hold body weight under dynamic loads.

Knot tying, belaying, and rope management are tested continuously. A failed belay or bad anchor means someone dies. There is no room for error on the rock face.

Technical Skills Topics
  • Rock climbing — basic movement, handholds, footholds, route reading
  • Rappelling — body rappel, seat-hip rappel, free rappel
  • Anchor systems — natural anchors, pitons, chocks, SLCDs
  • Knot tying — figure-8, prusik, munter hitch, water knot
  • Belaying — top-rope and lead belay techniques
  • Rope management — coiling, throwing, fixed lines
  • Basic mountaineering equipment familiarization

Mountain Survival is where AMWS students learn to live and operate in the mountain wilderness. Students spend five continuous days in the Green Mountains, maintaining themselves and their equipment in challenging alpine conditions.

Mountain weather is unpredictable and dangerous — conditions change in minutes at altitude. Shelter construction, fire starting, and water procurement are practiced under field conditions where failure means hypothermia or worse.

Mountain land navigation requires terrain association mastery. Map reading in mountainous terrain is fundamentally different from flat ground — contour lines, ridgelines, saddles, and draws become your primary reference points.

Survival Training Events
  • Mountain weather observation and prediction
  • Shelter construction — improvised and natural materials
  • Water procurement from streams, snow, and rain
  • Fire starting at altitude in adverse conditions
  • Mountain land navigation — terrain association
  • Altitude effects awareness and acclimatization
  • Route finding in mountainous terrain

The Mountain Operations FTX integrates every technical and survival skill into a sustained tactical operation in mountainous terrain. Students plan and execute cliff assaults, rappel insertions, and tactical patrols across steep, rugged terrain.

Leadership is evaluated throughout the FTX. Graduates must demonstrate they can lead soldiers in mountain operations — managing ropes, anchors, and personnel on exposed rock faces while executing tactical missions.

The final assessment determines whether a student has demonstrated the technical proficiency, survival skills, and leadership required to plan and conduct mountain operations. Successful graduates earn the Mountain Tab.

FTX Requirements
  • Plan and execute cliff assaults under tactical conditions
  • Conduct rappel insertions with full combat equipment
  • Establish and maintain mountain patrol base operations
  • Demonstrate leadership in mountainous terrain
  • Execute tactical movement across steep, rocky terrain
  • Successfully complete all graded leadership events

Winter is the most demanding Mountain Warfare cycle. The Green Mountains are buried in deep snow, rock faces are iced over, and temperatures regularly plunge below zero. Ice climbing is added to the curriculum, making winter the most technically challenging iteration.

Winter Gear Tips
  • ECWCS layering system — all layers essential
  • Insulated climbing gloves — dexterity in cold is critical
  • Crampons and ice tools for winter climbing
  • Extreme cold sleeping bag (-20°F rated minimum)
  • Hand warmers — cold fingers fail on rock faces

Spring in Vermont is mud season. Snowmelt saturates every trail and rock face, making climbing treacherous. Wet rock provides far less friction, demanding better technique and stronger grip. Weather swings wildly — morning frost can give way to afternoon rain.

Spring Gear Tips
  • Waterproof outer layers — constant wet conditions
  • Climbing shoes with aggressive tread for wet rock
  • Extra dry socks — wet feet lead to blisters and trench foot
  • Gaiters for mud and saturated trails
  • Quick-dry base layers for temperature swings

Summer offers the best climbing conditions. Dry rock provides maximum friction and long daylight hours allow extended training. However, afternoon thunderstorms at altitude are a real danger — lightning on exposed rock faces forces rapid tactical decisions.

Summer Gear Tips
  • Hydration system — heat and exertion cause rapid dehydration
  • Sun protection — exposed rock faces intensify UV
  • Lightweight rain shell for afternoon thunderstorms
  • Insect repellent — Vermont woods are thick with bugs
  • Light base layers for heat management on climbs

Fall offers excellent climbing conditions with cool, dry air and solid rock friction. However, dense foliage obscures terrain features, making mountain navigation significantly harder. Shorter days compress the training timeline and late-season classes may encounter early frost and ice on rock faces.

Fall Gear Tips
  • Layering system for wide temperature swings
  • Headlamp with extra batteries for shorter days
  • Compass and map skills — foliage obscures landmarks
  • Light insulation layer for cold morning rock faces
  • Mental preparation for rapid weather deterioration

Don't Be That Mountain Student.

Every failure at AMWS has a type. We built a fix for each one.

😵
Afraid of Heights?

Cliff faces and rappels test your nerve. If you freeze on exposed rock, you're done. Confront this fear before you arrive.
🪝
Can't Tie Knots?

Figure-8, prusik, munter hitch — know them cold. Fumbling with knots on a cliff face gets people killed. Practice until it's muscle memory.
🧰
Bad at Belaying?

Lives depend on your belay technique. One moment of inattention and your climbing partner falls. There are no second chances on the rock.
💨
Weak Cardio?

Altitude and inclines punish the unfit. Hauling gear up steep terrain with a combat load will destroy you if your cardio isn't there.
🪫
No Climbing Experience?

Basic rock skills are taught, but fitness matters more. If you can't do pull-ups and grip hangs, the rock face will expose you fast.
🗺
Can't Read Terrain?

Mountain nav requires terrain association mastery. Contour lines, ridgelines, saddles, and draws — if you can't read the map, you're lost.
