The Technical phase builds the foundation of every rappel operation. Students must master knot tying under pressure — the figure-eight, bowline, Prusik, and rappel knots must be tied correctly every single time. A failed knot means a dead soldier.

Rope inspection and equipment serviceability checks are drilled repeatedly. Rigging anchor systems — both natural (trees, rock) and artificial (vehicle, structural) — teaches students to create bombproof anchor points that hold thousands of pounds of force.

Tower rappels begin here, starting at 34 feet and progressing to 50-foot tower operations. Students learn body position, braking techniques, and how to manage fear while maintaining technical precision.

Technical Topics
  • Knot tying — figure-eight, bowline, Prusik, water knot, rappel knots
  • Rope inspection — identifying wear, damage, and serviceability
  • Anchor construction — natural and artificial systems
  • Rappel seat rigging — Swiss seat and harness systems
  • Tower rappelling — body position, braking, descent control
  • Safety procedures — belay techniques and risk mitigation
  • Equipment accountability and maintenance

The Practical / Aircraft phase transitions everything to live rotary-wing operations. Students rig UH-60 Black Hawk helicopters for rappel operations, learning the specific attachment points, rope deployment procedures, and aircraft-specific safety checks.

Live helicopter rappels are the culminating event. Students rappel from hovering aircraft at various altitudes, managing the rotor wash, noise, and disorientation that make helicopter operations fundamentally different from tower rappels.

The final certification evaluates whether a student can serve as the Rappel Master — the single individual responsible for the safety of every soldier exiting the aircraft. This means commanding the operation from inside the helicopter, inspecting every soldier's equipment, and making go/no-go calls in real time.

Aircraft Phase Events
  • Aircraft familiarization — UH-60 rappel points and procedures
  • Helicopter rigging — rope attachment, deployment, and recovery
  • Live helicopter rappels at multiple altitudes
  • Rappel Master duties — pre-jump inspection and go/no-go authority
  • Safety officer procedures — abort criteria and emergency actions
  • Night rappel operations (conditions dependent)
  • Final certification evaluation as Rappel Master

Winter classes face significant weather challenges. Flight operations are frequently delayed or cancelled due to low ceilings, icing conditions, and reduced visibility. Cold ropes are stiffer and harder to grip, and gloved hands reduce dexterity during rigging.

Winter Tips
  • Practice knot tying with gloves — cold fingers fail
  • Bring hand warmers for tower and airfield standby
  • Expect course extensions due to weather holds
  • Layer smart — you'll sweat on the tower, freeze on the airfield
  • Leather gloves maintain better rope grip in cold

Spring is the best time to attend Rappel Master. Weather cooperates more often, flight windows are longer, and temperatures are comfortable for extended tower and airfield operations. Most installations run their highest class volume in spring.

Spring Tips
  • Rain gear for wet tower and airfield standby
  • Wet rope handling — practice with damp ropes
  • Sunscreen for extended airfield operations
  • Hydration — warmer temps increase fluid needs
  • Book early — spring classes fill fast

Summer classes run but bring heat challenges. Metal carabiners and rappel rings absorb heat. Ropes on sun-exposed towers become hot to handle. Heat casualties are a real risk during extended airfield standby waiting for aircraft.

Summer Tips
  • Hydrate aggressively — tower work in the sun is brutal
  • Gloves protect against hot metal equipment
  • Sunscreen and eye protection for airfield ops
  • Expect thunderstorm delays in afternoon hours
  • Early morning flights are most reliable

Fall is the second-best window for Rappel Master. Temperatures are comfortable, severe weather is less frequent, and units push to fill school seats before the fiscal year ends. Flight conditions are generally stable with occasional morning fog delays.

Fall Tips
  • Layer for morning cold and afternoon warmth
  • Morning fog may delay early flights
  • Excellent rope handling conditions
  • FY closeout means more class availability
  • Prepare for transition to cold-weather rope work

Don't Be That Rappel Student.

Every failure at Rappel Master has a type. We built a fix for each one.

🪝
Can't Tie Knots?

Knot tying is the #1 failure point. If you can't tie a figure-eight, bowline, and Prusik blindfolded under time, you're going home. Practice until it's muscle memory.
😶
Afraid of Heights?

The tower is 50 feet. The helicopter is higher. If you freeze on the skid, you fail. Acrophobia must be managed before Day 1 — not discovered on it.
🚁
Bad Aircraft Procedures?

Helicopter operations have zero margin for error. Wrong hookup, wrong signal, wrong position — any of these can be fatal. Know the aircraft procedures cold.
🔨
Weak Rigging Skills?

Anchor systems hold lives. If your rigging fails inspection, you fail the course. Learn to build bombproof anchors that hold thousands of pounds of force.
😷
No Rope Experience?

Students with zero rope experience struggle from Day 1. Get hands on a rope before you arrive. Climbing gyms, mountaineering clubs — any rope time helps.
⚠
Safety Violation?

One safety violation and you're out. No warnings, no second chances. The Rappel Master is responsible for lives — there is zero tolerance for unsafe acts.
