Module 1
Jungle Terrain Analysis

Understanding jungle terrain is the foundation of effective mobility. The jungle environment varies dramatically, and each type presents unique challenges and opportunities for movement.

Types of Jungle Terrain:

  • Primary (Tropical Rainforest): Triple canopy cover with dense undergrowth. Visibility limited to 10-20 meters. Movement is slow and requires constant cutting.
  • Secondary Growth: Areas previously cleared or disturbed. Dense vegetation including thorns, vines, and bamboo. Extremely difficult to traverse.
  • Savanna/Grasslands: Open areas with tall grass offering concealment but limited cover. Excellent for rapid movement if security permits.
  • Mangrove Swamps: Coastal wetlands with tangled root systems. Requires wading or specialized movement techniques.
  • Deciduous Forest: Seasonal leaf drop creates varying visibility conditions. Easier movement during dry season.
  • Montane Jungle: Mountainous jungle with steep slopes and elevation changes. Combines jungle challenges with mountain terrain hazards.

Route Selection Factors:

  • Ridge Lines: Offer best drainage, fewest obstacles, and best visibility. Preferred when tactical situation permits.
  • Stream Beds: Provide clear paths but present waterborne hazards and limited escape routes. Use with caution.
  • Game Trails: Natural paths created by wildlife. Quiet and efficient but may lead to enemy positions or ambush sites.
  • Contour Lines: Following elevation contours minimizes energy expenditure on steep slopes.
  • Vegetation Density: Balance between concealment and movement speed. Thicker vegetation = slower movement.

Ground Assessment:

Before moving, analyze the ground for:

  • Soft ground indicating water or mud hazards
  • Falling hazards from dead branches ("widowmakers")
  • Vine entanglement that can trap equipment
  • Natural lines of drift that may be monitored
  • Escape routes and rally points
Module 2
Movement Techniques

Jungle movement requires modified techniques from standard patrolling. The dense vegetation restricts formations and demands constant adaptation.

Individual Movement Techniques:

  • High Crawl: Used when vegetation provides partial concealment. Body low, weight on forearms and legs, weapon held ready.
  • Low Crawl: Used when concealment is minimal. Body flat, pulling with arms, pushing with legs. Slow but quiet.
  • Sniper Low Crawl: Extremely slow movement for maximum stealth. Minimal disturbance of vegetation.
  • 3-5 Second Rush: Rapid movement from cover to cover. Essential when crossing danger areas or under fire.
  • Stepping: Deliberate foot placement to avoid snapping twigs or rustling leaves. Roll foot from outside to inside.

Element Movement Formations:

  • File Formation: Most common in jungle. Follow the leader's path. Maintains security 360 degrees through sectors.
  • Modified Wedge: Used when terrain permits slightly wider dispersion. Point man forward, slack and pace men angled.
  • Diamond Formation: Provides all-around security. Used during security halts or when enemy contact is expected.
  • Single Ranger: Individual movement for reconnaissance or when dispersion is critical. High risk, high stealth.

Movement Discipline:

  • Hand and Arm Signals: Silent communication only. Voice carries surprisingly far in jungle.
  • Interval: Maintain 3-5 meters between personnel in dense vegetation. Prevents bunching and casualties.
  • Security: Every member maintains 360-degree awareness. Jungle conceals threats at close range.
  • Pace Count: Critical for navigation when visibility is limited. Count steps and note terrain features.
  • Halts: Conduct security halts every 30-50 minutes or after crossing obstacles.

Speed Considerations:

Jungle movement rates are significantly slower than open terrain:

  • Primary jungle: 0.5-1 km per hour
  • Secondary growth: 0.25-0.5 km per hour
  • Game trails: 1-2 km per hour
  • Night movement: Reduce day rates by 50%
Module 3
Machete Operations

The machete is essential jungle equipment for clearing vegetation, preparing positions, and survival tasks. Proper technique prevents injury and maximizes efficiency.

Machete Types:

  • Latin Machete: Standard 18-24 inch blade. Versatile for clearing and chopping.
  • Bolo: Heavier blade weighted toward tip. Excellent for dense vegetation and light chopping.
  • Parang: Curved blade designed for woody vegetation. Preferred by many jungle warfare schools.
  • Golok: Shorter, heavier blade for heavy chopping tasks.

Safety Procedures:

  • Always maintain sharp edge—dull machetes require more force and cause more injuries
  • Clear swing area 360 degrees before cutting
  • Never cut toward yourself or others
  • Wear eye protection—ricocheting debris is common
  • Secure blade in sheath when not in use
  • Apply pressure dressing knowledge—machete injuries are common and serious

Cutting Techniques:

  • Forehand Cut: Standard cutting stroke. Start at shoulder height, follow through with hips.
  • Backhand Cut: Reverse stroke for vegetation on opposite side. Less power, more control.
  • Jab Cut: Thrusting motion for thick vines or targeted cutting. Not for woody stems.
  • Draw Cut: Pulling motion for controlled, precise cuts. Useful near sensitive equipment.
  • Chopping: Overhead stroke for heavy vegetation or construction. Most dangerous technique.

Jungle Lane Cutting:

When cutting a path through jungle:

  • Cut at 45-degree angle away from direction of movement
  • Clear vegetation at chest height first, then work down
  • Remove cut vegetation from path to prevent trip hazards
  • Rotate cutters every 5-10 minutes to prevent fatigue
  • Maintain security—cutting noise masks enemy approach
  • Mark route for rear elements using blazes or tape

Machete Maintenance:

  • Clean blade after each use—jungle vegetation is acidic
  • Sharpen regularly using file or stone
  • Oil blade to prevent rust in high humidity
  • Check handle for cracks or looseness
  • Replace worn blades—structural failure is dangerous
Module 4
Silent Movement

Stealth is critical in jungle operations. Sound carries farther than you expect, and the jungle provides ample opportunities for noise discipline.

Foot Placement:

  • Feel Before Stepping: Test ground with toe before committing weight
  • Outside Edge First: Roll foot from outside to inside to distribute weight
  • Avoid Deadfalls: Step over or around dry branches and leaves
  • Rock Walking: Use rocks and roots to avoid ground vegetation
  • Balance: Move slowly to maintain balance—recovery movements create noise

Equipment Noise Reduction:

  • Tape: Secure all loose straps, buckles, and metal components
  • Padding: Use cloth or foam between items that might clang
  • Magazine Taping: Tape magazines together to prevent rattling
  • Weapon Sling: Use padded sling and secure excess webbing
  • Canteens: Fill completely or pad to prevent sloshing
  • Medic Bag: Secure all contents to prevent rattling

Vegetation Management:

  • Move vegetation with hands rather than pushing through
  • Hold branches for following soldiers—release slowly
  • Avoid grabbing thorny or stinging vegetation
  • Step over logs rather than on them (hollow logs create noise)
  • Anticipate release of bent vegetation—timing matters

Communication Discipline:

  • Hand and Arm Signals: Primary method—rehearse extensively
  • Whispers: Cup hands around mouth and ear—whispers carry
  • Clickers: One click = stop, two clicks = danger, three = rally
  • Radio: Lowest power setting, earpiece, whisper mic
  • Runner: Most secure method for short distances

Environmental Awareness:

Use natural sounds to mask movement:

  • Wind gusts
  • Rain or drizzle
  • Running water
  • Wildlife movement
  • Aircraft or distant explosions
Module 5
Obstacle Negotiation

Jungle obstacles require specialized techniques to overcome safely and efficiently while maintaining tactical posture.

Stream and River Crossings:

  • Site Selection: Look for natural bridges, shallow fords, or narrow points
  • Current Assessment: Throw stick to assess speed—if you can't walk alongside, it's too fast
  • Individual Crossing: Face upstream, side-step, use walking stick for balance
  • Team Crossing: Link arms or use rope for mutual support
  • Equipment: Waterproof critical items, secure weapon
  • Post-Crossing: Dry feet immediately—maceration leads to incapacitation

Cliff and Slope Movement:

  • Diagonal Ascent/Descent: Zigzag to reduce effective slope angle
  • Three Points of Contact: Always maintain three secure points when climbing
  • Vine/Rope Use: Test before weight—jungle vines can support weight but verify first
  • Root Steps: Use exposed roots as natural ladder rungs
  • Rappelling: Natural anchors include trees (10-inch minimum diameter), rock horns
  • Fixed Lines: Install hand lines for difficult sections

Thick Vegetation:

  • Bamboo Groves: Navigate around when possible—nearly impenetrable
  • Thorn Areas: Use cutting to clear path; protective clothing essential
  • Wait-a-While: Hooked vines that catch equipment—cut or bypass
  • Fallen Trees: Use as elevated paths when stable; check for rot
  • Swamp Areas: Test footing with stick; stay on roots and tussocks

Waterlogged Terrain:

  • Mud Movement: Move quickly through—slow movement creates suction
  • Quicksand Identification: Bubbling, wet ground with vegetation. Stay calm, float, backstroke out.
  • Punji Areas: Probe with stick; look for disturbed ground
  • Leeches: Check constantly; salt or heat for removal

Navigation Obstacles:

  • Boxing In: When terrain forces deviation, document offset for correction
  • Catch Features: Use streams, ridgelines, or large terrain features to regain position
  • Dead Reckoning: Essential when visibility prevents terrain association
  • GPS Degradation: Triple canopy blocks signals—plan for manual navigation
Module 6
Tactical Considerations

Mobility in the jungle must always support tactical objectives. Movement techniques change based on the threat environment and mission.

Movement Techniques by Threat:

  • Traveling: Continuous movement when enemy contact unlikely. Faster but less secure.
  • Traveling Overwatch: Lead element moves while trail element overwatches. Used when contact possible.
  • Bounding Overwatch: Element-by-element movement with overwatch. Used when contact expected.
  • Stealth: Slow, silent movement for reconnaissance or close proximity to enemy. Maximum concealment.

Danger Areas:

Jungle danger areas include:

  • Stream Crossings: Limited fields of fire, channelized movement
  • Clearings: Loss of concealment, exposure to observation
  • Trails: Natural lines of drift, likely ambush sites
  • Ridge Lines: Skyline exposure, predictable routes
  • Swamps: Restricted movement, limited fields of fire

Cross danger areas using:

  • 90-degree offset to observe before crossing
  • Designated near and far side rally points
  • Rapid, covered movement
  • Security maintained throughout

Actions on Contact:

  • React to Contact: Immediate return fire, seek cover, report
  • Locate: Identify enemy position, strength, activity
  • Suppress: Fix enemy with fire while maneuvering
  • Attack: Close with and destroy enemy using fire and movement
  • Break Contact: If enemy too strong, withdraw using smoke, indirect fire

Counter-Ambush:

  • Avoid predictable routes and timing
  • Vary patrol patterns
  • Use point and slack men to detect early indicators
  • Maintain interval—bunching increases casualties
  • Immediate action drills rehearsed to standard
  • Counter-ambush through kill zone or into ambush position

Combat Load Considerations:

  • Balance combat load (35-50 lbs) against mission requirements
  • High-cut packs for prone movement
  • Hydration accessible without removing pack
  • Ammunition accessible for rapid reload
  • Medical equipment where any soldier can reach it
  • Redistribute loads during halts to prevent fatigue

Physical Conditioning:

Maintain jungle-specific fitness:

  • Rucking in heat and humidity with combat load
  • Pull-ups and grip strength for climbing
  • Balance and proprioception training
  • Core strength for load bearing
  • Ankle stability for uneven terrain
