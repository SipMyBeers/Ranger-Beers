The Jungle Skills phase teaches fundamental jungle movement and rope work. Students learn knot tying, rope bridge construction, waterfall rappelling, and basic jungle movement techniques in the dense vegetation surrounding Schofield Barracks.

Canopy navigation begins here — GPS is unreliable under triple canopy. Equipment maintenance in the tropical environment is critical — humidity rots gear, rust forms overnight, and everything stays wet.

Tropical disease prevention is introduced early. Students learn to manage heat, prevent fungal infections, and recognize symptoms of dehydration and heat-related injuries in the Hawaiian jungle.

Skills Phase Topics
  • Knot tying — 10+ knots including prusik, Swiss seat, bowline
  • Rope bridge construction — one-rope and suspension bridges
  • Waterfall rappelling under live water
  • Jungle movement — trail cutting, canopy navigation
  • Tropical disease prevention — heat injuries, fungal infections
  • Equipment maintenance in high-humidity environments
  • Basic jungle survival orientation

Jungle Survival is where JOTC gets real. Students spend seven continuous days in Oahu's Kahuku Training Area, maintaining themselves and their equipment in relentless heat, humidity, and rain.

Shelter construction, fire craft in wet conditions, water procurement from bamboo and vines, and edible plant identification are all practiced in the field. Land navigation under triple canopy is itself a test — GPS is useless and the terrain looks the same in every direction.

Students who cannot maintain their equipment, fail to prevent heat injuries, or refuse to continue in the jungle environment are dropped from the course.

Survival Phase Events
  • Improvised shelter construction in the jungle
  • Fire starting in wet, humid conditions
  • Water procurement from bamboo, vines, and natural sources
  • Edible and poisonous tropical plant identification
  • Animal trapping and food procurement
  • Land navigation under triple-canopy jungle
  • River crossings and waterborne operations

The Patrol Phase integrates every jungle skill into a sustained tactical operation. Students plan and execute ambush patrols, jungle lane patrolling, and stream crossings under fire — all while managing the constant threat of heat casualties and equipment failure in the jungle.

Leadership is evaluated throughout the FTX. JOTC produces jungle warriors — graduates must demonstrate they can lead soldiers in jungle conditions, not just survive them.

The final assessment determines whether a student has demonstrated the knowledge, skills, and leadership required to plan and execute operations in dense jungle terrain. Successful graduates earn the Jungle Tab.

Patrol Phase Requirements
  • Plan and execute jungle ambush patrols
  • Jungle lane patrolling in zero-visibility terrain
  • Stream crossings under simulated fire
  • Demonstrate leadership in jungle conditions
  • Sustained operations with minimal sleep and resupply
  • Successfully complete all graded leadership events

Winter is Hawaii's rainy season. Heavy, sustained rain floods streams, saturates trails, and makes every piece of gear permanently wet. Water crossings become significantly more dangerous with swollen rivers and unpredictable flash floods.

Winter Gear Tips
  • Waterproof dry bags for critical gear and electronics
  • Extra socks — change often to prevent trench foot
  • Anti-chafing products for constant wet conditions
  • Quick-dry clothing layers
  • Waterproof notebooks for patrol planning

Spring is transitional in Hawaii. Rainfall decreases, temperatures are comfortable, and conditions are generally favorable for training. Stream levels drop to manageable levels, making water crossings less hazardous than winter classes.

Spring Gear Tips
  • Sunscreen and insect repellent — essential daily
  • Lightweight moisture-wicking layers
  • Hydration system — heat injuries still common
  • Broken-in jungle boots (not new)
  • Anti-fungal foot powder for humid conditions

Summer is the dry season and offers the best training conditions. Reduced rainfall means drier trails, lower stream levels, and more predictable weather. Heat is the primary challenge — temperatures push into the upper 80s with sustained humidity.

Summer Gear Tips
  • Maximum hydration capacity — heat casualties spike
  • Electrolyte supplements for sustained operations
  • Lightweight, breathable clothing
  • Sun protection — boonie cap and sunscreen
  • Insect repellent — mosquitoes are worst in summer

Fall marks the return of rain and tropical storm risk. Hawaii's late hurricane season brings unpredictable storms, increasing rainfall, and rising humidity. Training may be impacted by weather delays, and stream crossings become more hazardous as water levels rise.

Fall Gear Tips
  • Waterproof bags for all sensitive equipment
  • Rain gear — lightweight, packable layers
  • Extra foot care supplies — wet conditions return
  • Mental preparation for unpredictable weather
  • Backup fire-starting supplies in waterproof container

Don't Be That Jungle Student.

Every failure at JOTC has a type. We built a fix for each one.

🪝
Can't Tie Knots?

10+ knots tested at JOTC, from prusik to Swiss seat. Rope bridges and rappelling depend on getting these right. No excuses.
🌊
Afraid of Water?

River crossings, waterfall rappels, swimming with full kit. JOTC is a waterborne course. If you can't swim, don't show up.
🧭
Bad with a Compass?

Triple-canopy blocks GPS. Your compass is your lifeline under the jungle canopy. Master land nav or get lost — literally.
💧
Can't Handle Humidity?

Everything rots, chafes, and molds in the jungle. Gear rusts overnight. Your body breaks down. Prepare for permanent discomfort.
👁
Weak on Patrols?

Ambush and recon patrols in zero-visibility terrain. You can't see the enemy, and they can't see you. Noise and light discipline is everything.
🌱
No Survival Skills?

Build shelters from palm fronds, start fire in the rain, find water from bamboo and vines. The jungle provides — if you know how to look.
