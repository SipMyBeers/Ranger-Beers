The Combat Medical Badge was established in March 1945 to recognize the unique sacrifices of medical personnel who provide care under hostile fire. It is the medical equivalent of the Combat Infantryman Badge (CIB).

Eligibility requires holding a medical MOS — primarily 68W (Combat Medic Specialist) — and being assigned or attached to an infantry, ranger, or Special Forces unit of brigade size or smaller engaged in active ground combat.

Medical officers (surgeons, physicians, physician assistants) serving with qualifying units are also eligible. The Soldier must be performing medical duties at the time of the combat engagement.

Eligible Personnel
  • 68W Combat Medic Specialist — primary qualifying MOS
  • Medical officers assigned to infantry/SF units
  • Flight medics during ground combat operations
  • Special Operations medical sergeants (18D)
  • Medical department Soldiers performing medical duties in combat
  • Must be assigned to a unit of brigade size or smaller
  • Retroactive eligibility to World War II (6 Dec 1941)

The core requirement for the CMB is performing medical duties while personally present and under hostile fire during active ground combat. Simply being present in a combat zone is not sufficient — the medic must be actively providing care.

The medic must be engaged in treating casualties, performing triage, or evacuating wounded under direct or indirect enemy fire. The emphasis is on the medical action — a medic who only returns fire without treating patients does not meet the criteria.

Combat engagements include firefights, ambushes, IED attacks, mortar/rocket attacks, and any ground combat situation where the medic treats wounded while under hostile fire.

Qualifying Combat Actions
  • Treating casualties during a firefight or ambush
  • Performing battlefield triage under direct fire
  • Casualty evacuation under hostile conditions
  • Providing aid during IED / mine strike engagements
  • Medical care during mortar, rocket, or artillery attack
  • Emergency surgical intervention in active combat
  • Sustained medical operations in prolonged engagement

Award processing begins with a commander's recommendation through the chain of command. The recommendation must be supported by evidence establishing the Soldier's presence in combat while performing medical duties under hostile fire.

Documentation typically includes sworn witness statements, after-action reports, medical treatment records, TCCC (Tactical Combat Casualty Care) cards, and SIGACT (Significant Activity) reports confirming the engagement.

The CMB can be awarded retroactively with sufficient documentation. Multiple awards (up to 3) are denoted by superimposed stars on the wreath, with each star representing a qualifying conflict.

Processing Steps
  • Commander initiates DA Form 638 recommendation
  • Gather sworn witness statements confirming combat medical action
  • Attach after-action reports and SIGACT records
  • Include medical treatment logs and TCCC documentation
  • Verify MOS eligibility and unit assignment orders
  • Route through chain of command to brigade commander

The CMB was created in March 1945 to recognize combat medics who served under fire during WWII. It was retroactive to December 6, 1941, covering all World War II combat operations. The badge gave medical personnel the same recognition infantrymen received with the CIB (established 1943).

WWII Context
  • Combat medics had some of the highest casualty rates in the war
  • D-Day medics treated wounded on open beaches under machine gun fire
  • Pacific theater medics faced tropical disease alongside combat
  • Aid stations routinely came under mortar and artillery fire
  • The Red Cross armband offered little protection in practice
Korea — The Forgotten War
1950 – 1953
TheaterKorean Peninsula

Key BattleChosin Reservoir
ChallengeMorphine froze in syringes

Korean War medics faced brutal conditions. At the Chosin Reservoir, medics treated casualties at -35°F — morphine froze in syringes, plasma froze in bottles, and bandages turned rigid. Combat medics performed their duties under Chinese human wave attacks.

Korea Context
  • Chosin Reservoir medics performed surgery in foxholes at -35°F
  • MASH (Mobile Army Surgical Hospital) units revolutionized battlefield medicine
  • Helicopter medevac first used extensively in combat
  • Medics often the last to evacuate during retreats
  • Korean War saw second award (1 star) CMBs for WWII veterans
Vietnam — "Doc" in the Jungle
1955 – 1975
TheaterRepublic of Vietnam
EnvironmentJungle, triple canopy
Enemy TacticMedics deliberately targeted
Avg Age of Medic19 years old
InnovationDustoff medevac
Survival RateImproved to ~82% if reached

Vietnam medics — universally called "Doc" — were deliberately targeted by enemy forces to demoralize units. The NVA and VC specifically aimed for medics to deny medical care to wounded Americans. Despite this, medics repeatedly exposed themselves to save their brothers.

Vietnam Context
  • Medics frequently targeted by snipers and ambush teams
  • Dustoff helicopter medevac became the lifeline of the battlefield
  • Many medics earned both the CMB and valor awards simultaneously
  • Average combat medic age was just 19 years old
  • Jungle conditions caused tropical disease alongside combat wounds
GWOT — IEDs, TCCC & Modern Combat Medicine
2001 – Present
TheatersAfghanistan & Iraq
Primary ThreatIEDs / blast injuries
DoctrineTCCC revolutionized care
TourniquetsEvery Soldier carries one
Survival Rate~90%+ if reached alive
Golden HourMedevac standard enforced

The Global War on Terror transformed combat medicine. IED blast injuries created complex polytrauma that demanded advanced battlefield care. Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC) became the standard, and combat medics became some of the most highly trained Soldiers in the Army.

GWOT Context
  • IED blast injuries required new treatment protocols and training
  • TCCC doctrine dramatically improved battlefield survival rates
  • Combat medics trained to a near-paramedic level
  • "Golden Hour" medevac policy saved thousands of lives
  • Highest battlefield survival rate in U.S. military history

What Qualifies?

Which MOS Eligible?

68W is the primary qualifying MOS, but medical officers, flight medics, and Special Operations medics (18D) may also qualify. Know the full eligibility list.

CMB vs EFMB?

The CMB recognizes combat medical action under fire. The EFMB tests medical skills in a non-combat evaluation. Both are prestigious — but earned differently.

Multiple Awards?

Key Takeaway: The CMB is not earned through training or testing — it is earned in combat. The medic must hold a qualifying medical MOS, be assigned to a qualifying unit, and perform medical duties while personally present and under hostile fire. Documentation is critical — many deserving medics miss the award due to incomplete packets.
