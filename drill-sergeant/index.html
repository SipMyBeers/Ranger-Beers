The Academic Phase builds the doctrinal foundation every Drill Sergeant needs. Candidates study Army regulations governing Initial Entry Training, the training doctrine that governs how Soldiers are made, and the counseling techniques required to develop recruits.

Drill and ceremony mastery begins here — candidates must execute and teach every movement in TC 3-21.5 to standard. Voice projection and command presence are evaluated from Day One — if you cannot command a formation, you cannot be a Drill Sergeant.

Physical Readiness Training (PRT) methodology teaches candidates not just to be fit, but to train others using the Army's standardized physical training system. Written exams test doctrinal knowledge throughout this phase.

Academic Topics
  • Army training doctrine — AR 350-1, TR 350-6, TRADOC regulations
  • Drill and ceremony — TC 3-21.5 complete mastery
  • Physical Readiness Training — PRT methodology and execution
  • Developmental counseling techniques
  • Command voice projection and military bearing
  • Equal Opportunity and SHARP awareness
  • IET Soldier management and standards

Practicum is where the classroom meets the training field. Candidates conduct actual BCT training events, running lanes, delivering classes, and leading physical training sessions as if they were already wearing the campaign hat.

Teaching ability is the core competency tested here. Candidates must deliver instruction to standard — clear, organized, and effective. If a recruit cannot learn from you, you are not a Drill Sergeant.

Candidates who cannot manage IET Soldiers, fail to maintain training schedules, or demonstrate poor leadership under pressure are identified and counseled. Peer evaluations add another layer of accountability.

Practicum Events
  • Conduct BCT training lanes — weapons, first aid, land navigation
  • Deliver graded classes on Army subjects
  • Lead Physical Readiness Training for formations
  • Manage IET Soldier daily activities and schedules
  • Peer and cadre leadership evaluations
  • Drill and ceremony practical exercises
  • Counseling sessions — practice developmental counseling

The Validation Phase determines who earns the campaign hat and Drill Sergeant identification badge. Every skill learned in the first six weeks is tested under graded conditions — teaching, drill, PT leadership, and military knowledge.

The Commandant's Board is the culminating event. Candidates appear before senior cadre and must demonstrate their fitness, military knowledge, teaching ability, and readiness to assume the responsibilities of a Drill Sergeant.

Successful graduates earn the right to wear the iconic "brown round" and the Drill Sergeant identification badge — symbols of the Army's most visible and demanding leadership position in Initial Entry Training.

Validation Requirements
  • Pass all graded practical teaching evaluations
  • Score 70% or higher on comprehensive written exams
  • Successfully complete the Commandant's Board
  • Pass the final ACFT to Drill Sergeant standard
  • Demonstrate command presence and voice projection
  • Receive satisfactory peer and cadre evaluations

Winter at Fort Jackson is mild. Temperatures rarely drop below freezing for extended periods. Morning PT sessions can be chilly, but the moderate climate makes outdoor drill and physical training comfortable compared to northern posts.

Winter Tips
  • Light cold-weather layers for early morning PT
  • Rain gear — winter showers are common
  • Comfortable running weather for ACFT prep
  • Good season for outdoor drill and ceremony practice
  • Lowest heat casualty risk of the year

Spring at Fort Jackson transitions quickly from pleasant to hot. March classes enjoy moderate weather, but by May the heat and humidity build rapidly. Candidates arriving in late spring should begin heat acclimatization before reporting.

Spring Tips
  • Begin heat acclimatization 2 weeks before report date
  • Hydration discipline — start increasing water intake now
  • Train outdoors in warm weather to build tolerance
  • Sunscreen — long hours on drill pads and PT fields
  • Break in boots thoroughly before arrival

Summer is the most physically punishing season at Fort Jackson. Brutal heat and humidity turn drill pads into furnaces. Candidates must project their voice for hours in sweltering conditions while maintaining impeccable bearing. Heat casualties are a constant threat.

Summer Survival Tips
  • Heat acclimatization is mandatory — train in heat 2+ weeks prior
  • Hydrate aggressively — minimum 1 canteen per hour
  • Know heat casualty signs — you'll be responsible for trainees
  • Moisture-wicking undergarments under ACUs
  • Black Flag conditions may alter training schedules

Fall is the best season to attend DSA. Temperatures cool significantly from summer's extreme heat, humidity drops, and outdoor training conditions are ideal. Cool morning PT and comfortable afternoon drill sessions allow candidates to focus on performance rather than survival.

Fall Tips
  • Best weather for ACFT performance
  • September still warm — maintain hydration discipline
  • October/November mornings can be cool — light layers
  • Lower heat casualty risk than summer classes
  • Focus energy on teaching skills, not fighting the weather

Don't Be That DS Candidate.

Every failure at DSA has a type. We built a fix for each one.

📣
Can't Project Your Voice?

Command voice is non-negotiable. If a formation of 200 trainees can't hear you, you fail. Practice projecting from your diaphragm until it's second nature.
🏋
Weak PT Score?

Drill Sergeants lead from the front. If you can't outperform your trainees on every ACFT event, you have no business wearing the hat. Get your score up now.
📝
Bad at Teaching?

DSA is a teaching school. You must deliver clear, organized instruction that Soldiers can learn from. If you can't break down a task and teach it step-by-step, start practicing now.
🎤
Afraid of Public Speaking?

Every day as a Drill Sergeant is public speaking. Formations, classes, counseling sessions — you are always on stage. Overcome it before you arrive.
📖
Don't Know Regulations?

AR 350-1, TR 350-6, TC 3-21.5 — you will be tested on these. Showing up without studying the doctrine is a fast path to academic failure.
🧠
No Leadership Presence?

Bearing, confidence, and discipline are evaluated constantly. A Drill Sergeant commands respect through presence alone. If you don't have it, develop it.

The Drill Sergeant Creed

I am a Drill Sergeant. I will assist each individual in their efforts to become a highly motivated, well-disciplined, physically and mentally fit Soldier, capable of defeating any enemy on today's modern battlefield.

I will instill pride in all I train. Pride in self, in the Army, and in Country. I will insist that each Soldier meets and maintains the Army's standards of military bearing and courtesy, consistent with the highest traditions of the U.S. Army.

I will lead by example, never requiring a Soldier to attempt any task I would not do myself. But first, last, and always, I am an American Soldier. Sworn to defend the Constitution of the United States against all enemies, both foreign and domestic.

I am a Drill Sergeant.
