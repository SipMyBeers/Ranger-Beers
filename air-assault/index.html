Course Overview

What is Air Assault School?

The Sabalauski Air Assault School (TSAAS) at Fort Campbell, Kentucky, is a ten-day course that trains soldiers in combat air assault operations. Students learn rappelling, sling-load operations, and helicopter insertion techniques used to deploy troops and equipment into combat via rotary-wing aircraft.

Air Assault School is one of the most physically demanding short courses in the Army. With an attrition rate near 50%, the course earns its reputation as "Ten Days of Hell." Graduates are awarded the Air Assault Badge and the skills to plan and execute air assault missions across all terrains.

Course Phases

10 Days to the Air Assault Badge

  • Day 0 Obstacle Course — The infamous Day Zero obstacle course. A timed combat assault course that eliminates up to 20% of candidates before training begins. Includes rope climbs, wall scaling, and confidence obstacles.
  • Days 1–3 Phase 1: Combat Assault — Aircraft familiarization, pathfinder operations, hand-and-arm signals, helicopter safety, and combat air assault planning. Covers pickup/landing zone selection and marking procedures.
  • Days 4–6 Phase 2: Sling Load — Rigging equipment for helicopter transport. Students learn to inspect, rig, and hook up cargo to rotary-wing aircraft using various sling-load configurations. Written and hands-on testing.
  • Days 7–9 Phase 3: Rappelling — Rappel training from the 34-foot tower and UH-60 Black Hawk helicopters. Students must demonstrate proficiency in rappelling techniques and belay operations. Includes a night rappel.
  • Day 10 12-Mile Ruck March — The final event: a 12-mile ruck march with a 35-pound minimum load, completed within 3 hours. Graduation ceremony follows for those who pass.
Key Standards

What It Takes

Candidates must pass the APFT with a minimum 60 points per event before attending. The Day Zero obstacle course must be completed within the time standard. Each phase ends with a written and practical exam. The 12-mile ruck march must be completed within 3 hours with a 35-pound minimum load.

Air Assault School is unforgiving — there are no second chances on most events. A single NO-GO on a graded event means elimination from the course. The combination of academic rigor, physical demands, and zero-defect grading makes the ~50% graduation rate one of the lowest among Army skill-badge courses.
