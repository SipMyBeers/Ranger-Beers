Day Zero is the gut check. Students must complete a timed obstacle course that includes rope climbs, wall climbs, low crawls, and balance beams. Approximately 20% of all students are eliminated on Day Zero alone. This is designed to test physical fitness, mental toughness, and the willingness to push through discomfort.

Injuries from the obstacle course are also common. Students who arrive without adequate grip strength and upper-body endurance are the first to go. The pace is unrelenting and the cadre have zero tolerance for quitters.

Day Zero Events
  • Timed obstacle course — pass/fail, no retests
  • Rope climb — full height, no leg wrapping
  • Wall climb — multiple heights
  • Low crawl — under barbed wire obstacles
  • Cargo net — climb and descend under time
  • Balance beam — elevated stability test

Phase I covers combat assault operations and sling-load procedures. Students learn to inspect, rig, and certify helicopter sling loads — a critical skill for moving equipment by air. The written sling-load test is a major elimination point.

Students also learn helicopter safety, landing zone operations, and pathfinder basics. Aircraft orientation covers the UH-60 Black Hawk and CH-47 Chinook, including approach/departure angles, danger areas, and loading procedures.

The academic rigor of Phase I catches many students off guard. The sling-load written exam requires precise knowledge of rigging procedures, weight calculations, and inspection criteria. Studying the manuals cold before arriving is essential.

Phase I Training
  • Sling load rigging — proper attachment and inspection
  • Sling load written exam — major elimination point
  • Sling load hands-on test — rig and certify under time
  • Aircraft orientation — UH-60 Black Hawk, CH-47 Chinook
  • LZ/PZ operations — setup and management
  • Pathfinder operations — basic procedures and signals

Phase II takes students to the rappel tower and then to live helicopter rappelling from UH-60 Black Hawks. Students must demonstrate safe rappelling technique from both the tower and aircraft. Refusal to rappel means immediate dismissal.

Cold weather makes the ropes and gloves difficult to manage. Students who have not practiced Swiss seat rigging and basic knot work will struggle under time pressure. Confidence on the tower translates directly to confidence on the aircraft.

Night rappelling adds another layer of difficulty — students must trust their training and technique when they cannot see the ground clearly. Belay procedures must be executed perfectly every time.

Phase II Training
  • 34-foot rappel tower — multiple descents, day and night
  • UH-60 helicopter rappel — live aircraft, full kit
  • Swiss seat rigging — proper construction and inspection
  • Belay procedures — safety protocols and communication
  • Night rappel — limited visibility operations
  • Refusal to rappel — immediate dismissal from course

The final event and the one that gives Air Assault School its reputation: "The Ten Toughest Days in the Army." The 12-mile ruck march with a 35-pound minimum load must be completed in 3 hours or less.

Students who have survived everything else often fall here due to blisters, heat, or simply running out of gas. The route at Fort Campbell is notoriously hilly, and the pace required to finish under three hours is punishing.

Progressive ruck training in the weeks before the course is non-negotiable. Students who show up without putting serious miles under a heavy pack will not make the time standard. Foot care and pacing strategy are just as important as raw fitness.

Phase III Events
  • 12-mile ruck march — 3:00:00 time limit, no exceptions
  • 35lb minimum dry weight — weighed before start
  • Fort Campbell route — hilly terrain, no flat stretches
  • Foot care — blisters are the #1 preventable failure
  • Pacing strategy — 15:00/mile pace required
  • Graduation — pinning of the Air Assault Badge

Winter at Campbell is cold and wet. Temperatures regularly drop below freezing, and students spend hours waiting at the rappel tower in the wind. Cold hands affect grip strength on the rappel rope — glove inserts are essential.

Winter Gear Tips
  • Glove inserts — cold hands fail on the rope
  • Polypro base layers under BDUs
  • Neck gaiter — tower wind is brutal
  • Hand warmers for waiting periods
  • Waterproof boots — slush and mud everywhere

Spring brings the best temperatures for physical events. The 12-mile ruck march is most comfortable in March-April. However, Tennessee spring storms can delay rappel tower and sling-load training.

Spring Gear Tips
  • Rain jacket — afternoon thunderstorms are common
  • Extra socks — wet boots from morning dew
  • Allergy meds — Tennessee pollen peaks in April
  • Light layers for temperature swings
  • Broken-in boots for the ruck march

Summer is the hardest season for Air Assault. The 12-mile ruck march in 90°F heat with full kit produces the most heat casualties of any season. Hydration discipline must start days before the event.

Summer Gear Tips
  • Hydration system — CamelBak under ruck
  • Electrolyte packets for every water refill
  • Anti-chafe for ruck march — humidity causes hot spots
  • Cooling towel for tower waits
  • Arrive early to heat acclimatize

Fall is the best time for Air Assault School. Temperatures are ideal for both physical events and tower training. The 12-mile ruck march in cool fall weather has the highest completion rates of any season.

Fall Gear Tips
  • Light layers for morning-to-afternoon variation
  • Standard packing list is sufficient
  • Broken-in boots — no excuses
  • Sunglasses for clear-sky rappel days
  • Lip balm — dry fall air cracks lips
