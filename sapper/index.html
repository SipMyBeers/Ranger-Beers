Assessment and Selection mirrors RAP Week — it's the initial filter designed to drop candidates who aren't physically or technically ready. The ACFT, 12-mile ruck march, water survival assessment, and combat engineer stakes (demolitions calculations, bridging, mine warfare) eliminate approximately 20% of candidates in the first three days.

Unlike other Army schools where physical fitness alone gets you through the door, SLC demands technical proficiency from day one. The combat engineer stakes test your knowledge of demolitions formulas, bridging procedures, and mine warfare before the course even begins in earnest.

Land navigation is tested both day and night — a critical skill that will be used throughout the course. The written exam covers engineer fundamentals and weeds out candidates who haven't studied before arrival.

Assessment Events
  • ACFT — Army Combat Fitness Test (pass required)
  • 12-mile ruck march — 3:00:00 time limit with 35-lb load
  • Water survival assessment — swim evaluation
  • Combat engineer stakes — demolitions calculations, bridging, mine warfare
  • Land navigation — day and night courses
  • Written exam — engineer fundamentals baseline

General Engineering phase covers the breadth of combat engineering — demolitions calculations and employment, obstacle construction, bridging operations, mine warfare, and route clearance. Students must demonstrate technical proficiency on calculations (demolitions formulas, timber calculations) and hands-on tasks.

This is where demolitions calculations eliminate a significant number of candidates. The formulas for breaching charges, cratering charges, and timber cutting charges must be memorized and applied under time pressure. There is no partial credit — get the math wrong and the charge fails.

Bridging operations cover both float bridge and Bailey bridge construction and emplacement. Route clearance procedures teach systematic approaches to detecting and neutralizing obstacles and IEDs along movement corridors.

Engineering Phase Training
  • Demolitions — calculations, priming, firing systems, charge placement
  • Obstacle construction — wire obstacles, minefields, abatis
  • Bridging operations — float bridge, Bailey bridge emplacement
  • Route clearance — systematic detection and neutralization
  • Mine warfare — emplacement, marking, detection, recording
  • Timber construction calculations — load-bearing formulas

The Tactical Phase combines engineering skills with small unit tactics. Students conduct tactical breaching operations (mechanical, ballistic, explosive), route clearance patrols, and combat engineer support to maneuver forces. Graded on leadership, technical execution, and decision-making under stress.

This is where classroom knowledge meets combat reality. Breaching requires precise technique — mechanical breaching with engineer tools, ballistic breaching with shotguns, and explosive breaching with shaped charges all demand different skill sets executed under time pressure and simulated fire.

Engineer reconnaissance operations teach candidates to assess terrain, identify obstacles, and recommend courses of action to supported maneuver commanders. Tactical obstacle emplacement applies the construction skills from the Engineering Phase in a combat context.

Tactical Phase Training
  • Tactical breaching — mechanical, ballistic, and explosive methods
  • Route clearance — mounted and dismounted patrol operations
  • Engineer reconnaissance — terrain assessment and obstacle identification
  • Combat engineer support — integration with maneuver forces
  • Tactical obstacle emplacement — defensive and protective positions
  • Decision-making under stress — graded leadership scenarios

The Patrol/FTX phase is a multi-day continuous operations exercise where candidates plan and execute engineer-supported combat operations while rotating through graded leadership positions. Sleep and food deprivation mirror Ranger School conditions. Candidates must demonstrate the ability to lead under extreme stress.

Each candidate must earn at least one GO in a graded leadership position — Patrol Leader, Platoon Sergeant, or Squad Leader. The evaluations measure your ability to plan, brief, and execute complex engineer operations while managing a team of exhausted, hungry, and stressed candidates.

This phase separates technically competent engineers from engineer leaders. Knowing how to calculate a breaching charge is not enough — you must be able to lead a team through the entire operation from planning through execution under combat conditions.

Patrol/FTX Training
  • Graded patrols — PL, PSG, and SL leadership positions
  • Continuous operations — multi-day FTX with minimal sleep
  • Engineer support planning — OPORDs and tactical integration
  • Demolitions operations — live charges in field environment
  • Tactical movement — under combat loads, day and night
  • Peer evaluations — assessed by fellow candidates

The Final Assessment consolidates all training and testing into a comprehensive evaluation. Candidates must demonstrate retained knowledge and physical capacity after 26 days of demanding operations. Peer evaluations are also factored in.

After 26 days of demolitions, breaching, tactical operations, and continuous field exercises, this final phase determines who has retained the knowledge and maintained the physical capacity to earn the Sapper Tab. It's a deliberate test of what you remember when you're at your most exhausted.

Successful candidates are awarded the Sapper Tab — one of only four permanent skill tabs authorized by the U.S. Army. The tabbing ceremony marks entry into an elite group of combat engineer leaders.

Final Assessment Events
  • Comprehensive evaluation — all engineer skills tested
  • Peer evaluations — factored into final determination
  • Final physical events — endurance under cumulative fatigue
  • Equipment turn-in & after-action reviews
  • Graduation — Sapper Tab pinning ceremony

Course Duration28 days

Missouri winters bring freezing temps, ice, and snow. Demolitions and construction tasks in frozen ground are significantly harder. Digging fighting positions, emplacing obstacles, and conducting bridging operations all take longer and demand more physical effort when the ground is frozen solid.

Frostbite risk during the extended FTX is a real danger. Your hands are your most critical tool as an engineer — lose dexterity to cold and you can't tie knots, prime charges, or operate equipment. Cold weather management is a survival skill, not a comfort measure.

Winter-Specific Gear
  • Cold weather layers — waffle top and bottom
  • Glove inserts — maintain dexterity for engineer tasks
  • Hand warmers — keep hands functional for demo work
  • Insulated boots — protect against frozen ground
  • Neck gaiter & beanie — heat loss prevention
  • Sleeping pad — ground insulation during FTX

Rain and mud make movement and construction tasks challenging. Variable temps mean layering is key. Missouri spring weather can swing from near-freezing mornings to 70°F afternoons, requiring constant gear management while conducting engineer operations.

Ticks and chiggers become active in spring — and Missouri has plenty of both. Bites get infected fast when you're sleep-deprived and can't properly treat them. Wet conditions make demolitions work more complex — keeping detonation systems dry is critical for firing reliability.

Spring-Specific Gear
  • Wet weather layers — rain jacket and pants
  • Bug spray — DEET for ticks and chiggers
  • Waterproof bags — protect equipment and dry clothes
  • Extra socks — rotate to prevent trench foot
  • Layering system — handle 30°F temperature swings
  • Foot powder — wet feet are guaranteed

Missouri heat and humidity are brutal — 95°F+ with high humidity. Heat casualties are the biggest threat during physical events and the 12-mile ruck. The combination of heavy exertion, combat loads, and oppressive heat creates dangerous conditions during every outdoor evolution.

The 12-mile ruck march during Assessment is the peak heat casualty event in summer classes. Candidates who haven't heat-acclimated before arrival are at serious risk. The FTX phase compounds the problem with days of continuous movement and engineering tasks under direct sun.

Summer-Specific Gear
  • Electrolytes — add to every canteen, critical for heat management
  • Sunscreen — SPF 50+, reapply throughout the day
  • Extra water — carry maximum capacity
  • Cooling towels — neck cooling during breaks
  • Anti-chafe cream — sweat and movement cause blisters fast
  • Light, quick-dry undergarments — minimize heat retention
Heat Management Tips
  • Heat-acclimate before arrival — train in full kit in heat for 2+ weeks
  • Drink water before you're thirsty — by the time you feel it, you're behind
  • Eat salt with every meal — electrolyte loss is the silent killer
  • Report heat symptoms early — heat stroke can kill
  • Wet your neck and wrists during any break opportunity
Fall Sapper — Best Conditions Overall
Sep – Nov
MonthsSeptember – November
Temperature45–80°F
ConditionsMost temperate season
Biggest ThreatLate Nov cold snaps
Bug PressureLow — tapering off
FTX WeatherCool nights, mild days
Course Duration28 days

Most temperate season. Cool nights, mild days. Best conditions overall but late November can bring early winter weather. Fall classes avoid the extremes of summer heat and winter cold, letting candidates focus on the course itself rather than fighting the environment.

That said, Missouri weather is unpredictable. Late November classes can face freezing temperatures that catch candidates off guard if they packed for fall. A versatile kit that handles both 70°F days and 30°F nights is essential.

Fall-Specific Gear
  • Light cold weather layers — versatile for temperature swings
  • Versatile kit — handle 40°F temperature ranges
  • Beanie and glove inserts — for cold nights during FTX
  • Poncho liner (woobie) — essential for any season
  • Standard wet weather gear — fall rain is common
Why Fall Is Preferred
  • No extreme heat — lower heat casualty risk during ruck and PT
  • No frozen ground — digging and construction tasks are manageable
  • Bugs declining — less distraction and infection risk
  • Moderate FTX conditions — focus on leadership, not survival
  • Lighter packing list — fewer extreme weather items needed
