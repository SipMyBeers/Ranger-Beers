Module 1
Course Overview

The Sapper Leader Course (SLC) is a 28-day combat engineering course at Fort Leonard Wood, Missouri. It is one of only four permanent skill tabs authorized for wear by the U.S. Army, alongside Special Forces, Ranger, and President's Hundred. The washout rate averages 65% — preparation is not optional.

Course Structure:

  • Assessment & Selection (Days 1-3): ACFT, 12-mile ruck, water survival, land nav, engineer stakes
  • General Engineering (Days 4-12): Demolitions, mine warfare, bridging, obstacles, timber
  • Tactical Phase (Days 13-20): Breaching, route clearance, engineer recon, tactical operations
  • Patrol/FTX (Days 21-26): Multi-day field exercise with graded leadership positions
  • Final Assessment (Days 27-28): Comprehensive evaluation, peer reviews, graduation

What Makes Sapper Different:

Unlike Ranger School which focuses on light infantry leadership, SLC trains combat engineers in technical demolitions, obstacle emplacement/breaching, and engineer integration with maneuver forces. You must be physically fit AND technically proficient. The demolitions calculations alone eliminate candidates who thought they could "wing it."

The Essayons Spirit:

"Essayons" — Let Us Try. This is the motto of the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers. Sapper School tests your willingness to attempt difficult technical tasks under stress, when failure has real consequences. The tab represents both technical mastery and leadership under pressure.

Module 2
Prerequisites & Physical Standards

Physical fitness is the foundation, but technical knowledge is the separator. You must pass both to earn the tab.

Entry Prerequisites:

  • ACFT: Pass the Army Combat Fitness Test (standards vary by MOS)
  • 12-Mile Ruck March: 35 lb dry + weapon, under 3:00:00
  • Water Survival Assessment: Swim evaluation in uniform
  • Basic demolitions knowledge: Familiarity with formulas and calculations
  • Land navigation: Day and night course capability

Physical Standards Required:

  • Ability to carry 35-50 lb ruck for 12+ miles under 3 hours
  • Upper body endurance for obstacle construction and demolitions work
  • Swimming confidence for water survival assessment
  • Ability to function on 2-4 hours of sleep during FTX
  • Manual dexterity for knot tying and charge preparation

Technical Prerequisites:

  • Memorize demolitions formulas (P = R x K x C)
  • Understand basic timber calculation principles
  • Know standard military knots: bowline, square knot, figure-eight
  • Understand mine warfare fundamentals
  • Familiarity with basic bridging concepts

Pre-Course Study Plan:

Start at least 8 weeks before your report date. Focus on: 1) Demolitions calculations — this is the #1 academic failure point, 2) Ruck endurance — you'll ruck more than you think, 3) Knots — tested and used throughout, 4) Land nav — you'll navigate day and night.

Module 3
Engineer Stakes — The Initial Filter

Engineer Stakes occur during Assessment & Selection (Days 1-3). This is where technically unprepared candidates are identified and dropped before the course investment grows. You cannot fake engineer knowledge.

Engineer Stakes Components:

  • Demolitions Calculations Test: Written test on charge formulas, placement, and safety
  • Wire Obstacle Construction: Build a specified wire obstacle under time pressure
  • Mine Warfare Knowledge: Identification, emplacement, and recording procedures
  • Basic Knots Test: Tie specified knots on demand
  • Land Navigation: Day and night courses — 4 of 6 points day, 1 of 3 points night
  • Written Fundamentals Exam: Engineer basics, safety, and procedures

Demolitions Calculations — Critical:

The formula P = R x K x C must be memorized and applied correctly:

  • P = Pounds of explosive (TNT equivalent)
  • R = Thickness of material to be breached (feet)
  • K = Material factor (concrete = 0.5, steel = 1.0, etc.)
  • C = Breaching factor (depends on placement and confinement)

Knots You Must Know Cold:

  • Square knot — joining two ropes of equal diameter
  • Bowline — fixed loop that won't slip
  • Figure-eight — stopper knot, also used in mountaineering
  • Clove hitch — securing rope to objects
  • Round turn and two half hitches — securing under tension

Preparation Strategy:

Drill calculations until you can solve standard problems in under 60 seconds. Practice knots until you can tie them with eyes closed or in the dark. The stakes are deliberately stressful — you must perform under pressure with RIs watching every move.

Module 4
Packing List Deep Dive

Your packing list is your life for 28 days. Missouri weather varies dramatically by season. Pack wrong and you suffer — or fail.

Universal Essentials:

  • Broken-in boots (2 pairs minimum) — Missouri terrain is rocky and wet
  • Socks (12+ pairs) — wool or synthetic, no cotton
  • Boot socks and liner socks — double socking reduces blisters
  • ACU/OCP uniforms (3-4 sets) — you'll be wet and dirty daily
  • Undergarments — synthetic, quick-dry material
  • Waffle top and bottom — cold weather layering
  • Gloves and glove inserts — for demo work and cold
  • Beanie and neck gaiter — heat retention

Engineer-Specific Gear:

  • Work gloves — leather or mechanics gloves for demo/construction
  • 550 cord (100+ feet) — essential for knots, rigging, expedient charges
  • Carabiner — at least one locking carabiner for mountaineering phase
  • Protractor and military map case — land nav is critical
  • Waterproof notebook — Rite in Rain or equivalent
  • Pens and pencils — multiple, stored waterproof
  • Calculator — basic scientific calculator for demo calcs (check current SOP)

Field Gear:

  • Ruck with frame — must carry 50+ lbs comfortably
  • Sleep system — including waterproof bivy
  • Poncho and poncho liner (woobie) — rain and warmth
  • Entrenching tool — compact, sturdy
  • Headlamp with red lens — night work essential
  • Earplugs — sleeping near generators and vehicles

Season-Specific Considerations:

  • Winter (Dec-Feb): Heavy cold weather gear, hand warmers, insulated boots
  • Spring (Mar-May): Rain gear, bug spray (ticks/chiggers), waterproof bags
  • Summer (Jun-Aug): Extra electrolytes, sunscreen, anti-chafe cream, maximum water capacity
  • Fall (Sep-Nov): Versatile layers, light cold weather gear for November

Pro Tips:

  • Waterproof EVERYTHING — Missouri is wet
  • Label all gear — theft happens, mix-ups are common
  • Pack your ruck the same way every time — muscle memory
  • Bring foot care supplies — moleskin, tape, foot powder, nail clippers
  • Pack a small dry bag with one set of dry clothes for after FTX
Module 5
Demolitions Math Mastery

Demolitions calculations are the most tested technical skill in Sapper School. Get them wrong and you fail. There is no partial credit.

Breaching Charge Formula (Memorize):

P = R x K x C

  • P = Pounds of TNT needed
  • R = Thickness (feet) of material to breach
  • K = Material factor (Concrete = 0.5, Steel = 1.0, Rock = 0.8, Earth = 0.3)
  • C = Breaching factor (External charge = 1.5, Uncased internal = 1.0, Cased internal = 0.5)

Timber Cutting Formula:

P = (D² / 40) x K

  • P = Pounds of TNT needed
  • D = Diameter of timber (inches)
  • K = Material factor (Timber = 1.0 for most species)
  • 40 = Constant for timber cutting

Steel Cutting Formula:

P = 3/8 x A

  • P = Pounds of TNT needed
  • A = Cross-sectional area (square inches)
  • 3/8 = Constant for steel cutting

Explosive Equivalents (Must Know):

  • TNT = 1.0 (baseline)
  • C4 = 1.34 (more powerful than TNT by weight)
  • M112 Block Demolition Charge = 1.25 lb TNT equivalent
  • M183 Demolition Charge Assembly = 20 lb TNT equivalent

Practice Problems:

Drill these until answers are automatic:

  1. Breach 3-foot concrete wall with external charge: P = 3 x 0.5 x 1.5 = 2.25 lb TNT
  2. Cut 20-inch diameter timber: P = (400/40) x 1 = 10 lb TNT
  3. Breach 2-foot steel door with cased internal charge: P = 2 x 1.0 x 0.5 = 1.0 lb TNT
  4. Cut steel beam with 4 sq in cross-section: P = 3/8 x 4 = 1.5 lb TNT

Testing Standards:

You will be tested on calculations under time pressure with no reference materials. The standard is 100% accuracy — no partial credit. One wrong answer on a critical test can result in academic failure and course drop.

Module 6
Phase-Specific Tips

Assessment & Selection (Days 1-3):

  • ACFT is first — be ready to perform immediately upon arrival
  • 12-mile ruck is the big physical filter — practice with 35+ lbs
  • Engineer stakes are pass/fail — technical knowledge is non-negotiable
  • Land nav: Terrain association is king, but pace count matters at night
  • Peer evaluations start immediately — how you treat others matters

General Engineering (Days 4-12):

  • Demolitions calculations are tested weekly — stay sharp
  • Wire obstacle construction — practice before arrival, saves time
  • Mine warfare — memorize marking systems and reporting formats
  • Bridging — pay attention to assembly order and safety procedures
  • Timber calculations — know the formulas cold

Tactical Phase (Days 13-20):

  • Breaching types: Mechanical (tools), Ballistic (shotguns), Explosive (charges)
  • Route clearance — systematic procedures, no shortcuts
  • Engineer recon — terrain assessment and obstacle identification
  • Integration with maneuver forces — support, don't lead
  • Time management — you have limited time to plan and execute

Patrol/FTX (Days 21-26):

  • Graded leadership positions — you must get at least one GO as PL, PSG, or SL
  • Continuous operations — sleep deprivation is real
  • OPORDs and FRAGOs — brief clearly under stress
  • Peer leadership — candidates evaluate each other
  • Field expedient charges — improvisation under pressure

Final Assessment (Days 27-28):

  • Comprehensive written exam — all topics covered
  • Final physical events — endurance under cumulative fatigue
  • Peer evaluations factor into final decision
  • Equipment turn-in — don't lose gear, track everything
Module 7
Graduation Requirements

Earning the Sapper Tab requires meeting standards across all phases. There is no partial graduation.

Academic Requirements:

  • Pass all written examinations (70% minimum, varies by test)
  • Pass all practical exercises (GO/NO-GO standards)
  • Demonstrate retained knowledge through cumulative testing
  • No academic failures in critical skill areas

Physical Requirements:

  • Pass all physical fitness evaluations
  • Complete 12-mile ruck march under time limit
  • Pass water survival assessment
  • Maintain operational capability through FTX

Leadership Requirements:

  • Minimum one GO in a graded leadership position (PL, PSG, or SL)
  • Acceptable peer evaluation scores
  • Demonstrated ability to lead under stress
  • Effective communication and decision-making

Technical Requirements:

  • Pass demolitions calculations tests
  • Demonstrate safe handling of explosives and demolitions equipment
  • Successfully construct wire obstacles and fighting positions
  • Demonstrate mine warfare proficiency
  • Pass bridging practical exercises

The Tab:

Upon successful completion, you are awarded the Sapper Tab — one of only four permanent skill tabs in the U.S. Army. It is worn on the left shoulder of the uniform, below the Ranger Tab if also earned. The tab represents 28 days of the Army's most demanding combat engineer training.

After Graduation:

Graduates are assigned as Sapper-qualified engineers, eligible for specialized assignments and advanced training. The knowledge and leadership skills developed at SLC serve throughout an engineer career, from platoon leader to battalion command.
