Course Overview

What is RSLC?

The Reconnaissance and Surveillance Leaders Course (RSLC) is the U.S. Army's premier course for developing reconnaissance leaders, conducted at Fort Moore, Georgia. The course trains soldiers to plan, execute, and lead reconnaissance and surveillance operations at the company level and below in a contested environment.

RSLC produces scouts and reconnaissance leaders capable of operating independently deep in enemy territory for extended periods. Graduates possess advanced skills in land navigation, communications, intelligence collection, and surveillance techniques that make them invaluable assets to their commanders. The course's low graduation rate reflects its demanding standards.

Course Phases

4 Weeks Behind Enemy Lines

  • Week 1 Individual Skills — Advanced land navigation, tactical communications, intelligence reporting formats, surveillance techniques, hide-site construction, and individual fieldcraft required for extended operations in denied areas.
  • Wks 2-3 Patrol Operations — Reconnaissance patrols, surveillance site operations, long-range observation post establishment, evasion techniques, route and area reconnaissance, and small-team tactical movement in hostile territory.
  • Week 4 FTX — 72+ hour culminating field training exercise requiring full mission planning, execution of reconnaissance and surveillance tasks, intelligence collection, reporting to higher headquarters, and exfiltration under simulated combat conditions.
Key Standards

What It Takes

Candidates must demonstrate advanced land navigation skills in challenging terrain, establish and operate surveillance sites for extended periods, and produce accurate, timely intelligence reports that meet operational standards. Every report must contain actionable information that a commander can use to make decisions.

The culminating FTX is the ultimate test — students must plan, brief, and execute a multi-day reconnaissance mission from infiltration through exfiltration, collecting and reporting intelligence while avoiding detection by opposing forces. Failure to complete the FTX results in course failure regardless of previous performance.
