Course Overview

What is the EFMB?

The Expert Field Medical Badge (EFMB) is the medical branch's equivalent of the EIB. Established in 1965, it is awarded to medical soldiers who demonstrate exceptional proficiency in field medical skills, warrior tasks, and physical fitness. The EFMB has consistently maintained the lowest pass rate of any expert-level badge in the Army.

The EFMB tests not only medical knowledge but also core soldier skills. Candidates must be equally proficient in trauma care, evacuation procedures, land navigation, weapons handling, and NBC operations. The breadth of tested tasks — combined with zero-deficiency grading — makes the EFMB one of the most challenging qualifications in the military.

Course Phases

5 Days of Testing

  • Day 1 APFT & Written Exam — Extended-scale APFT with minimum 80% per event, followed by a comprehensive written exam covering medical doctrine, trauma protocols, evacuation procedures, and common soldier tasks.
  • Days 2-3 Hands-On Medical & Warrior Tasks — Graded lanes testing tactical combat casualty care (TCCC), IV administration, airway management, litter carries, splinting, land navigation, weapons qualification, communications, and NBC procedures. All tasks GO/NO-GO.
  • Day 4 Trauma Lanes & Evacuations — Scenario-based trauma lanes requiring triage, treatment, and evacuation under simulated combat conditions. Candidates must manage multiple casualties with competing priorities and limited resources.
  • Day 5 12-Mile Ruck March — The final event: a 12-mile foot march with a 35-pound minimum load, completed within 3 hours. Successful completion earns the Expert Field Medical Badge.
Key Standards

What It Takes

Candidates must be 68-series (Medical) MOS. The APFT requires an extended-scale score of 80% per event. The breadth of tested tasks is enormous — candidates must be proficient in both medical procedures and common warrior tasks. A single NO-GO on any lane typically results in elimination.

The ~15% pass rate speaks for itself. The EFMB demands months of dedicated preparation across two entirely different skill domains: medicine and soldiering. Successful candidates are true dual-threat experts who can save lives while fighting alongside their infantry counterparts.
