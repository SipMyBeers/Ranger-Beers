Recycle & Recovery Guide

What happens if you fail a phase, how recycling works, staying motivated through setbacks, and the path back to graduation. No shame in the recycle game.

Module 1
How Recycling Works

Roughly 50% of students who enter Ranger School do not graduate with their starting class. That does not mean they all fail out. Many recycle — meaning they get another shot at the phase they did not pass. Understanding how the recycle system works removes the fear of the unknown and lets you focus on what matters: earning the tab.

What Triggers a Recycle

There are several reasons a student gets recycled instead of dropped from the course entirely:

  • Patrol Grade Failure: You did not receive enough "GO" grades on your graded leadership positions. The standard is typically a 50% GO rate overall. If you are close — say 1 of 3 — cadre may recycle you to attempt more patrols.
  • Peer Report: Your peers ranked you in the bottom portion of the class. This signals a leadership or teamwork problem the cadre want you to fix before moving on. Peer evaluations carry real weight.
  • Academic Failure: You failed a graded event like land navigation during RAP Week, a patrol order, or a hands-on task (knots, demo, etc.). Some of these are one-shot gates. Others allow a retest before a recycle decision.
  • Medical Recycle: Injury or illness prevents you from continuing with your class. If the injury is recoverable, you get recycled into a later class once medically cleared. Stress fractures, cellulitis, and hypothermia are common medical recycles.
  • Motivational / Safety: In rare cases, cadre may recycle a student who showed dangerous judgment or quit on a patrol but later demonstrated they want to continue.

Recycle vs. Drop

The distinction matters. A recycle means the cadre believe you can pass with more time. You stay at Ranger School and get inserted into the next class at the start of the phase you failed. A drop means you are removed from the course entirely — you go back to your unit. Drops happen for serious failures: quitting, integrity violations, two consecutive recycles of the same phase, or medical conditions that will not resolve in time.

Where You Get Inserted

You always recycle back to the beginning of the phase you failed. If you fail Darby Phase, you do not skip ahead to the patrol you failed — you start Darby over from Day 1 with the next class. This means repeating the classroom instruction, the practice patrols, and the graded patrols. You eat the same meals, walk the same terrain, and lose the same sleep all over again.

How Many Times Can You Recycle?

The standard rule: you get one recycle per phase. If you fail the same phase twice, you are typically dropped from the course. However, you can recycle different phases across your time at Ranger School. Some graduates recycle two or even three times across different phases before earning the tab. The course record-holders have spent 6+ months in Ranger School.

The Numbers

  • RAP Week attrition is typically 40-60% of the starting class
  • Of those who make it past RAP, roughly 20-30% will recycle at least one phase
  • Most recycles happen in Darby Phase (patrol grade failures) and Mountains (environmental + fatigue)
  • Medical recycles can happen at any phase
  • Many Ranger tab holders are recyclers — it is not a mark of shame
Module 2
The Day After You Fail

Getting recycled is one of the hardest moments in Ranger School — not physically, but mentally. Here is what actually happens and how to handle it.

The Notification

At the end of a phase, cadre will post or announce who is moving on and who is not. Sometimes it happens in a formation. Sometimes your RI pulls you aside. Either way, you will know. The cadre will tell you whether you are being recycled or dropped. If recycled, they will tell you which phase you are going back to and roughly when the next class arrives.

The First 24 Hours

This is the hardest part. Your squad — the people you suffered with — are moving on without you. You are watching them pack up and head to the next phase while you walk to the recycle barracks. The emotions are real: anger, embarrassment, frustration, self-doubt. Every recycler experiences this. Here is what to do:

  1. Call home. Talk to your family, your partner, your battle buddy back at your unit. Tell them what happened. Do not isolate. The worst thing you can do is sit in your own head for 48 hours.
  2. Eat. You have been starving for weeks. The recycle barracks has access to real food. Eat well. Your body needs calories to recover.
  3. Sleep. You have been running on 2-4 hours a night. You are not thinking clearly. Sleep for 12+ hours. Everything looks different after rest.
  4. Do not make any decisions for 48 hours. Do not call your unit and say you want to come home. Do not decide you are a failure. Do not quit. Just rest and let your body and mind reset.

Administrative Steps

  • You will be assigned to the Ranger Training Brigade's holding area (sometimes called "the Gulag")
  • You keep your gear — do not turn anything in unless told to
  • You will be given a timeline for when your next class starts
  • You may be assigned details — work duties around Camp Rogers, Camp Merrill, or Eglin depending on where you recycled
  • Medical recyclers report to sick call and follow the recovery plan

Calling Your Unit

Your unit leadership will be notified through official channels, but you should call your first-line leader and let them know what happened. Keep it professional: "I was recycled at [phase] for [reason]. My next insertion date is approximately [date]. I am still committed to earning the tab." Your unit expects this — recycles are common and understood.

What Not to Do

  • Do not quit in the heat of the moment. The desire to leave is strongest in the first 48 hours and fades fast once you rest.
  • Do not blame your squad, your RI, or the weather. Own it. Cadre recycle people for a reason.
  • Do not compare yourself to your classmates who passed. Their path is not yours.
  • Do not hide the recycle from people. Every senior Ranger leader has either recycled or knows someone who did. There is no shame in it.
Module 3
Training During the Wait

The time between your recycle and your next class insertion is a gift. Most students waste it. Here is how to use it.

How Long Is the Wait?

It depends on when the next class starts and how many recycle slots are available. Typical wait times:

  • RAP Week recycle: 2-3 weeks until the next class starts RAP
  • Darby recycle: 1-3 weeks until the next class reaches Darby
  • Mountain recycle: 1-2 weeks (classes rotate through Camp Merrill frequently)
  • Florida recycle: 1-3 weeks until the next class arrives at Eglin
  • Medical recycle: variable — could be weeks or months depending on recovery

Physical Training

You finally have time to train and food to eat. Use it wisely:

  • Eat at maintenance or slight surplus. You have been in a massive caloric deficit. Rebuild muscle and energy stores. Focus on protein and carbs.
  • Do not go beast mode. Your body is broken down from weeks of Ranger School. Do not start crushing PRs in the gym. Focus on recovery training: easy runs, bodyweight exercises, stretching, mobility work.
  • Walk with weight. Keep rucking but at moderate loads (35-45 lbs) and moderate pace. Maintain your feet calluses and load-bearing fitness.
  • Sleep 7-8 hours every night. Sleep is your primary recovery tool. Do not stay up late. Go to bed early, wake up for PT, nap if you can.
  • Fix your feet. Change socks, air out your boots, treat any blisters or hot spots. Your feet are your most important equipment.

Study Your Weak Areas

You know exactly why you were recycled. Attack that weakness:

  • Patrol grades: Review your patrol evaluations. What specific feedback did the RI give you? Practice giving patrol orders out loud. Walk through battle drills in your head. Study the operations order format until you can brief it in your sleep.
  • Land nav: If you failed the land nav course, get a map and protractor and plot points every single day. Walk the Camp Rogers land nav course if they let you. Practice pace count on different terrain.
  • Peer evaluation: This is harder to fix. Think honestly about what your peers saw. Were you pulling your weight on movements? Were you falling asleep on watch? Were you sharing food? Were you leading from the front or hiding in the middle of the formation?
  • Knots / RTTs: Practice every day. Tie the knots while blindfolded. Practice anchor systems. If you failed a hands-on test, you should be able to do it perfectly 10 out of 10 times before you reinsert.

Help the New Class

When the next class arrives, you have something they do not: experience. You know the terrain, the daily schedule, the RI expectations, and the common mistakes. Be a resource, not a know-it-all. Help your new squad without being arrogant about it. This also directly improves your peer evaluations — the new class will see you as a leader.

Details and Duties

You will be assigned work while you wait. Common details include:

  • Ammo point duty
  • Training area setup and teardown
  • Vehicle maintenance
  • Supply room support
  • Helping run events for incoming classes (lane setup, safety, etc.)

Do the details well. Cadre notice effort. Being a reliable worker in the holding area builds rapport with the RIs who will grade you next time. Show up on time, do the job, do not complain.

Mental Preparation

The hardest part of the wait is the mental game. You are watching new classes come and go. You are doing menial work while your peers back at your unit are doing their jobs. Boredom and frustration will creep in. Combat it:

  • Write down why you came to Ranger School. Read it every morning.
  • Visualize yourself passing the phase. Walk through the scenarios mentally.
  • Talk to other recyclers. They understand what you are going through better than anyone.
  • Set small daily goals: study for 1 hour, run 3 miles, practice knots for 30 minutes.
Module 4
Coming Back Stronger

Your second attempt is not the same as your first. You have an enormous advantage: you have been through it before. Here is how to use that advantage and avoid the traps.

The Recycler's Edge

Things you know that your new classmates do not:

  • The daily schedule and battle rhythm of the phase
  • The terrain — you have walked it before
  • What the RIs are looking for on graded patrols
  • How to manage sleep deprivation and caloric deficit
  • The common mistakes that get people recycled or dropped
  • The administrative process, the chow schedule, the land nav course layout

This experience is valuable. But it is also dangerous if you let it make you complacent or arrogant.

The Recycler's Traps

  • Arrogance: "I've done this before, I know how it works." Your new squad does not want to hear how you did things last time. Lead by example, not by lecturing. Share knowledge when asked, but do not position yourself as the expert.
  • Complacency: "I know this terrain, I can relax." No. You failed this phase already. The terrain does not change, but the conditions, weather, and RI expectations might. Treat every patrol like it is your first.
  • Bitterness: "This is bullshit, I should have passed last time." Maybe. But you didn't. The cadre's decision is final. Holding onto resentment will poison your attitude and your peers will see it immediately.
  • Age gap: If you have been at Ranger School for a while, you may be significantly older (in Ranger School time) than your new classmates. They are fresh out of RAP Week. You have been grinding for months. Bridge the gap — do not let it become a barrier.

Tactical Adjustments for Round Two

  1. Volunteer for leadership early. Do not wait to be assigned a graded position. Raise your hand. The best time to get your GO is when you are relatively rested at the start of the phase, not at the end when everyone is destroyed.
  2. Peer evaluations start on Day 1. Your new squad is forming impressions immediately. Carry extra weight. Share food. Stay awake on watch. Help people with their gear. Be the person everyone wants in their team.
  3. Fix the specific thing that got you recycled. If it was your patrol order, have your 5-paragraph format memorized cold. If it was movement to contact, rehearse the battle drill until it is muscle memory. Do not rely on general improvement — target the exact failure point.
  4. Stay humble. When your new squadmates ask "have you been through this before?" the right answer is: "Yeah, I recycled. Happy to help however I can." Not: "Yeah, and I got screwed. Let me tell you how this really works."
  5. Take care of your body. You have had time to recover. Do not throw it away by skipping meals or not hydrating. You know how bad it gets in week 3 — front-load calories and water.

The Reality: Many Rangers Are Recyclers

Here is the truth that nobody tells you before Ranger School: a significant percentage of tab holders recycled at least one phase. Some of the most respected senior NCOs and officers in the Army took two or three attempts to graduate. The tab does not say which attempt you earned it on. Nobody checks. Nobody cares. The only thing that matters is whether you have it or not.

Recycling tests something that going straight through does not: whether you can take a punch, get back up, and finish the fight. That resilience is the entire point of Ranger School. A Ranger who recycled and graduated has proven they will not quit when things go wrong — and things will go wrong in combat.

Famous Recyclers

Without naming specific individuals (they know who they are), know this:

  • Multiple recipients of the Merrill's Marauders Award (top graduate) were recyclers
  • Battalion and Brigade commanders across the Army are recyclers
  • Special operations soldiers who went on to storied careers recycled Ranger School
  • Some of the best RIs teaching at Ranger School were recyclers as students

The Bottom Line

If you get recycled, you have a choice: quit and go home, or stay and earn the tab. The fact that you are reading this course means you are choosing to stay. Good. Reset your mind, rebuild your body, fix your weakness, and go earn it. The tab is waiting. Rangers Lead the Way.
