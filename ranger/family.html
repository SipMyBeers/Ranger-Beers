Family & Spouse Guide

What your family needs to know before, during, and after Ranger School. Communication expectations, support resources, and what to send in care packages.

Before He/She Leaves
Setting Expectations

Ranger School is 62 days of minimal sleep, minimal food, and almost zero communication with the outside world. The most important thing you can do as a spouse or family member is understand what's coming and set realistic expectations.

  • Communication will be almost nonexistent. Expect a 2-5 minute phone call between phases — maybe. Some classes get none. No texting, no email, no social media.
  • No news is good news. If you don't hear anything, that means they're still in the course and moving forward. If something goes wrong (injury, recycle, drop), you'll be contacted through the unit's rear detachment.
  • They will lose 20-35 lbs. This is normal. They're burning twice the calories they're eating. They'll look rough at graduation. It all comes back.
  • They might get recycled. This isn't failure — it's extremely common. About 50-60% of tab holders were recycled at least once. Support them through it.
  • Set up a POC. Designate one person (usually a spouse or parent) as the point of contact for the unit. Make sure rear detachment has current phone numbers.
During the Course
Communication Windows

Here's when you might hear from them:

  • After RAP Week: Brief phone call if they passed. If you don't hear anything within 5 days of class start, they're likely still in.
  • Between Darby and Mountain Phase: Possible 2-5 minute phone call during transit day.
  • Between Mountain and Swamp Phase: Possible 2-5 minute phone call during transit day.
  • Graduation: They'll call you with a date and time once they know they're graduating.
What to do if you're worried

Contact the rear detachment at their unit — NOT the Ranger Training Brigade directly. Your unit FRG (Family Readiness Group) leader can help you get connected. Do not call Camp Rogers, Camp Merrill, or Camp Rudder asking for status updates.

Care Packages
What to Send (and What Not To)

Mail is not guaranteed, but some classes allow care packages between phases. Here's what to send and what to avoid.

Food (High Calorie)
  • Beef jerky / meat sticks
  • Trail mix / mixed nuts
  • Pop-Tarts (classic favorite)
  • Peanut butter packets
  • Protein bars (dense ones)
  • Honey packets
Comfort Items
  • Baby wipes (unscented)
  • Foot powder (Gold Bond)
  • Lip balm
  • Moleskin / blister care
  • Ibuprofen
  • Hand warmers (winter class)
DO NOT Send
  • Anything perishable
  • Electronics or phones
  • Tobacco products
  • Alcohol (obviously)
  • Anything bulky or heavy
  • Cologne / scented items
Pro tip from spouses

Write a short letter with each care package. Keep it positive and supportive. Don't mention problems at home — they can't do anything about it and it'll distract them. Tell them you're proud of them and that everything at home is handled.

After Graduation
The Homecoming

Graduation is at Camp Rudder (Eglin AFB, FL). Family is welcome and encouraged to attend. Here's what to expect:

  • They'll be thin, exhausted, and emotional. This is normal. Don't be alarmed by their appearance — 62 days of sleep and food deprivation takes a toll.
  • They'll want to eat. A lot. Take them to their favorite restaurant. They've been dreaming about real food for two months.
  • Give them space to decompress. They've been under extreme stress. They may be quiet, irritable, or emotional for a few days. This passes.
  • Physical recovery takes 2-4 weeks. Weight comes back quickly. Joints and feet may take longer. Don't push them to work out right away.
  • They earned the tab. Be proud. This is one of the most difficult military schools in the world. Fewer than 50% of those who start will finish.
If They Get Recycled
Supporting Through a Setback

Getting recycled is not failing — it's getting another chance. Most Ranger School graduates were recycled at least once. Here's how to support them:

  • Don't treat it as a failure. They're going through the hardest military school in the world. Recycling is part of the process for many.
  • Be encouraging but realistic. "You'll get it next time" is exactly what they need to hear. Mean it.
  • The wait can be weeks. Depending on phase and availability, they may wait 2-6 weeks in "the Gulag" (recycle barracks) before re-inserting. This is a mentally tough period.
  • They may be able to call more often. Recycles sometimes get phone privileges. Use this time to reconnect.
  • The decision to go back or quit is theirs. Support whatever they decide. Some people recycle 2-3 times and earn the tab. Others decide to move on. Both are valid.
