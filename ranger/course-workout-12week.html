12-Week Ranger Prep Program

A structured 12-week training program to get you physically ready for Ranger School. Running is the foundation — everything else builds on top of it.

Module 1
Program Overview

This is a 12-week progressive training program designed with one goal: arrive at Ranger School with a strong aerobic base, not just peak strength. Ranger School is 61+ days of sleep deprivation, calorie deficit, and continuous operations. You need to sustain performance across the entire course — not just RAP week.

Weekly Structure: 5–6 training days, 1 full rest day. The program is built around four pillars, with running as the clear primary focus.

Running
Primary pillar. Aerobic base, intervals, plate carrier runs, and barefoot training.
Rucking
Weekly ruck marches building to 12 miles at 35 lbs under 3:00.
Upper Body Endurance
Push-ups and sit-ups, high volume, timed sets for RPFT prep.
Swimming
CWST prep — 15m swim in ACUs, 3m blindfolded drop.

The focus is RUNNING. It is the foundation of everything else. Your aerobic capacity determines how well you ruck, how fast you recover between patrols, and whether you can keep up during 5-mile formation runs on zero sleep. If you only have time for one thing, run.

RPFT Standards to Hit:
  • 49 push-ups (2 minutes)
  • 59 sit-ups (2 minutes)
  • Sub-40:00 5-mile run
  • 12-mile ruck in under 3:00:00 (35 lbs dry)

These are minimums. You should be exceeding all of them comfortably before you report. If you are borderline on any standard, you are already behind.

Phase 1 Key Takeaway

If you are hitting these workouts consistently with no injury, you are on track. Do not skip the easy days or turn them into hard days. The aerobic base is built on volume at low intensity, not by redlining every session.

Module 3
Weeks 5–8: Endurance Phase

Phase 2 increases both volume and intensity. Your aerobic base from the first 4 weeks supports longer runs, heavier rucks, and faster intervals. This is where you start simulating the demands of Ranger School.

Key Changes:

  • Long run builds to 8 miles
  • Intervals shift to 800m repeats x 6
  • Plate carrier increases to 20–25 lbs, 3-mile runs, 2x/week (Tue/Thu)
  • Barefoot running progresses to track surface, 1.5–2 miles
  • 5-mile run time trial at Week 6
  • Rucking increases to 6–8 miles, 30–35 lbs
  • Push-ups/sit-ups shift to timed sets (2 min max reps) mimicking RPFT format
  • Swimming: CWST simulation (15m swim in ACUs)

Phase 2 Key Takeaway

After your Week 6 time trial, you should know exactly where you stand on the 5-mile run. If you are over 42 minutes, you need to add an extra running day. If you are under 38, you are in a strong position. Adjust the remaining weeks based on your result.

Module 4
Weeks 9–12: Peak Phase

This is where everything comes together. You are training at your highest volume and intensity through Week 10, then tapering in Week 12 so you arrive at Ranger School fresh, not broken. Week 11 includes a full RPFT practice test.

Key Changes:

  • 5-mile runs 2x/week at race pace
  • Long run peaks at 10 miles in Week 10
  • Plate carrier increases to 30–35 lbs, 4–5 mile runs
  • Barefoot running progresses to smooth trail, 2–3 miles
  • Combined events: ruck then run, swim then ruck
  • Rucking peaks at 12 miles, 35 lbs dry, target sub-3:00
  • Week 11: RPFT practice test
  • Week 12: taper all volume by 40%, maintain intensity

Taper Week Warning

Do not panic-train during taper week. The fitness you have built over 11 weeks will not disappear in 7 days. But an injury from overdoing it in the final week will follow you into Day 1. Reduce volume by 40%, keep intensity the same, sleep as much as possible, and hydrate aggressively.

Module 5
Running Focus: Plate Carrier & Barefoot

Two specialized running programs run alongside your standard training throughout the 12 weeks. Both serve distinct purposes and require careful progression to avoid injury.

Plate Carrier Running

Why: Builds strength endurance for carrying kit during patrols. At Ranger School, you will move under load constantly — on patrols, road marches, and movements to contact. Plate carrier running trains your body to maintain running form and aerobic output while bearing weight.

Equipment: A weighted vest or plate carrier with removable plates. Start light and add plates as you progress. If you only have a ruck, it works — but a vest distributes weight better for running.

12-Week Weight Progression:

Wk 1–4: 15 lbs → Wk 5–6: 20 lbs → Wk 7–8: 25 lbs → Wk 9–10: 30 lbs → Wk 11–12: 35 lbs

Distance Progression: Start at 2 miles, build to 5 miles by Week 10.

Form Tips:

  • Slightly shorter stride than normal running
  • Higher cadence to compensate for reduced stride length
  • Upright posture — do not lean forward excessively
  • Keep core engaged to prevent lower back strain
  • Run on soft surfaces (grass, dirt trail) when possible to protect joints

Recovery: Allow 48 hours between plate carrier sessions. This is load-bearing impact training and your joints need time to adapt. Do not stack plate carrier runs on consecutive days.

Barefoot Running

Why: Strengthens feet, improves proprioception (your body's awareness of foot placement), and reduces overstriding. At Ranger School, your feet take a beating. Stronger feet with better mechanics means fewer blisters, fewer stress fractures, and better performance under fatigue.

Technique Fundamentals:

  • Forefoot/midfoot strike — not heel. Land on the ball of your foot.
  • Short stride — feet land directly under your hips, not in front
  • High cadence — target 180+ steps per minute
  • Land under hips — if your foot lands ahead of your center of mass, you are overstriding

Surface Progression:

Wk 1–4: Grass → Wk 5–8: Track → Wk 9–12: Smooth Trail

Distance Progression: Start at 0.5–1 mile, build to 3 miles by Week 11. Do not rush this. Barefoot running loads your calves, Achilles, and foot muscles differently than shod running.

Foot Strengthening Drills (do after every barefoot session):

  • Towel scrunches: Place a towel flat on the floor. Use your toes to scrunch it toward you. 3 sets.
  • Marble pickups: Pick up marbles or small objects with your toes. 20 reps per foot.
  • Calf raises: Single-leg calf raises on a step. 3 x 15 per leg.
  • Single-leg balance: Stand on one foot, eyes closed. Hold 30 seconds per leg. 3 sets.
Injury Rule

Stop immediately if you feel sharp pain during barefoot running. Dull soreness in the calves or arches after the first few sessions is normal (adaptation). Sharp, localized pain is not. If it hurts, stop, ice it, and return to grass at a shorter distance.

Module 6
Nutrition & Recovery

Training this hard without fueling and recovering properly is a recipe for injury, overtraining, and showing up to Ranger School already broken. This module covers how to eat, sleep, and recover during the 12-week program.

Calorie Needs

During heavy training weeks (Weeks 5–11), you need 3,500–4,500 calories per day depending on your body weight and training volume. You are running, rucking, swimming, and doing calisthenics — you cannot maintain this on a standard 2,000-calorie diet.

Macronutrient Breakdown

MacroPriorityTargetWhy
CarbohydratesPrimary fuel50–60% of caloriesFuels running volume. Glycogen is your primary energy source for aerobic work.
ProteinRecovery0.8–1g per lb bodyweightMuscle repair, tendon/ligament health. Do not skimp on this.
FatHormone support20–30% of caloriesSupports testosterone, joint health, and sustained energy.

Hydration

Minimum: half your bodyweight in ounces per day. A 180-lb athlete needs at least 90 oz of water daily. On long run or ruck days, add 20–30 oz with electrolytes. Urine should be pale yellow — if it is dark, you are behind.

Sleep

7–8 hours per night. You will not get this at Ranger School — you will average 1–3 hours for weeks at a time. That makes pre-course sleep even more important. Your body adapts, repairs muscle, and consolidates motor learning during sleep. Cutting sleep during training undermines everything else you are doing.

Think of it as banking sleep. You cannot fully "store" it, but arriving at Ranger School well-rested vs. sleep-deprived makes a measurable difference in your first week of performance.

Recovery Protocols

  • Foam rolling: 10–15 minutes after every training session. Focus on quads, IT band, calves, and glutes.
  • Stretching: Static stretching after workouts (not before). Hip flexors, hamstrings, calves, and shoulders.
  • Ice baths: After long runs (7+ miles) and peak ruck sessions. 10–15 minutes in cold water (50–60°F). Reduces inflammation and speeds recovery.
  • Active recovery: On rest days, walk 20–30 minutes. Do not sit all day — light movement promotes blood flow and recovery.

Injury Management

The number one rule: do not push through sharp pain. Dull muscle soreness is training adaptation. Sharp, localized pain is a warning sign. Common injuries during this program:

  • Shin splints: Reduce running volume, run on softer surfaces, check shoe wear
  • IT band syndrome: Foam roll aggressively, reduce hill running, stretch hip flexors
  • Plantar fasciitis: Roll a frozen water bottle under your foot, reduce barefoot volume
  • Stress fractures: See medical immediately. Do not run on a suspected stress fracture. Period.

If an injury sidelines you for more than a week, adjust the program — skip back a phase and rebuild. It is better to arrive at Ranger School slightly undertrained than injured.

Pre-Ranger School Nutrition Prep

Starting in Week 9, begin preparing your body for the calorie deficit you will face at Ranger School:

  • Practice eating MREs: Eat 1–2 MREs per week to acclimate your gut. Know which MRE components have the most calories (pound cake, cheese spread, peanut butter).
  • Calorie deficit simulation: One day per week in Weeks 9–11, eat only 1,500 calories while still training. This teaches your body to perform underfueled — a daily reality at Ranger School.
  • Pre-load before report date: In the final week, eat at a slight surplus (500+ calories above maintenance). You will burn through these reserves quickly once the course starts.
Final Thought

The 12 weeks before Ranger School are the only time you have full control over your training, nutrition, sleep, and recovery. Once you report, those variables are taken from you. Use this time wisely. Be disciplined, be consistent, and trust the process. The work you put in now will carry you when everything else is stripped away.
