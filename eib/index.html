Day one eliminates candidates who aren't physically or academically prepared. The ACFT must be passed to Army standard, and the written exam covers infantry doctrine, weapons systems, and tactical procedures. The written exam is often multiple choice but the bar is firm — no retests.

Day 1 Events
  • ACFT — Army Combat Fitness Test to Army standard
  • Written examination — infantry doctrine and weapons systems
  • Tactical procedures knowledge assessment
  • Medical screening and readiness verification
  • No retests — fail either event and you're done

This is where EIB earns its reputation. Each candidate must perform approximately 30 individual warrior tasks to a zero-deficiency standard. Tasks include weapons assembly/disassembly, call for fire, radio procedures, first aid, land navigation, and demolitions.

One missed step on any single task means NO GO for the entire lane — there are no partial credits and no retests. This zero-deficiency standard is why EIB has an ~80% failure rate.

Warrior Task Categories
  • Weapons — M4, M249, M240, M18 Claymore assembly/disassembly
  • Call for fire — proper fire mission request format
  • 9-line MEDEVAC — casualty evacuation request procedures
  • Radio procedures — proper communications protocol
  • First aid — tourniquet, NPA, chest seal application
  • Land navigation — day and night plotting and movement
  • Hand grenades — employment and safety procedures
  • Demolitions — demolition charge fundamentals

Day 4
DurationDay 4

The patrol lane tests the candidate's ability to operate as part of a small unit under simulated combat conditions. Candidates must react to contact, execute battle drills, and demonstrate tactical movement.

Evaluators grade on proper technique, communication, and leadership under stress.

Patrol Lane Events
  • React to contact — immediate action under simulated fire
  • React to ambush — near and far ambush battle drills
  • Break contact — disengage and withdraw under fire
  • Tactical movement — proper formations and techniques
  • Battle drill execution — standard battle drills to standard
  • Communication — proper reporting and hand signals
  • Leadership under stress — decision-making under pressure
12-Mile Ruck March — Final Gate
Day 5
DurationDay 5

The final event — a 12-mile forced march under a 35-pound rucksack in 3 hours or less. After 4 days of intense testing, candidates are physically and mentally drained.

The ruck march is the last gate, and plenty of candidates who survived the warrior task lanes fail here due to blisters, heat, cold, or simply running out of time.

Ruck March Details
  • 12-mile ruck march — the final qualifying event
  • 3:00:00 time limit — 15-minute mile pace required
  • 35lb minimum dry weight — weighed before and after
  • After 4 days of testing — cumulative fatigue is the enemy
  • Blisters, heat, cold — environmental factors eliminate many
  • Progressive ruck training before testing is essential
Seasons
Winter — Cold Weather Challenges
Dec – Feb
Testing FrequencyLow — most units avoid winter
Land Nav ImpactShort daylight complicates day/night nav
Hands/DexterityCold affects warrior task performance
Ruck MarchCold but manageable — less heat risk
Injury RiskCold muscles increase strain injuries
Biggest ThreatCold-weather injuries, numbness during tasks

Winter EIB testing is rare but happens. Cold weather affects fine motor skills needed for warrior tasks. Land navigation is complicated by shorter daylight and potentially frozen/muddy terrain. The upside: no heat casualties on the ruck.

Winter Prep Tips
  • Practice warrior tasks with cold hands — dexterity matters
  • Layer system for early morning lanes
  • Bring hand warmers for waiting between stations
  • Waterproof boots — frozen mud is miserable
  • Extra warm-up time before the ruck march

Spring is the most popular EIB testing season. Temperatures are comfortable for all events, and units schedule testing to align with training calendars. Watch for rain during warrior task lanes — wet equipment adds complexity.

Spring Prep Tips
  • Rain plan — practice tasks in wet conditions
  • Allergy meds if testing at southern installations
  • Light layers for early morning start times
  • Broken-in boots for the ruck march
  • Bug spray for land nav — ticks emerge in spring
Summer — Heat Is the Hidden Test
Jun – Aug
Testing FrequencyModerate — heat limits some units
Heat CasualtiesHighest risk — especially ruck march
Land NavHeavy vegetation obscures terrain
Ruck MarchHeat is the #1 drop reason
Black Flag RiskTraining halts above heat cat 5
Biggest ThreatHeat exhaustion / heat stroke

Summer EIB is a heat battle. The 12-mile ruck march in peak heat produces the most non-task-related failures. Black flag conditions can halt training entirely, delaying the schedule by days.

Summer Prep Tips
  • Heat acclimatize 2 weeks before testing
  • Hydration — start loading water days before
  • Electrolytes for every water source
  • Anti-chafe for humid conditions
  • Practice ruck marching in heat to build tolerance
Fall — Ideal Testing Conditions
Sep – Nov
Testing FrequencyHigh — second most popular
WeatherCool and dry at most installations
Land NavLeaves falling improves visibility

Fall is excellent for EIB testing. Cooler temperatures make the ruck march manageable, vegetation is thinning for better land nav visibility, and units often schedule make-up testing in the fall.

Fall Prep Tips
  • Light layers for early morning lanes
  • Headlamp for early-morning or late-evening events
  • Leaf litter on trails — watch footing on ruck
  • Standard packing list is generally sufficient
  • Daylight planning for land nav course

Unit-Level Testing

EIB testing is conducted at the unit level across the Army. There is no centralized Army-wide schedule. Contact your unit S-3 or training NCO for upcoming EIB test dates at your installation.
