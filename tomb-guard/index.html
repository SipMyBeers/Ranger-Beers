New Soldier Training builds the foundation of a Tomb Guard. Volunteers must memorize an enormous body of knowledge including the complete history of the Tomb, Arlington National Cemetery, and every Medal of Honor recipient buried there. They must be able to recite any of this information on demand.

Uniform preparation is an art form — hours of daily work pressing, shining, and perfecting every element. Precision drill begins immediately — the sentinel's walk, rifle manual, and changing of the guard ceremony must become second nature.

Physical training standards are among the highest in the Army. Soldiers must maintain an immaculate appearance at all times, meeting height and weight standards that go beyond Army minimums.

Training Focus Areas
  • Tomb history and Arlington National Cemetery knowledge
  • Medal of Honor recipient biographies and burial locations
  • Uniform preparation — pressing, shining, inspections
  • Precision drill and ceremonial movements
  • Rifle manual of arms for ceremonial weapon
  • Physical fitness and appearance standards
  • Military bearing and discipline under public observation

Relief Training is where Tomb Guard candidates begin walking the mat under supervision. The sentinel's walk — 21 steps across 90 feet, a 21-second pause, a precise turn, and repeat — must be executed with mechanical perfection every single time.

The Changing of the Guard ceremony is practiced hundreds of times. Every movement, every rifle inspection, every step must be identical. Fractions of an inch matter. Senior Sentinels observe and correct relentlessly.

Candidates who cannot maintain the precision, the uniform standards, or the physical appearance required are removed from training. There is no room for "close enough" at the Tomb.

Relief Training Events
  • Sentinel's walk — 21 steps, 21-second pause, turn
  • Changing of the Guard ceremony rehearsals
  • Rifle inspection sequence and manual of arms
  • Walking the mat in all weather conditions
  • Wreath-laying ceremony procedures
  • Challenging unauthorized visitors after hours
  • Maintaining bearing under intense public scrutiny

Sentinel Qualification is the culmination of nine months of relentless preparation. Candidates must pass a comprehensive written exam scoring 95% or higher on Tomb history, Arlington National Cemetery, Medal of Honor recipients, and ceremonial procedures.

The uniform inspection is absolute — a single thread, a dull spot on brass, an imperfect crease results in failure. The practical evaluation requires walking the mat under formal grading conditions, demonstrating every movement with zero deficiency.

The final board review by senior NCOs determines whether a Soldier has demonstrated the knowledge, bearing, precision, and dedication required to wear the Tomb Guard Identification Badge — the second rarest badge in the U.S. Army.

Qualification Requirements
  • Written knowledge exam — 95%+ score required
  • Flawless uniform inspection — zero deficiencies
  • Practical mat walk under formal evaluation
  • Changing of the Guard ceremony execution
  • Senior NCO board review and oral examination
  • Demonstrated commitment to lifelong standards

Winter at the Tomb means walking the mat in freezing temperatures, snow, and ice. Sentinels march their 21 steps regardless of weather. The marble plaza becomes treacherous when iced over, but the walk must remain precise. There are no weather cancellations.

Winter Conditions
  • Guard walks continue through snow, ice, and sub-freezing temps
  • Overcoats are authorized but standards remain identical
  • Rifle handling in numb conditions requires extra discipline
  • Holiday crowds — Wreath Across America in December
  • Overnight shifts in the coldest hours of the year

Spring brings the highest-profile ceremonies of the year. Memorial Day at Arlington National Cemetery is the most significant event — the President typically lays a wreath at the Tomb. Sentinels must be at absolute peak performance during this period.

Spring Conditions
  • Memorial Day — highest-profile ceremony of the year
  • Presidential wreath-laying events
  • Increasing visitor traffic and public scrutiny
  • Rain walks — mat becomes slick, uniform gets soaked
  • Transition from overcoat to standard uniform

DC summers are brutal in a full ceremonial uniform. Temperatures above 90°F with high humidity create heat casualty risk. Guard rotations shorten to 30 minutes during summer months. Sentinels must maintain perfect bearing while fighting heat exhaustion.

Summer Conditions
  • 30-minute guard rotations due to heat (vs. 1 hour in cooler months)
  • Full wool uniform in 90°F+ heat and humidity
  • Peak tourist season — maximum public observation
  • Independence Day ceremonies and events
  • Hydration management critical between shifts

Fall Conditions

  • Transition to overcoat as temperatures drop

  • Preparation intensifies for holiday season events

Don't Be That Tomb Guard Candidate.

Every failure at the Tomb has a type. We built a fix for each one.

📚
Can't Memorize?

You must memorize the complete history of the Tomb, Arlington National Cemetery, and every Medal of Honor recipient buried there. On-demand recall. No notes. No hesitation.
👔
Imperfect Uniform?

Hours of daily preparation. Every crease razor-sharp. Every button polished to a mirror finish. One loose thread or dull spot on brass means failure.
🧖
Bad March Cadence?

21 steps. Exactly 90 feet. 21-second pause. Turn. Repeat. Every step identical. Every movement precise to fractions of an inch. Mechanical perfection required.
💪
Not Physically Perfect?

Physical fitness standards exceed normal Army requirements. Appearance must be immaculate. Height, weight, and bearing are evaluated constantly.
🤐
Can't Handle Silence?

Hours of walking in silence. No conversation. No distraction. Complete focus on the mission of guarding the Tomb. Mental toughness is non-negotiable.
🕐
Commitment Issues?

Tomb Guards voluntarily accept restrictions on personal conduct for life. This isn't a tour — it's a lifelong commitment to the honor of the Unknown Soldiers.

Key Takeaway: The Tomb Guard Identification Badge is earned through months of unrelenting discipline, memorization, and physical perfection. There are no shortcuts. The standard is absolute — every uniform flawless, every step precise, every fact memorized. Only ~20% of volunteers earn the badge, and fewer than 700 have been awarded since 1958.

Sentinel's Creed

My dedication to this sacred duty is total and whole-hearted. In the responsibility bestowed on me, never will I falter. And with dignity and perseverance, my standard will remain perfection.

Through the years of diligence and praise and the discomfort of the elements, I will walk my tour in humble reverence to the best of my ability.

It is he who commands the respect I protect, his bravery that made us so proud. Surrounded by well-meaning combatants who struggle to comprehend the depth of his valor, so combatants I shall remain — and I will carry their memory with me until the day I too join their ranks.
