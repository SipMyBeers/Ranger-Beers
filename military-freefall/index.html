Ground Training is where students learn the fundamentals of military free-fall parachuting before ever leaving the aircraft. Every procedure, malfunction response, and body position must be committed to muscle memory on the ground.

The altitude chamber familiarizes students with hypoxia symptoms — at 25,000 feet, supplemental oxygen is mandatory and recognizing oxygen system failures is life or death. Students also learn the MC-6 parachute system inside and out, including rigging, inspection, and emergency procedures.

Body position training uses suspended harness rigs and ground-level practice. A stable freefall arch is the foundation of everything that follows — students who cannot maintain stability will not progress.

Ground Training Topics
  • MC-6 parachute system — rigging, inspection, and packing
  • Freefall body position — stable arch, turns, tracking
  • Aircraft exit procedures for various airframes
  • Emergency procedures — cutaway, reserve, mid-air collision
  • Altitude chamber — hypoxia recognition and oxygen equipment
  • Combat equipment rigging — rucksack, weapon, and O2
  • Landing pattern analysis and wind assessment

The vertical wind tunnel is where ground training meets reality. Students experience 120+ mph wind speeds that simulate freefall conditions, refining body position and stability before their first actual jump.

Basic freefall jumps begin from 10,000–13,000 feet. Students must demonstrate stable exits, controlled turns, altitude awareness, and proper deployment. Under canopy, they practice steering, pattern entry, and precision landings.

Students who cannot maintain stable body position in the tunnel or during initial jumps are dropped. This phase separates those who can handle the sensory overload of freefall from those who cannot.

Tunnel / Basic Events
  • Vertical wind tunnel sessions — body stabilization and control
  • Initial freefall jumps from 10,000–13,000 ft
  • Stable exit techniques from various aircraft
  • In-air turns, tracking maneuvers, and heading control
  • Altitude awareness — altimeter checks and pull altitude discipline
  • Canopy control — steering, pattern work, and flare timing
  • Progressive complexity with each successive jump

Advanced Freefall is where students become military free-fall parachutists. Jump altitudes increase to 25,000 feet, requiring supplemental oxygen and cold-weather protection at temperatures reaching -30°F in the aircraft doorway.

HALO operations — exiting at high altitude and freefalling to low opening altitudes — demand precise altitude awareness and body control while falling at 120+ mph. HAHO operations add long-range canopy navigation, gliding 20+ miles to the target.

Night jumps under NVGs, combat equipment jumps with rucksack and weapon, and group freefall formations round out the training. Successful graduates earn the Military Free-Fall Parachutist Badge.

Advanced Freefall Requirements
  • HALO jumps from up to 25,000 feet with oxygen
  • HAHO jumps with long-range canopy navigation
  • Night operations — NVG freefall and precision landings
  • Combat equipment jumps — rucksack, weapon, O2
  • Group freefall formations and breakoff procedures
  • Precision landing accuracy under all conditions

Winter is the best time for MFF School. Yuma's desert winter brings mild temperatures, clear skies, and calm winds — ideal conditions for freefall training. Most classes are scheduled during these months to maximize jump days.

Winter Gear Tips
  • Lightweight base layers for cool mornings on the DZ
  • Sunscreen — desert sun still intense in winter
  • Hydration — dry desert air dehydrates quickly
  • Gloves for altitude jumps — -30°F at 25,000 ft
  • Running shoes for PT — pleasant running weather

Spring classes face increasing heat and wind. March is still comfortable, but by May temperatures push into the 90s. Dust devils and thermal turbulence can delay jump operations, though most days remain flyable.

Spring Gear Tips
  • High-SPF sunscreen — applied multiple times daily
  • Electrolyte supplements for increasing heat
  • Lightweight, moisture-wicking clothing
  • Quality sunglasses — bright desert glare on the DZ
  • CamelBak or hydration system for long DZ days

Summer at Yuma is brutal. Ground temperatures exceed 130°F on the drop zone. Heat casualties are a real threat during rigging, manifesting, and waiting for aircraft. Jump operations shift to early morning to avoid the worst heat and thermals.

Summer Survival Tips
  • Heat acclimatization — begin 2 weeks before reporting
  • 1+ gallon of water per day minimum on the DZ
  • Electrolyte tablets — not optional in 115°F heat
  • Shade shelter and cooling towels between jumps
  • Early morning PT — avoid midday exertion

Fall is the second-best window for MFF School. September can still be hot, but by October and November conditions approach winter ideal. Calm winds, clear skies, and moderate temperatures return for consistent jump operations.

Fall Gear Tips
  • Layering options — warm mornings turn cool by November
  • Continue hydration discipline through September
  • Sun protection still essential in early fall
  • Light jacket for early morning DZ operations
  • Optimal time to focus on physical training

Don't Be That MFF Student.

Every failure at MFF School has a type. We built a fix for each one.

🤸
Bad Body Position?

Stability is everything in freefall. An unstable arch means uncontrolled spins, tumbles, and failed jumps. Master the arch before you show up.
🎯
Can't Land on Target?

Precision landing is a graded event. If you can't read winds, fly a pattern, and flare on the mark, you won't graduate. Practice canopy control now.
😵
Fear of Freefall?

The aircraft door at 25,000 feet is no place to discover you can't jump. Mental preparation and civilian skydiving experience reduce voluntary withdrawals.
🦳
Altitude Sick?

Hypoxia at 25,000 feet impairs judgment and motor skills. Learn to recognize symptoms, trust your oxygen equipment, and react before it's too late.
🚶
Wrong Exit?

A bad exit creates instability from the start. Tumbling out of the aircraft means fighting for stability instead of flying your body. Practice exits relentlessly.
🧷
No Canopy Skills?

Free-fall is half the jump. Under canopy you must navigate to the target, manage traffic, and land precisely. Poor canopy skills fail students every class.
