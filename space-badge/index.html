Phase 1 establishes the foundational knowledge of the space domain. Students learn orbital mechanics — Kepler's Laws, orbital elements, and how satellites maintain their orbits. Understanding these principles is essential for everything that follows.

Space domain awareness covers the ability to detect, track, and identify objects in space. Students learn the space surveillance network, conjunction assessments, and the importance of maintaining a clear picture of the space environment.

Satellite systems architecture, GPS/PNT operations, and SATCOM fundamentals round out the week. The space threat environment — including adversary anti-satellite capabilities, electronic warfare, and cyber threats to space systems — is covered extensively.

Classroom Topics
  • Orbital mechanics — Kepler's Laws, orbital elements, maneuvers
  • Space domain awareness — surveillance, tracking, catalog maintenance
  • Satellite systems — architecture, payloads, ground segments
  • GPS / PNT — signal structure, accuracy, denial environments
  • SATCOM — MILSATCOM, commercial SATCOM, link budgets
  • Space threats — ASAT, jamming, spoofing, cyber attacks
  • Space policy — treaties, norms, rules of engagement

Phase 2 shifts from theory to tactical application. Students learn how to integrate space capabilities into ground operations at every echelon — from battalion to theater level. The focus is on making space relevant to the warfighter.

Space support team (STT) operations teach students how these small teams deploy with tactical units to provide direct space support. Theater space coordination and the role of space in the military decision-making process (MDMP) are covered in depth.

The week culminates in a capstone exercise that evaluates students' ability to apply all space concepts in a realistic operational scenario. This is where theory meets practice and the badge is earned.

Applied Operations Events
  • Tactical space support — space capabilities at brigade and below
  • Space enablers integration — ISR, SIGINT, GEOINT from space
  • STT operations — deployment, employment, and reach-back
  • Theater space coordination — ARSPACE, JFCC-Space interface
  • Space effects in MDMP — Annex N, running estimates, IPB
  • Capstone exercise — full-spectrum scenario evaluation
  • Space operations brief — present space support plan to cadre

Winter in Colorado Springs is cold but manageable. Temperatures range from 15–45°F with dry conditions and frequent clear skies. Snow is possible but rarely extreme. The SCBC is a classroom-based course, so weather is secondary — but the clear skies make for excellent space observation conditions.

Winter Packing Tips
  • Warm layers for commuting — mornings can be bitter
  • Hydration — altitude and dry air dehydrate fast
  • Lip balm and moisturizer — the air is extremely dry
  • Sunglasses — Colorado sun is intense even in winter
  • Personal vehicle or rental recommended for off-duty

Spring weather in the Springs is variable. March can still bring significant snowstorms, but by May conditions are warming nicely. The dry air persists year-round. Variable weather means packing layers is always smart — but you'll spend most of your time in the classroom.

Spring Packing Tips
  • Layerable clothing — temps swing 30+ degrees daily
  • Wind-resistant jacket for gusty Front Range days
  • Continue aggressive hydration — altitude effect
  • Sunscreen — UV is intense at 6,000+ feet
  • Great time to explore Garden of the Gods off-duty

Summer is the most comfortable season for SCBC. Warm but not oppressive days with cool mountain nights make for pleasant TDY conditions. Afternoon thunderstorms are common but brief. Evenings are perfect for exploring Pikes Peak, Garden of the Gods, and the surrounding area.

Summer Packing Tips
  • Light layers — mornings can still be cool at altitude
  • Rain jacket — afternoon thunderstorms hit fast
  • Sunscreen and hat — Colorado sun burns quickly
  • Hydration is critical — altitude amplifies dehydration
  • Hiking gear for weekend trips to Pikes Peak

Fall in Colorado Springs is spectacular. Crisp, clear air with stunning views of Pikes Peak and the Front Range. Temperatures cool steadily through the season, with first snow typically arriving in October. The clear skies continue to make this an ideal location for space observation and study.

Fall Packing Tips
  • Warm layers — temps drop quickly after sunset
  • Light jacket for crisp mornings
  • Camera — Pikes Peak fall colors are stunning
  • Hydration — dry air persists year-round
  • November classes may need heavier cold-weather gear

Don't Be That Space Student.

Every failure at SCBC has a type. We built a fix for each one.

🛰
Don't Know Orbital Mechanics?

Kepler's Laws, orbital elements, and satellite maneuvers are the foundation of everything in space operations. If you can't visualize an orbit, you'll be lost from day one.
📡
Weak on SATCOM?

Satellite communications underpin almost every tactical operation. Understanding link budgets, frequency bands, and MILSATCOM architecture is non-negotiable.
🌎
Can't Integrate Space Effects?

Space is useless if you can't translate capabilities into tactical effects. The capstone exercise will expose every gap in your integration skills.
📍
No PNT Knowledge?

GPS is more than a blue dot on a screen. Understanding signal structure, denial environments, and alternative PNT is critical for any space professional.
🔍
Poor Threat Analysis?

Adversary ASAT capabilities, jamming, spoofing, and cyber threats to space systems are growing. You need to understand the threat to protect the domain.
📝
Bad at MDMP Space Annex?

Writing Annex N and integrating space into the military decision-making process is where space professionals earn their keep. Don't show up unprepared.

Key Takeaway: The SCBC is an academic course — the challenge is intellectual, not physical. Orbital mechanics and SATCOM fundamentals trip up the most students. Arrive with a solid STEM foundation, review basic physics, and study JP 3-14 (Space Operations) before class. The capstone exercise requires integrating everything you've learned into a realistic scenario.
